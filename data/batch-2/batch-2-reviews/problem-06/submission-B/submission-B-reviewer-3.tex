\subsection*{Overview / Strategy}

The proof has two main parts. First, a vertex of weight \(1\) is automatically irreducible, because a decomposition \(u=p+q\) with \(p\cdot q\ge 0\) would force \(1=u^2\ge p^2+q^2\ge 2\). Thus, after this easy case, we may assume that no vertex has weight \(1\).

In that remaining case, let \(b\) be the unique vertex with \(w(b)<d_T(b)\). We prove that \(b\) itself is irreducible. The key input is a rooted-branch estimate for each component of \(T-\{b\}\). That estimate is proved by induction using a Schur-complement calculation and a parity argument for the inverse Gram matrix. Combining the branch estimates with the Schur complement at \(b\), any hypothetical decomposition \(b=x+(b-x)\) with \(x\cdot(b-x)\ge0\) is forced to have \(x=0\) or \(x=b\), contradicting the requirement that both summands be nonzero.

\subsection*{Statement and notation}

Let \(T\) be a finite tree with vertex set \(V\), edge set \(E\), and weight function \(w:V\to\Z\). We write \(d_T(v)\) for the degree of a vertex \(v\) in \(T\). Let \(L(T)\) be the free abelian group generated by \(V\). We identify a vertex with the corresponding basis element of \(L(T)\). The bilinear form on \(L(T)\) is the symmetric form determined on generators by
\[
u\cdot v=
\begin{cases}
w(u), & u=v,\\
-1, & \{u,v\}\in E,\\
0, & u\ne v \text{ and } \{u,v\}\notin E.
\end{cases}
\]
For \(x\in L(T)\), write \(x^2=x\cdot x\). The form is called positive definite if its extension to \(L(T)\otimes_{\Z}\mathbb R\) is positive definite. Since the Gram matrix has integer entries, if the form is positive definite, then every nonzero \(z\in L(T)\) satisfies \(z^2\in\Z_{>0}\).

A nonzero lattice element \(y\in L(T)\) is called \emph{irreducible} if there do not exist nonzero \(a,c\in L(T)\) such that
\[
y=a+c
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
a\cdot c\ge 0.
\]

\begin{theorem}[Original problem]\label{thm:main}
Let \(T\) be a finite weighted tree with vertex set \(V\), edge set \(E\), and weight function \(w:V\to\Z\). Equip \(L(T)\) with the symmetric bilinear form above. Suppose this form is positive definite, and suppose there is exactly one vertex \(b\in V\) such that
\[
w(b)<d_T(b).
\]
Then \(T\) contains a vertex \(u\) such that \(u\) is irreducible in \(L(T)\).
\end{theorem}

For a connected subgraph or subtree \(C\), write \(d_C(u)\) for the degree of \(u\) inside \(C\). The group \(L(C)\) is the free abelian group on the vertices of \(C\), equipped with the restricted form. If \(x\in L(C)\), write \(x_u\) for the coefficient of the vertex \(u\) in \(x\).

\subsection*{Auxiliary lemmas}

\begin{lemma}[A weight-one vertex is irreducible]\label{lem:weight-one}
Assume the form on \(L(T)\) is positive definite. If \(u\in V\) satisfies \(w(u)=1\), then \(u\) is irreducible.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\review{Writing $p,q$ for vectors and $p^2,q^2$ for their pairing is an odd notational choice, since conventionally $p,q$ are used to denote integers.}
Suppose, for contradiction, that
\[
u=p+q
\]
where \(p,q\in L(T)\) are nonzero and \(p\cdot q\ge 0\).
Since the form is positive definite and integral, \(p^2,q^2\in\Z_{>0}\). Hence
\[
1=u^2=(p+q)^2=p^2+q^2+2p\cdot q
\ge 1+1+0=2,
\]
which is impossible. Therefore no such decomposition exists, and \(u\) is irreducible.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Schur complement calculation]\label{lem:schur}
Let
\[
M=
\begin{pmatrix}
a & \gamma^T\\
\gamma & B
\end{pmatrix},
\]
where \(B\) is symmetric positive definite. Set
\[
S=a-\gamma^T B^{-1}\gamma.
\]
Then \(M\) is positive definite if and only if \(S>0\). When \(S>0\),
\[
M^{-1}=
\begin{pmatrix}
S^{-1} & -S^{-1}\gamma^T B^{-1}\\
-B^{-1}\gamma S^{-1} &
B^{-1}+B^{-1}\gamma S^{-1}\gamma^T B^{-1}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We have the factorization
\[
M=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & \gamma^T B^{-1}\\
0 & I
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
S & 0\\
0 & B
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0\\
B^{-1}\gamma & I
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Indeed, multiplying the last two factors gives
\[
\begin{pmatrix}
S & 0\\
\gamma & B
\end{pmatrix},
\]
and multiplying by the first factor gives
\[
\begin{pmatrix}
S+\gamma^T B^{-1}\gamma & \gamma^T\\
\gamma & B
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
a & \gamma^T\\
\gamma & B
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
The first and third factors are transposes of one another and are invertible. Congruence by an invertible matrix preserves positive definiteness, so \(M\) is positive definite exactly when the middle block diagonal matrix is positive definite. Since \(B\) is already positive definite, this is equivalent to \(S>0\).

When \(S>0\), invert the displayed factorization:
\[
M^{-1}=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0\\
-B^{-1}\gamma & I
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
S^{-1} & 0\\
0 & B^{-1}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & -\gamma^T B^{-1}\\
0 & I
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Multiplying these three factors gives exactly the stated block formula.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Rooted branch estimate]\label{lem:branch}
Let \(C\) be a finite weighted tree rooted at a vertex \(r\). Let \(A\) be its Gram matrix, and assume \(A\) is positive definite. Suppose
\[
w(r)\ge d_C(r)+1,
\qquad
w(u)\ge d_C(u)\quad\text{for every }u\ne r.
\]
Let \(G=A^{-1}\), and define
\[
\alpha=(A^{-1})_{rr}=e_r^TGe_r,
\]
where \(e_r\) is the coordinate vector of \(r\). Then \(0<\alpha\le 1\), and for every \(x\in L(C)\), if \(c=x_r\), then for every \(k\in\Z\),
\[
(2k-1)c-x^2\le k(k-1)\alpha.
\]
Moreover, if \(k=0\) or \(k=1\), and equality holds with \(x\ne 0\), then \(C\) contains a vertex of weight \(1\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}

\[
0<\alpha\le 1
\]
and that every vector \(\lambda\in\Z^C\) satisfying
\[
\lambda_r\equiv 1\pmod 2,
\qquad
\lambda_u\equiv 0\pmod 2\quad(u\ne r)
\]
also satisfies
\[
\lambda^T G\lambda\ge \alpha.
\]
This is proved by induction on \(|C|\).

If \(C=\{r\}\), then \(A=[w(r)]\). The root hypothesis gives \(w(r)\ge 1\), and positive definiteness also gives \(w(r)>0\).

\[
\alpha=\frac{1}{w(r)}\le 1.

If \(\lambda_r=s\) is odd, then \(s^2\ge 1\), so
\[
\lambda^TG\lambda=\frac{s^2}{w(r)}\ge \frac{1}{w(r)}=\alpha.
\]
This proves the base case.

Assume now that \(|C|>1\). Let \(C_1,\dots,C_m\) be the connected components of \(C-\{r\}\), and let \(r_i\) be the unique vertex of \(C_i\) adjacent to \(r\). Root \(C_i\) at \(r_i\). Let \(A_i\) be the Gram matrix of \(C_i\), set \(G_i=A_i^{-1}\), let \(e_i=e_{r_i}\), and put

\alpha_i=e_i^TG_ie_i.

Each \(A_i\) is positive definite because it is a principal submatrix of \(A\). Also, each rooted tree \(C_i\) satisfies the same type of weight hypotheses: since \(r_i\ne r\),
\[
w(r_i)\ge d_C(r_i)=d_{C_i}(r_i)+1,

and every other vertex \(u\in C_i\) satisfies
\[
w(u)\ge d_C(u)=d_{C_i}(u).
\]
Therefore, by induction,
\[

\qquad\text{for all }i.

\end{pmatrix},

\[

then \(S>0\) and
\[
\alpha=(A^{-1})_{rr}=\frac{1}{S}.

Since \(B^{-1}=G_1\oplus\cdots\oplus G_m\), we have

\eta^TB^{-1}\eta=\sum_i \alpha_i.

Also \(m=d_C(r)\), so the root hypothesis and the inductive bounds \(\alpha_i\le 1\) imply
\[
S=w(r)-\sum_i\alpha_i
\ge (m+1)-m

Thus \(0<\alpha=1/S\le 1\).

\begin{lemma}[Calculation at a vertex]\label{lem:bad-vertex}
Let \(T\) be a finite weighted tree whose form is positive definite, and let \(b\) be a vertex of \(T\). Let \(C\) range over the components of \(T-\{b\}\). For each such component \(C\), let \(r_C\) be the unique vertex of \(C\) adjacent to \(b\), let \(A_C\) be the Gram matrix of \(C\), and define
\[
\alpha_C=(A_C^{-1})_{r_Cr_C}.
\]
Then
\[
\sum_C \alpha_C<w(b).
\]
Moreover, if
\[
x=kb+\sum_C x_C,
\]
where \(k\in\Z\) and \(x_C\in L(C)\), and if \(c_C=(x_C)_{r_C}\), then
\[
x\cdot(b-x)
=
k(1-k)w(b)
+
\sum_C\Bigl((2k-1)c_C-x_C^2\Bigr).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If \(T=\{b\}\), then there are no components \(C\), the sum \(\sum_C\alpha_C\) is empty, and the first assertion says \(0<w(b)\), which follows from positive definiteness because \(w(b)=b^2\). The displayed formula also reduces immediately to
\[
(kb)\cdot(b-kb)=k(1-k)w(b).
\]
Thus assume \(T\ne \{b\}\).

Order the vertices of \(T\) by putting \(b\) first, followed by the vertices in the components \(C\) of \(T-\{b\}\). With respect to this order, the full Gram matrix has block form
\[
\begin{pmatrix}
w(b) & -\eta^T\\
-\eta & B
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B=\bigoplus_C A_C,
\]
where the \(C\)-block of \(\eta\) is \(e_{r_C}\). The matrix \(B\) is positive definite because it is a principal submatrix of the positive definite Gram matrix of \(T\). Applying Lemma \ref{lem:schur}, the Schur complement is positive:
\[
w(b)-\eta^TB^{-1}\eta>0.
\]
Since \(B^{-1}=\bigoplus_C A_C^{-1}\), we have
\[
\eta^TB^{-1}\eta=\sum_C \alpha_C.
\]
Therefore
\[
\sum_C\alpha_C<w(b).
\]

Now write
\[
x=kb+\sum_C x_C.
\]
Different components of \(T-\{b\}\) are orthogonal to one another, and \(b\) pairs nontrivially with a component \(C\) only at the root \(r_C\), where \(b\cdot r_C=-1\). Hence
\[
b\cdot x=kw(b)-\sum_C c_C.
\]
Also,
\[
x^2
=
k^2w(b)-2k\sum_C c_C+\sum_C x_C^2.
\]
Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
x\cdot(b-x)
&=x\cdot b-x^2\\
&=
\left(kw(b)-\sum_C c_C\right)
-
\left(k^2w(b)-2k\sum_C c_C+\sum_C x_C^2\right)\\
&=
k(1-k)w(b)
+
\sum_C\Bigl((2k-1)c_C-x_C^2\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]
This proves the formula.
\end{proof}

\subsection*{Proof of the theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:main}]
Let \(b\) denote the unique vertex of \(T\) satisfying \(w(b)<d_T(b)\). Thus every vertex \(u\ne b\) satisfies
\[
w(u)\ge d_T(u).
\]

If \(T\) contains a vertex of weight \(1\), then Lemma \ref{lem:weight-one} shows that this vertex is irreducible, and the theorem follows. Hence assume from now on that \(T\) contains no vertex of weight \(1\). We will prove that \(b\) is irreducible.

Suppose, for contradiction, that \(b\) is reducible. Then there exists \(x\in L(T)\) such that
\[
x\ne 0,
\qquad
b-x\ne 0,
\qquad
x\cdot(b-x)\ge 0.
\]
Decompose \(x\) according to the components of \(T-\{b\}\):
\[
x=kb+\sum_C x_C,
\]
where \(k\in\Z\), each \(x_C\in L(C)\), and the sum ranges over the components \(C\) of \(T-\{b\}\). Let \(r_C\) be the vertex of \(C\) adjacent to \(b\), and put
\[
c_C=(x_C)_{r_C}.
\]

We now verify that each rooted component \(C\), rooted at \(r_C\), satisfies the hypotheses of Lemma \ref{lem:branch}. Since \(r_C\ne b\), the uniqueness of \(b\) gives
\[
w(r_C)\ge d_T(r_C)=d_C(r_C)+1,
\]
because the only edge incident to \(r_C\) that is not in \(C\) is the edge from \(r_C\) to \(b\). If \(u\in C\) and \(u\ne r_C\), then no edge incident to \(u\) was removed when passing from \(T\) to \(C\), so
\[
w(u)\ge d_T(u)=d_C(u).
\]
\review{This discussion of vertex degree seems like an unnecessary level of detail.}
Finally, the Gram matrix \(A_C\) of \(C\) is positive definite because it is a principal submatrix of the positive definite Gram matrix of \(T\). Thus Lemma \ref{lem:branch} applies to each component \(C\).

Let
\[
\alpha_C=(A_C^{-1})_{r_Cr_C}.
\]
Applying Lemma \ref{lem:branch} to each component, with the same integer \(k\), gives
\[
(2k-1)c_C-x_C^2\le k(k-1)\alpha_C.
\]
Using Lemma \ref{lem:bad-vertex}, we obtain
\[
\begin{aligned}
x\cdot(b-x)
&=
k(1-k)w(b)
+
\sum_C\Bigl((2k-1)c_C-x_C^2\Bigr)\\
&\le
k(1-k)w(b)+k(k-1)\sum_C\alpha_C\\
&=
k(k-1)\left(\sum_C\alpha_C-w(b)\right).
\end{aligned}
\]
By Lemma \ref{lem:bad-vertex},
\[
\sum_C\alpha_C-w(b)<0.
\]
If \(k\notin\{0,1\}\), then \(k(k-1)>0\), so the last upper bound is strictly negative. This contradicts \(x\cdot(b-x)\ge 0\). Therefore
\[
k\in\{0,1\}.
\]

For \(k=0\) or \(k=1\), we have \(k(1-k)=0\), and Lemma \ref{lem:branch} gives
\[
(2k-1)c_C-x_C^2\le 0
\qquad\text{for every component }C.
\]
Lemma \ref{lem:bad-vertex} therefore gives
\[
x\cdot(b-x)
=
\sum_C\Bigl((2k-1)c_C-x_C^2\Bigr),
\]
a sum of nonpositive terms. Since \(x\cdot(b-x)\ge 0\), every term in this sum must be equal to \(0\). Hence, for every component \(C\),
\[
(2k-1)c_C-x_C^2=0.
\]
If some \(x_C\ne 0\), then the equality statement in Lemma \ref{lem:branch} implies that \(C\), and therefore \(T\), contains a vertex of weight \(1\). This contradicts our standing assumption that \(T\) has no weight-one vertex. Thus
\[
x_C=0
\qquad\text{for every component }C.
\]
Consequently \(x=kb\). If \(k=0\), then \(x=0\), contradicting \(x\ne 0\). If \(k=1\), then \(b-x=0\), contradicting \(b-x\ne 0\). Both possibilities are impossible.

Therefore no such decomposition of \(b\) exists, and \(b\) is irreducible. Hence \(T\) contains an irreducible vertex.
\end{proof}
