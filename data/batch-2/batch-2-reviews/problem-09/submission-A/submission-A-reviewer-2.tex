\section{Review}
%\paragraph{Summary. } This submission successfully solves the problem by showing  the coefficients $\{c_{(a, 1^b)}(n)\}$ count ordered set partitions $\sigma$ of sets of the form $\{1, \ldots, r\}$ (over all $2 \leq r \leq n$) which satisfy certain special conditions. The conditions involve  the \textit{inversions} of $\sigma$, whether $r$ is in its own block, and the number of blocks of $\sigma$.

The proof is quite elegant and short, with the main points being:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Use diagonal supersymmetry and the structure of super schur functions for hook shapes to show the Hilbert series of the superspace \footnote{one commuting variable, one anticommuting variable} coinvariant ring  is of the form $H_n = 1 + (q + z)\sum_{a \geq 1, b \geq 0} c_{(a, 1^b)}(n)q^{a - 1}z^b$.
    \item Use the Rhoades--Wilson formula for $H_n$, which is in terms of $q$-Stirling numbers, and classical recursions of $q$-Stirling numbers to show that $H_n$ satisfies $\frac{H_n - 1}{q + z} = \sum_{r = 1}^{n - 1}\sum_{k = 1}^r[k]_q F_{r, k}z^{r - k}$, where $F_{r, k} = [k]!_q \Stir_q(r, k)$. 
    \item Show that $[k]_qF_{r, k}$ is the generating function tracking inversions of ordered set partitions of $\{1, 2, \ldots, r + 1\}$ into $k$  parts with $r + 1$ not in its own block.
    \item Finally, to prove the claimed interpretation, simply substitute (3) into (2), then extract the coefficient of $q^{a - 1}z^b$, which by (1), matches $c_{(a, 1^b)}(n)$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Correctness.}
This proof seems to be correct, as far as I'm able to tell. There are some incorrect (or confusing) superfluous details, and there might be a missing (but very easy) edge case (see inline comments). Otherwise, the argument seems true.

\paragraph{Novelty.}  In some sense, I find this combinatorial interpretation the most natural and elegant of the four model solutions -- it is certainly the easiest formula to compute examples with! The proof is also much shorter than the others, and also much shorter than the human solution. However, given that the combinatorics of the superspace ring has a lot to do with ordered set partitions of $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$, it in some sense feels ``unnatural'' that this interpretation is given in terms of ordered set partitions of $\{1, \ldots, r\}$ as $r$ varies, rather than just for $r = n$. (The human solution is the only one whose combinatorial interpretation is in terms of special ordered set partitions of $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}.$)

\paragraph{Attributions.} Although this submission includes  all  the papers it used in its final bibliography, it is missing several important in-text citations. It says some statements are ``standard'' which are actually the main results of these recent papers. The rare in-text citation does not give explicit theorem statements,  putting extra work on the reader.

\paragraph{Exposition.} The claim is proven all in one theorem. It would be nicer to read if it was broken into several lemmas. There are also a couple of unnecessary/irrelevant comments which do not make sense to me, though this doesn't actually impact the argument.

\begin{center}
{\bf Hook coefficients in the universal Hilbert series of diagonal coinvariants}
\end{center}

\subsection*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I interpret \(R_n^{(m)}\) as the diagonal coinvariant algebra
\[
 R_n^{(m)}=\mathbb C[x_i^{(r)}:1\le i\le n,\ 1\le r\le m]\,
/\,\langle \mathbb C[x_i^{(r)}]^{\mathfrak S_n}_{+}\rangle,
\]
where \(\mathfrak S_n\) acts by simultaneously permuting the subscript \(i\) in all
\(m\) sets of variables.  Thus the coefficients \(c_\lambda(n)\) are the stable
multiplicities of the polynomial \(GL_m\)-irreducibles \(S_\lambda(\mathbb C^m)\).
\review{Although this does not actually matter to the argument, I am a bit confused by the meaning of the last sentence. This would make more sense to me if the coefficients $c_\lambda(n)$ were arising by somehow looking at the character of an action of $GL_m$ on $R_n^{(m)}$, but they are instead arising within the \textit{Hilbert series} of $R_n^{(m)}$?}

\subsection*{The combinatorial model}

For \(r,k\ge 1\), let \(\OP_{r,k}\) be the set of ordered set partitions
\(\sigma=(B_1|\cdots|B_k)\) of \([r]\) into \(k\) nonempty blocks.  An inversion of
\(\sigma\) is a pair \((i,j)\) such that
\[
        i<j,\qquad i=\min B_m,\qquad j\in B_\ell,\qquad \ell<m .
\]
Let \(\inv(\sigma)\) be the number of such pairs.  When \(k=r\), this is the
usual inversion number of the permutation obtained by reading the singleton
blocks from left to right.

\begin{theorem}
Let \(\lambda=(a,1^b)\) be a hook, with \(a\ge 1\) and \(b\ge 0\).  Then
\(c_{(a,1^b)}(n)\) is the number of ordered set partitions
\(\sigma=(B_1|\cdots|B_k)\) of \([r]\), for some \(2\le r\le n\), such that
\[
        r-k=b+1,\qquad r\hbox{ is not a singleton block of }\sigma,\qquad
        \inv(\sigma)=a-1 .
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\sum_{a\ge1,\ b\ge0} c_{(a,1^b)}(n)\,q^{a-1}z^b
=
\sum_{\substack{2\le r\le n\\1\le k\le r-1}}
\ \sum_{\substack{\sigma\in \OP_{r,k}\\
                  r\text{ not singleton in }\sigma}}
        q^{\inv(\sigma)}z^{r-k-1}.
\]
The empty partition coefficient is \(c_{\varnothing}(n)=1\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We use two standard inputs.  First, diagonal supersymmetry says \review{Should cite J. Lentfer!  This is a new paper, not a ``standard input.''} that the
universal Schur coefficients for diagonal coinvariants can be read from the
coinvariant ring with one bosonic alphabet and one fermionic alphabet,
\[
 SR_n=\bigl(\mathbb C[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\otimes
          \bigwedge(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n)\bigr)
/\langle(\mathbb C[x]\otimes\bigwedge\theta)^{\mathfrak S_n}_{+}\rangle .
\]
More explicitly, the supersymmetric Frobenius/character expansion has the same
universal multiplicity modules as the purely bosonic expansion; after applying
the dimension map to the \(\mathfrak S_n\)-Frobenius factor  one obtains exactly
the Hilbert coefficients \(c_\lambda(n)\) appearing in the question. \review{The next conclusion is correct, but I am confused by this explanation in the previous sentence -- what is meant by the ``purely bosonic expansion''?} Therefore
\[
        \Hilb(SR_n;q,z)=\sum_\lambda c_\lambda(n)s_\lambda(q/z),
\]
where \(s_\lambda(q/z)\) denotes the super Schur polynomial in one even variable
\(q\) and one odd variable \(z\).   With one even and one odd variable,
\(s_\lambda(q/z)\) vanishes unless \(\lambda\) lies in the \((1,1)\)-hook \review{What do you mean $\lambda$ lies in the $(1, 1)$-hook? Also, there should be a condition on $a$ here ($\geq 1$).}, i.e.
unless \(\lambda\) is a hook, and
\[
        s_{(a,1^b)}(q/z)=q^a z^b+q^{a-1}z^{b+1}
        =q^{a-1}z^b(q+z).
\]
Thus
\[
        \Hilb(SR_n;q,z)
        =1+(q+z)\sum_{a\ge1,\ b\ge0}
              c_{(a,1^b)}(n)q^{a-1}z^b .          \tag{1}
\]

Second, the Hilbert series of \(SR_n\), due to Rhoades--Wilson, is
\[
        H_n(q,z):=\Hilb(SR_n;q,z)
        =\sum_{k=1}^{n} z^{\,n-k}[k]!_q\,\Stir_q(n,k),                 \tag{2}
\]
where \([k]_q=1+q+\cdots+q^{k-1}\), \([k]!_q=[k]_q[k-1]_q\cdots[1]_q\), and
\[
        \Stir_q(r,k)=\Stir_q(r-1,k-1)+[k]_q\Stir_q(r-1,k).
\] \review{Missing $k = 0$ term in Hilbert series? But I guess this only matters in the edge case that $n = 0$? This trivial edge case should probably be addressed.}
The boundary conditions \review{Isn't the third set of boundary conditions (that $\Stir_q(r, k) = 0$ outside $ k \leq r$ is superfluous? Doesn't it follow from the first two sets.} are
\(\Stir_q(0,0)=1\), \(\Stir_q(r,0)=0\) for \(r>0\), and
\(\Stir_q(r,k)=0\) outside \(0\le k\le r\).  Put
\(F_{r,k}=[k]!_q\Stir_q(r,k)\).  Then \(F_{r,k}=0\) outside
\(1\le k\le r\), except for the harmless convention \(F_{0,0}=1\), and
\[
        F_{r,k}=[k]_qF_{r-1,k-1}+[k]_qF_{r-1,k}.
\]
Using \([k+1]_q=1+q[k]_q\) in (2), we get, for \(n\ge2\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
H_n(q,z)
&=\sum_{k=1}^{n}z^{n-k}\bigl([k]_qF_{n-1,k-1}
                         +[k]_qF_{n-1,k}\bigr)\\
&=H_{n-1}(q,z)
 +(q+z)\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}[k]_qF_{n-1,k}z^{n-1-k}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Since \(H_1(q,z)=1\), iteration gives
\[
        \frac{H_n(q,z)-1}{q+z}
        =
        \sum_{r=1}^{n-1}\sum_{k=1}^{r}
             [k]_q\,F_{r,k}\,z^{r-k}.                    \tag{3}
\]

It remains to identify the right side of (3).  The polynomial \(F_{r,k}\) is
the inversion enumerator of ordered set partitions:
\[
        F_{r,k}=\sum_{\tau\in \OP_{r,k}}q^{\inv(\tau)}.                \tag{4}
\]

Indeed, both sides satisfy the same recurrence.  To see this directly, insert
the largest letter \(r\) into an ordered partition of \([r-1]\).  To obtain
\(k\) final blocks, either insert \(r\) as a singleton block into one of the
\(k\) possible final block positions, or insert \(r\) into one of the \(k\)
existing blocks.  If the final position is \(i\) from the left, the new
inversions involving \(r\) are exactly the minima of the blocks to its right, so
their number is \(k-i\).  Summing over \(i=1,\ldots,k\) contributes
\([k]_q\) in each of the two cases, giving the recurrence above and the initial
condition.

Now restrict this insertion argument to insertions of \(r+1\) into an existing
block of an ordered partition \(\tau\in\OP_{r,k}\).  The result is precisely an
ordered partition \(\sigma\in\OP_{r+1,k}\) in which \(r+1\) is not a singleton,
and the extra inversion contribution is again one of \(0,1,\ldots,k-1\).
Hence
\[
 [k]_qF_{r,k}
 =
 \sum_{\substack{\sigma\in\OP_{r+1,k}\\ r+1\text{ not singleton}}}
      q^{\inv(\sigma)} .
\]
Substituting this into (3), and replacing \(r+1\) by \(r\), gives exactly
\[
\sum_{a\ge1,\ b\ge0}c_{(a,1^b)}(n)q^{a-1}z^b
=
\sum_{\substack{2\le r\le n\\1\le k\le r-1}}
\sum_{\substack{\sigma\in \OP_{r,k}\\r\text{ not singleton}}}
q^{\inv(\sigma)}z^{r-k-1}.
\]
Extracting the coefficient of \(q^{a-1}z^b\) proves the claimed interpretation.
\end{proof}
