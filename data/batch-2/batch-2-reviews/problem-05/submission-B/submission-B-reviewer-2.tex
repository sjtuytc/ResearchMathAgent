\subsection*{Overview / Strategy}

The original target is to decide whether the Markov semigroup \(P_t\) of the
Dirichlet stochastic heat equation with distributional drift \(\delta_0\) has
at most one invariant probability measure.  The first part of the argument
constructs the natural Gibbs measure
\[
\nu(\dd v)=Z^{-1}\exp\!\left(2\int_0^1\1_{\{v(x)>0\}}\dd x\right)\gamma(\dd v),
\]
where \(\gamma\) is Brownian-bridge law, and proves that \(\nu\) is invariant by
passing to the limit from the smooth mollified equations.  This is carried out
in Theorem \ref{thm:invariance}.

Uniqueness requires more than this invariant-measure construction.  The clean
abstract mechanism is that pairwise non-singularity of one transition skeleton
implies uniqueness; see Theorem \ref{thm:pairwise}.  A compact-local Hellinger
overlap estimate for the mollified last-step kernels would imply such
non-singularity in the limit; see Theorem \ref{thm:hellinger}.  A second route is
to prove that every invariant measure is absolutely continuous with respect to
\(\nu\); see Theorem \ref{thm:uac} and Proposition \ref{prop:resolvent}.  The
stated ABLM-type assumptions provide existence, uniqueness in law, and weak
convergence of the mollified equations, but they do not themselves provide any
of these deterministic-start smoothing or overlap estimates.  Thus the
unconditional conclusion proved here is invariance of \(\nu\), together with
precise conditional criteria under which the desired uniqueness follows.

\subsection{Standing assumptions, notation, and the target statement}

Let
\[
E:=C_0([0,1])
=\{v\in C([0,1],\R):v(0)=v(1)=0\},
\qquad H:=L^2(0,1),
\]
with \(E\) equipped with the supremum norm.  Let
\[
e_k(x):=\sqrt2\sin(k\pi x),\qquad \lambda_k:=\pi^2k^2,\qquad k\ge1,
\]
be the Dirichlet eigenbasis of \(-\partial_{xx}\).  Let \(\gamma\) denote
Brownian-bridge law on \(E\), equivalently the centered Gaussian measure with
covariance \((-\partial_{xx})^{-1}\):
\[
\E_\gamma[(v,e_k)_H(v,e_\ell)_H]=\frac{\delta_{k\ell}}{\lambda_k}.
\]

The SPDE from the original problem is
\begin{align}
\tag{\(*\)}\label{eq:spde}
\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}(x,t)
&=\frac12\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial x^2}(x,t)
+\delta_0(u(x,t))+\dot W(x,t),\qquad t\ge0,\\
u(x,0)&=u_0(x)\in E,\nonumber\\
u(0,t)&=u(1,t)=0.\nonumber
\end{align}

\begin{assumption}[ABLM-type input on the bounded interval]\label{ass:ablm}
For every \(u_0\in E\) and \(T>0\), there is a unique-in-law adapted mild
solution \(u\in C([0,T],E)\) to \eqref{eq:spde}, and the solutions define a
Markov semigroup \((P_t)_{t\ge0}\) on \(E\).  For
\[
b_n(r):=G_{1/n}(r)=\sqrt{\frac n{2\pi}}e^{-nr^2/2},
\]
let \(P_t^n\) be the semigroup of the mollified equation obtained by replacing
\(\delta_0\) with \(b_n\).  For every \(t>0\), \(v\in E\), and \(f\in C_b(E)\),
\[
P_t^n f(v)\longrightarrow P_tf(v),
\]
or equivalently \(P_t^n(v,\cdot)\Rightarrow P_t(v,\cdot)\).
\end{assumption}

Define
\[
F_n(r):=\int_{-\infty}^r b_n(s)\dd s,\qquad
\Phi_n(v):=\int_0^1F_n(v(x))\dd x,\qquad
\Phi(v):=\int_0^1\1_{\{v(x)>0\}}\dd x.
\]
Set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gibbs}
\nu_n(\dd v):=Z_n^{-1}e^{2\Phi_n(v)}\gamma(\dd v),
\qquad
\nu(\dd v):=Z^{-1}e^{2\Phi(v)}\gamma(\dd v),
\end{equation}
where \(Z_n\) and \(Z\) are the normalizing constants.  Since
\(0\le\Phi_n,\Phi\le1\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:density-bounds}
1\le Z_n,Z\le e^2,
\qquad
e^{-2}\le \frac{\dd\nu_n}{\dd\gamma},\frac{\dd\nu}{\dd\gamma}\le e^2.
\end{equation}

For a Markov kernel \(K\) and a probability measure \(\mu\), write
\(\mu K(A):=\int_EK(x,A)\mu(\dd x)\).  A probability measure \(\mu\) is invariant
for a semigroup \((P_t)\) if \(\mu P_t=\mu\) for all \(t\ge0\).

\begin{theorem}[Original problem: target and established claim]\label{thm:headline}
The target assertion from the original problem is
\[
\tag{U}\label{eq:target}
\text{\((P_t)\) has at most one invariant probability measure on \(E\).}
\]
Under Assumption \ref{ass:ablm}, the Gibbs measure \(\nu\) in
\eqref{eq:gibbs} is invariant.  Moreover, \eqref{eq:target} follows if one adds
any of the deterministic-start smoothing or overlap hypotheses in Theorem
\ref{thm:hellinger}, Theorem \ref{thm:uac}, or Proposition
\ref{prop:resolvent} together with Proposition \ref{prop:acunique}.  In that
case the unique invariant probability measure is \(\nu\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The invariance statement is Theorem \ref{thm:invariance}.  The Hellinger
overlap hypothesis of Theorem \ref{thm:hellinger} implies pairwise
non-singularity of one skeleton, and Theorem \ref{thm:pairwise} then gives
uniqueness.  The compact-local uniform absolute-continuity hypothesis of
Theorem \ref{thm:uac} gives \(\mu\ll\nu\) for every invariant \(\mu\), and its
third hypothesis, supplied for instance by Proposition \ref{prop:acunique},
then gives \(\mu=\nu\).  Finally, Proposition \ref{prop:resolvent} is a
resolvent version of the same absolute-continuity route.  Since
Theorem \ref{thm:invariance} supplies an invariant measure, any ``at most one''
conclusion identifies the invariant law with \(\nu\).
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Gibbs invariant measure}

\begin{theorem}[Invariance of the Gibbs measure]\label{thm:invariance}
Under Assumption \ref{ass:ablm}, the measure
\[
\nu(\dd v)
=
Z^{-1}\exp\!\left(2\int_0^1\1_{\{v(x)>0\}}\dd x\right)\gamma(\dd v)
\]
is invariant for \((P_t)\).
\end{theorem}
\review{This should be cited to Bounebache--Zambotti (2014).}

\begin{proof}
We prove this by approximation.

\emph{Step 1: \(\nu_n\to\nu\) in total variation.}
For each \(r\ne0\), \(F_n(r)\to\1_{\{r>0\}}\).  Let \(B\sim\gamma\).  For
\(x\in(0,1)\), \(B(x)\) is a nondegenerate centered Gaussian, hence
\(\gamma(B(x)=0)=0\).  Therefore, by Fubini,
\[
\E_\gamma\bigl|\{x\in[0,1]:B(x)=0\}\bigr|
=\int_0^1\gamma(B(x)=0)\dd x=0,
\]
where the endpoints do not matter.  Thus \(|\{x:B(x)=0\}|=0\) for
\(\gamma\)-a.e. \(B\).  For such \(B\),
\[
\Phi_n(B)=\int_0^1F_n(B(x))\dd x
\longrightarrow
\int_0^1\1_{\{B(x)>0\}}\dd x=\Phi(B)
\]
by dominated convergence in \(x\).  Since \(0\le\Phi_n,\Phi\le1\), dominated
convergence in \(\gamma\) gives \(Z_n\to Z\) and
\[
\int_E\left|
Z_n^{-1}e^{2\Phi_n(v)}-Z^{-1}e^{2\Phi(v)}
\right|\gamma(\dd v)\longrightarrow0.
\]
Hence \(\|\nu_n-\nu\|_{\TV}\to0\).

\emph{Step 2: \(\nu_n\) is invariant for \(P_t^n\).}
For fixed \(n\), the drift \(b_n\) is bounded and Lipschitz.  Also
\(\Phi_n\) has \(H\)-gradient \(D\Phi_n(v)=b_n(v)\), because for
\(h\in H\),
\[
D\Phi_n(v)[h]=\int_0^1b_n(v(x))h(x)\dd x=\ip{b_n(v)}{h}_H.
\]
On smooth cylinder functions the formal generator is
\[
L_nF(v)=\frac12\operatorname{Tr}D^2F(v)
-\frac12\ip{-\partial_{xx}v}{DF(v)}_H
+\ip{D\Phi_n(v)}{DF(v)}_H.
\]
The Gaussian integration-by-parts formula for \(\gamma\), together with
\(\dd\nu_n=Z_n^{-1}e^{2\Phi_n}\dd\gamma\), gives for smooth cylinder \(F,G\)
\[
\int_EG\,L_nF\,\dd\nu_n
=
-\frac12\int_E\ip{DG}{DF}_H\dd\nu_n.
\]
Indeed, the derivative of \(e^{2\Phi_n}\) contributes
\(2e^{2\Phi_n}D\Phi_n\), which cancels exactly the added drift term
\(\ip{D\Phi_n}{DF}_H\).  Taking \(G\equiv1\) gives
\[
\int_EL_nF\,\dd\nu_n=0.
\]
For bounded Lipschitz gradient drifts, the standard martingale-problem or
symmetric-Dirichlet-form construction of the stochastic heat equation identifies
\(P_t^n\) with the Markov semigroup generated by the closure of \(L_n\); see,
for example, \cite{DPZ14,MR92}.  Infinitesimal invariance on the cylinder core
therefore implies
\[
\nu_nP_t^n=\nu_n,\qquad t\ge0.
\]

\emph{Step 3: pass to the singular limit.}
Let \(f\in C_b(E)\) and \(t>0\).  By Assumption \ref{ass:ablm},
\(P_t^nf(v)\to P_tf(v)\) for every \(v\in E\).  Using
\(\dd\nu_n/\dd\gamma\le e^2\), dominated convergence gives
\[
\int_E|P_t^nf-P_tf|\dd\nu_n
\le
e^2\int_E|P_t^nf-P_tf|\dd\gamma
\longrightarrow0.
\]
Also, \(\nu_n\to\nu\) in total variation and \(P_tf\) is bounded, so
\[
\int_EP_tf\dd\nu_n\longrightarrow\int_EP_tf\dd\nu,
\qquad
\int_Ef\dd\nu_n\longrightarrow\int_Ef\dd\nu.
\]
Since \(\nu_nP_t^n=\nu_n\),
\[
\int_EP_tf\dd\nu
=
\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_EP_t^nf\dd\nu_n
=
\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_Ef\dd\nu_n
=
\int_Ef\dd\nu.
\]
Thus \(\nu P_t=\nu\) for all \(t>0\), and the case \(t=0\) is trivial.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Pairwise overlap and Hellinger criteria}

\begin{theorem}[Pairwise non-singularity of one skeleton implies uniqueness]\label{thm:pairwise}
Let \(E\) be Polish and \(K\) a Markov kernel on \(E\).  If
\[
K(x,\cdot)\not\perp K(y,\cdot)\qquad\forall x,y\in E,
\]
then \(K\) has at most one invariant probability measure.  Consequently, if a
semigroup \((P_t)\) has some \(t_0>0\) such that
\(P_{t_0}(x,\cdot)\not\perp P_{t_0}(y,\cdot)\) for all \(x,y\), then
\((P_t)\) has at most one invariant probability measure.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Assume that \(\mu_1\ne\mu_2\) are invariant for \(K\).  Let
\(\sigma:=\mu_1-\mu_2\), and write its Jordan decomposition
\(\sigma=\sigma^+-\sigma^-\).  Since \(\sigma(E)=0\),
\(m:=\sigma^+(E)=\sigma^-(E)>0\).

For every finite signed measure \(\eta\),
\[
|\eta K|\le |\eta|K.
\]
Indeed, for Borel \(A\), taking the supremum over finite measurable partitions
\(\{A_i\}\) of \(A\),
\[
|\eta K|(A)
\le
\sup_{\{A_i\}}\sum_i\int_EK(x,A_i)|\eta|(\dd x)
=
\int_EK(x,A)|\eta|(\dd x).
\]
Since \(\sigma K=\sigma\), we get \(|\sigma|\le|\sigma|K\).  The two measures
have equal total mass, so \(|\sigma|K=|\sigma|\).  Hence
\[
\sigma^+K=\frac12(|\sigma|K+\sigma K)=\sigma^+,
\qquad
\sigma^-K=\frac12(|\sigma|K-\sigma K)=\sigma^-.
\]
Thus \(\alpha:=\sigma^+/m\) and \(\beta:=\sigma^-/m\) are invariant
probabilities, and \(\alpha\perp\beta\).  Choose a Borel set \(A\) with
\(\alpha(A)=1\) and \(\beta(A)=0\).  Invariance gives
\[
1=\int_EK(x,A)\alpha(\dd x),\qquad
0=\int_EK(y,A)\beta(\dd y).
\]
Therefore \(K(x,A)=1\) for \(\alpha\)-a.e. \(x\) and \(K(y,A)=0\) for
\(\beta\)-a.e. \(y\).  Choosing such \(x,y\), \(K(x,\cdot)\) is concentrated on
\(A\) while \(K(y,\cdot)\) is concentrated on \(A^c\), so
\(K(x,\cdot)\perp K(y,\cdot)\), a contradiction.  The semigroup assertion
follows because every invariant measure for \((P_t)\) is invariant for the
skeleton \(P_{t_0}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Hellinger affinity]
For probability measures \(\mu,\rho\) on \(E\), define
\[
\Aff(\mu,\rho)
:=
\int_E\sqrt{\frac{\dd\mu}{\dd\lambda}\frac{\dd\rho}{\dd\lambda}}\,\dd\lambda,
\]
where \(\lambda\) is any finite measure dominating both \(\mu\) and \(\rho\).
The value is independent of \(\lambda\).  We use the normalized total variation
norm
\[
\|\mu-\rho\|_{\TV}:=\sup_{A\in\cB(E)}|\mu(A)-\rho(A)|.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Elementary Hellinger facts]\label{lem:hellfacts}
For probability measures \(\mu,\rho\),
\[
\mu\perp\rho
\iff
\|\mu-\rho\|_{\TV}=1
\iff
\Aff(\mu,\rho)=0,
\]
and
\[
\|\mu-\rho\|_{\TV}
\le
\sqrt{1-\Aff(\mu,\rho)^2}.
\]
Moreover, if \(\mathcal P\) ranges over finite measurable partitions of \(E\),
then
\[
\Aff(\mu,\rho)
=
\inf_{\mathcal P}\sum_{B\in\mathcal P}\sqrt{\mu(B)\rho(B)}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Take \(\lambda=\mu+\rho\), and write \(f=\dd\mu/\dd\lambda\),
\(g=\dd\rho/\dd\lambda\).  The definition is independent of \(\lambda\) because
changing the dominating measure multiplies both densities by the same
Radon--Nikodym factor.  If \(\mu\perp\rho\), then \(fg=0\) \(\lambda\)-a.e., so
\(\Aff(\mu,\rho)=0\).  Conversely, if \(\Aff(\mu,\rho)=0\), then
\(\sqrt{fg}=0\) \(\lambda\)-a.e.; the sets \(\{f>0\}\) and \(\{g>0\}\) separate
\(\mu\) and \(\rho\), so \(\mu\perp\rho\).  The equivalence with
\(\|\mu-\rho\|_{\TV}=1\) is the standard characterization of singularity for
probability measures.

Also,
\[
\begin{aligned}
\|\mu-\rho\|_{\TV}
&=\frac12\int_E|f-g|\dd\lambda                                      \\
&=\frac12\int_E|\sqrt f-\sqrt g|(\sqrt f+\sqrt g)\dd\lambda          \\
&\le \frac12
\left(\int_E(\sqrt f-\sqrt g)^2\dd\lambda\right)^{1/2}
\left(\int_E(\sqrt f+\sqrt g)^2\dd\lambda\right)^{1/2}               \\
&=\sqrt{1-\Aff(\mu,\rho)^2}.
\end{aligned}
\]

For the partition formula, let \(\mathcal P\) be finite.  On each atom \(B\),
\[
\int_B\sqrt{\E_\lambda(f\mid\mathcal P)\E_\lambda(g\mid\mathcal P)}\dd\lambda
=
\sqrt{\mu(B)\rho(B)}.
\]
Conditional Cauchy--Schwarz gives
\[
\E_\lambda(\sqrt{fg}\mid\mathcal P)
\le
\sqrt{\E_\lambda(f\mid\mathcal P)\E_\lambda(g\mid\mathcal P)},
\]
so every partition sum is at least \(\Aff(\mu,\rho)\).  Since \(E\) is Polish,
\(\cB(E)\) is countably generated.  Choosing finite partitions increasing to a
countable generating algebra, the bounded martingale convergence theorem
(applied with \(\lambda=\mu+\rho\), so \(0\le f,g\le1\)) gives partition sums
converging down to \(\int\sqrt{fg}\dd\lambda\).  Hence the infimum equals
\(\Aff(\mu,\rho)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Mixture lower bound]\label{lem:mixture}
Let \(K\) be a Markov kernel on \(E\).  Suppose \(C\subset E\) is measurable and
there is \(a>0\) such that
\[
\Aff(K(v,\cdot),K(w,\cdot))\ge a\qquad\forall v,w\in C.
\]
Then, for all probability measures \(\alpha,\beta\),
\[
\Aff(\alpha K,\beta K)\ge a\,\alpha(C)\beta(C).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\mathcal P\) be a finite partition.  For each atom \(B\),
\[
\sqrt{(\alpha K)(B)(\beta K)(B)}
\ge
\int_C\int_C\sqrt{K(v,B)K(w,B)}\,\alpha(\dd v)\beta(\dd w),
\]
by Cauchy--Schwarz applied to the functions \(\sqrt{K(v,B)}\) and
\(\sqrt{K(w,B)}\).  Summing over \(B\in\mathcal P\) gives
\[
\sum_{B\in\mathcal P}\sqrt{(\alpha K)(B)(\beta K)(B)}
\ge
\int_C\int_C
\sum_{B\in\mathcal P}\sqrt{K(v,B)K(w,B)}
\,\alpha(\dd v)\beta(\dd w).
\]
The inner sum is at least
\(\Aff(K(v,\cdot),K(w,\cdot))\) by Lemma \ref{lem:hellfacts}; hence the last
display is at least \(a\alpha(C)\beta(C)\).  Taking the infimum over
\(\mathcal P\) and using the partition formula proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Lower semicontinuity of total variation]\label{lem:tv-lsc}
If \(\mu_n\Rightarrow\mu\) and \(\rho_n\Rightarrow\rho\) weakly on a Polish
space, then
\[
\|\mu-\rho\|_{\TV}
\le
\liminf_{n\to\infty}\|\mu_n-\rho_n\|_{\TV}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For \(\eta=\mu-\rho\), regularity of the finite signed measure \(\eta\) implies
\[
\|\eta\|_{\TV}
=
\sup_{\substack{f\in C_b(E)\\0\le f\le1}}
\left|\int_Ef\dd\eta\right|.
\]
Indeed, indicators of Borel sets are approximated from inside and outside by
continuous functions using regularity of \(|\eta|\) and Urysohn's lemma.  For
each such \(f\),
\[
\int_Ef\dd(\mu_n-\rho_n)\longrightarrow\int_Ef\dd(\mu-\rho),
\]
so
\[
\left|\int_Ef\dd(\mu-\rho)\right|
\le
\liminf_n\|\mu_n-\rho_n\|_{\TV}.
\]
Taking the supremum over \(f\) proves the result.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Conditional uniqueness from compact-local Hellinger overlap]\label{thm:hellinger}
Let \(E\) be Polish.  Let \((P_s)\) and \((P_s^n)\) be Markov semigroups on
\(E\).  Fix \(0<\varepsilon<t\).  Assume:
\begin{enumerate}
\item for every \(x\in E\),
\[
P_t^n(x,\cdot)\Rightarrow P_t(x,\cdot),
\qquad
P_{t-\varepsilon}^n(x,\cdot)\Rightarrow P_{t-\varepsilon}(x,\cdot);
\]
\item for every compact \(C\subset E\), there is \(a(C)>0\) such that
\[
\Aff(P_\varepsilon^n(v,\cdot),P_\varepsilon^n(w,\cdot))\ge a(C)
\]
for all \(n\ge1\) and all \(v,w\in C\).
\end{enumerate}
Then \(P_t(x,\cdot)\not\perp P_t(y,\cdot)\) for all \(x,y\in E\).  Consequently,
if this holds for some \(t>0\), then \((P_s)\) has at most one invariant
probability measure.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(x,y\in E\), and set
\[
\alpha_n:=P_{t-\varepsilon}^n(x,\cdot),\qquad
\beta_n:=P_{t-\varepsilon}^n(y,\cdot).
\]
The two sequences are tight, because they are weakly convergent.  Hence there
is a compact \(C\subset E\) such that
\(\alpha_n(C)\ge1/2\) and \(\beta_n(C)\ge1/2\) for all \(n\).  By hypothesis 2,
\[
\Aff(P_\varepsilon^n(v,\cdot),P_\varepsilon^n(w,\cdot))\ge a(C),
\qquad v,w\in C.
\]
Using the semigroup property and Lemma \ref{lem:mixture},
\[
\Aff(P_t^n(x,\cdot),P_t^n(y,\cdot))
=
\Aff(\alpha_nP_\varepsilon^n,\beta_nP_\varepsilon^n)
\ge
a(C)\alpha_n(C)\beta_n(C)\ge \frac{a(C)}4.
\]
By Lemma \ref{lem:hellfacts},
\[
\|P_t^n(x,\cdot)-P_t^n(y,\cdot)\|_{\TV}
\le
\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{a(C)}4\right)^2}
=:\rho<1.
\]
Weak convergence at time \(t\), followed by Lemma \ref{lem:tv-lsc}, yields
\[
\|P_t(x,\cdot)-P_t(y,\cdot)\|_{\TV}\le\rho<1.
\]
Thus the two limiting transition probabilities are not singular.  The final
claim follows from Theorem \ref{thm:pairwise}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Absolute continuity routes and the Dirichlet-form limitation}

\begin{theorem}[Conditional uniqueness from compact-local uniform absolute continuity]\label{thm:uac}
Let \(E=C_0([0,1])\), and let \(\nu\) be the Gibbs measure in
\eqref{eq:gibbs}.  Fix \(t_0>0\).  Assume:
\begin{enumerate}
\item for every \(x\in E\) and \(f\in C_b(E)\),
\[
P_{t_0}^nf(x)\to P_{t_0}f(x);
\]
\item for every compact \(K\subset E\), the family
\[
\{P_{t_0}^n(x,\cdot): n\ge1,\ x\in K\}
\]
is uniformly absolutely continuous with respect to \(\nu\);
\item every \(P_t\)-invariant probability measure that is absolutely continuous
with respect to \(\nu\) equals \(\nu\).
\end{enumerate}
Then \(\nu\) is the unique invariant probability measure of \((P_t)\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\mu\) be invariant for \((P_t)\), and set
\(\rho_n:=\mu P_{t_0}^n\).  For \(f\in C_b(E)\), dominated convergence and
hypothesis 1 give
\[
\int_Ef\dd\rho_n
=
\int_EP_{t_0}^nf(x)\mu(\dd x)
\longrightarrow
\int_EP_{t_0}f(x)\mu(\dd x)
=
\int_Ef\dd\mu,
\]
so \(\rho_n\Rightarrow\mu\).

Let \(B\) be Borel with \(\nu(B)=0\).  Fix \(\varepsilon,\eta>0\).  By tightness
of \(\mu\), choose compact \(K\) with \(\mu(K^c)<\eta\).  By uniform absolute
continuity on \(K\), choose \(\delta>0\) such that
\[
\nu(A)<\delta
\implies
\sup_{n\ge1}\sup_{x\in K}P_{t_0}^n(x,A)<\varepsilon.
\]
By outer regularity, choose open \(O\supset B\) with \(\nu(O)<\delta\).  Then
for every \(n\),
\[
\rho_n(O)
=
\int_EP_{t_0}^n(x,O)\mu(\dd x)
\le
\varepsilon+\eta.
\]
Since \(O\) is open and \(\rho_n\Rightarrow\mu\), Portmanteau gives
\[
\mu(O)\le\liminf_n\rho_n(O)\le\varepsilon+\eta.
\]
Thus \(\mu(B)\le\varepsilon+\eta\).  Letting \(\varepsilon,\eta\downarrow0\)
shows \(\mu\ll\nu\).  Hypothesis 3 gives \(\mu=\nu\).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[A simple sufficient condition for uniform absolute continuity]
Hypothesis 2 of Theorem \ref{thm:uac} follows if, for every compact
\(K\subset E\), there are \(p>1\) and \(C_K<\infty\) such that
\(P_{t_0}^n(x,\cdot)=r_{n,x}\nu\) and
\[
\sup_{n\ge1}\sup_{x\in K}\|r_{n,x}\|_{L^p(\nu)}\le C_K.
\]
Indeed, with \(q=p/(p-1)\), Holder's inequality gives
\(P_{t_0}^n(x,A)\le C_K\nu(A)^{1/q}\).
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Pointwise resolvent absolute continuity]\label{prop:resolvent}
Assume that \(\nu\) is invariant and that every invariant probability measure
absolutely continuous with respect to \(\nu\) equals \(\nu\).  If, for some
\(\lambda>0\),
\[
R_\lambda(x,\cdot)
:=
\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t}P_t(x,\cdot)\dd t
\ll\nu
\qquad\forall x\in E,
\]
then \(\nu\) is the unique invariant probability measure.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\mu\) be invariant and let \(B\) be Borel with \(\nu(B)=0\).  Then
\(R_\lambda(x,B)=0\) for every \(x\), and therefore
\[
0
=
\int_ER_\lambda(x,B)\mu(\dd x)
=
\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t}\mu P_t(B)\dd t
=
\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t}\mu(B)\dd t
=
\frac1\lambda\mu(B).
\]
Thus \(\mu\ll\nu\), and by the assumed uniqueness among \(\nu\)-absolutely
continuous invariant measures, \(\mu=\nu\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[What the Gibbs Dirichlet form proves]\label{prop:acunique}
Let \((T_t)\) be a \(\nu\)-symmetric Markov semigroup on \(L^2(\nu)\), and let
\(\mathcal E\) be its closed Dirichlet form.  Assume that the only
zero-energy functions in the domain of \(\mathcal E\) are constants.  Then every
\(T_t\)-invariant probability measure \(\mu\) with \(\mu\ll\nu\) equals \(\nu\).

For the Gibbs measure above, the zero-energy condition holds for the gradient
form
\[
\mathcal E(F,G)=\frac12\int_E\ip{DF}{DG}_H\dd\nu
\]
provided the limiting semigroup is indeed the symmetric semigroup associated
with this form.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(h=\dd\mu/\dd\nu\).  Invariance gives, for bounded \(f\),
\[
\int_ET_tf\,h\dd\nu=\int_Ef\,h\dd\nu.
\]
By symmetry and the \(L^1\)-extension of \(T_t\), this says \(T_th=h\) in
\(L^1(\nu)\).  For \(m\ge1\), let \(h_m=h\wedge m\).  Since
\(r\mapsto r\wedge m\) is concave and \(T_t\) is Markov,
\[
T_th_m\le (T_th)\wedge m=h_m.
\]
Integrating both sides against the invariant measure \(\nu\) gives equality,
hence \(T_th_m=h_m\).  Now \(h_m\in L^2(\nu)\), and a fixed \(L^2\)-function has
zero energy:
\[
\mathcal E(h_m,h_m)
=
\lim_{t\downarrow0}\frac1t\ip{h_m-T_th_m}{h_m}_{L^2(\nu)}
=0.
\]
Thus \(h_m\) is constant.  By monotone convergence,
\(\int h_m\dd\nu\to\int h\dd\nu=1\), so \(h_m\to1\) and \(h=1\) \(\nu\)-a.e.

For the displayed Gibbs gradient form, the density bounds \eqref{eq:density-bounds}
make \(\nu\) equivalent to \(\gamma\) with bounded density above and below.
The Gaussian Poincare inequality for the Brownian-bridge measure,
\[
\operatorname{Var}_\gamma(F)
\le
\frac1{\pi^2}\int_E\|DF\|_H^2\dd\gamma
\]
on cylinder functions, extends by closure.  Hence zero \(\nu\)-energy implies
zero \(\gamma\)-energy and therefore constancy.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Irreducibility in \(L^2(\nu)\) does not rule out singular invariant laws]\label{ex:nullmod}
Let \(E=[0,1]\), let \(\nu\) be Lebesgue measure, and define a Markov kernel
\[
K(x,\cdot)=
\begin{cases}
\nu(\cdot),&x\ne0,\\
\delta_0(\cdot),&x=0.
\end{cases}
\]
Then \(\nu K=\nu\) because \(\nu(\{0\})=0\), and \(\delta_0K=\delta_0\).  On
\(L^2(\nu)\), however, \(Kf=\int f\dd\nu\) \(\nu\)-a.e.; hence the \(L^2(\nu)\)
operator is symmetric and has only constant fixed functions.  The extra
invariant measure \(\delta_0\) is invisible to the \(L^2(\nu)\) theory because
it is supported on a \(\nu\)-null point.
\end{example}

\subsection{What is missing from the stated assumptions}

The preceding results identify exactly what would close the uniqueness proof.
One needs a deterministic-start smoothing or overlap estimate, such as:
\[
P_{t_0}(x,\cdot)\not\perp P_{t_0}(y,\cdot)\quad\forall x,y,
\]
or the compact-local Hellinger estimate of Theorem \ref{thm:hellinger}, or
compact-local uniform \(\nu\)-absolute continuity as in Theorem
\ref{thm:uac}, or pointwise resolvent absolute continuity as in Proposition
\ref{prop:resolvent}.  These are not consequences of weak convergence alone.

\begin{example}[Weak convergence can destroy absolute continuity and overlap]
On \(\R\), each Gaussian law \(\mathcal N(0,1/n)\) is absolutely continuous with
respect to Lebesgue measure, but
\[
\mathcal N(0,1/n)\Rightarrow\delta_0,
\]
and \(\delta_0\) is singular with respect to Lebesgue measure.  Likewise,
\(\mathcal N(0,1/n)\) and \(\mathcal N(1,1/n)\) have positive Hellinger affinity
for each \(n\), but their affinities tend to \(0\), and the weak limits
\(\delta_0\) and \(\delta_1\) are singular.  Thus weak convergence of kernels
does not by itself preserve absolute continuity, uniform integrability, or a
positive overlap lower bound.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[Finite-dimensional domination as a possible missing theorem]
A convenient way to prove the compact-local Hellinger input would be a uniform
finite-dimensional heat-kernel domination theorem.  Let \(P_t^{n,m}\) be the
\(m\)-mode Galerkin approximation of the mollified equation and \(Q_t^m\) the
\(m\)-dimensional Dirichlet Ornstein--Uhlenbeck kernel.  A bound of the form
\[
P_t^{n,m}(a,\cdot)\ge c\,Q_t^m(a,\cdot),
\]
with \(c>0\) independent of \(n,m,a\), together with a passage from Galerkin
kernels to the infinite-dimensional kernels, would yield overlap.  For the
Galerkin potential
\[
\Psi_{n,m}(a)
=
\int_0^1F_n\!\left(\sum_{k=1}^ma_ke_k(x)\right)\dd x,
\]
one has the layer-cake representation, with
\(e(x)=(e_1(x),\ldots,e_m(x))\),
\[
\Psi_{n,m}(a)
=
\int_0^1\int_{\R}
\1_{\{\ip{e(x)}{a}>s\}}\,b_n(s)\dd s\,\dd x.
\]
This displays \(\Psi_{n,m}\) as an average of affine half-space membrane
potentials of total mass \(1\).  Proving the domination estimate is, however,
an additional finite-dimensional heat-kernel theorem; it is not part of
Assumption \ref{ass:ablm}.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Final conclusion}

The natural Gibbs measure \(\nu\) is rigorously proved invariant for the
limiting singular semigroup.  The desired uniqueness statement follows from
any of the deterministic-start smoothing or overlap estimates isolated above.
Under only the assumptions explicitly stated in the original problem---namely
well-posedness and weak convergence of the mollified equations---the argument
does not rule out invariant measures singular with respect to \(\nu\).  Thus the
candidate proof establishes invariance and conditional uniqueness, but an
additional smoothing or overlap theorem is needed for an unconditional proof of
\eqref{eq:target}.
