\section{Review}


\paragraph{Correctness.}

The AI got the problem correct.   This is a working proof, with all essential details included.   The proof in both halves is efficient, and the proof in the second half is especially clean.  I really enjoyed reading the second half.   


\paragraph{Novelty.}

Let me first discuss the context for the problem and then describe how the AI did.  The problem is based on my solution of the Halpern-Weaver Conjecture, a result which asks for the infimal
aspect ratio needed for a smooth and isometric embedding of a flat Moebius band into $\R^3$.  My paper was published in the {\it Annals\/} in January, 2025 and there are also some of accounts of the result in the popular media. The problem the AI considered is the same problem in a different category   of maps, piecewise linear maps with a certain side condition called squeezing. 

The first half of the problem amounts to showing that $\sqrt 3$ is a lower bound on the allowable aspect ratio. For this, the AI seems to have followed my paper very closely, adapting essentially every one of my arguments to the new category of mappings.  One thing that troubled me is that the AI did not cite my work at all, even though the arguments were extremely close. Indeed, the AI used the same terminology in my paper -- e.g. ''T-patterns'' and ''bends'' -- and also used the same letters, $B,T,D,H$ for the important objects in the proof. The chances of this happening without the AI actually reading and absorbing my paper seem to be a trillion to one against.  If I found this kind of thing written on a student's exam -- close adaptation without any acknowledgement -- I would probably give them an F for the exam.

The second half of the problem amounts to constructing an example for every aspect ratio greater than $\sqrt 3$.  For this, the AI did a beautiful and,
as far as I can tell, original job. The AI produced a $6$-triangle example which works for a wide range of parameter values in the one-sided neighborhood of $\sqrt 3$.   (For large aspect ratio the result is obvious.) This construction was both cool and suprising, just great.







\section{Recommendation}


I would recommend that the paper is publishable with minor revisions, though with a big {\it caveat\/}.  I will indicate the revisions with inline comments below.
Now let me explain the one {\it caveat\/}.  As indicated above, the close adaptation of my arguments without any citation was annoying.  I would consider this a kind of ethical failing in a human author.  For an AI, it is hard to tell. 
Possibly I would reject the paper on the grounds of ``unethical AI behavior'', whatever that might mean.

\begin{center}
{\bf The infimum is \(\sqrt3\)}
\end{center}

Let \(M_\beta=\Sigma/\langle G_\beta\rangle\).  This is a compact flat
Mobius band, with boundary a circle of length \(2\beta\).  If \(\beta\)
is realized, condition (2) says exactly that the given map is constant on
the \(G_\beta\)-orbits and separates distinct orbits.  Hence it descends
to a continuous injection \(F:M_\beta\to\R^3\), and this injection is an
embedding because \(M_\beta\) is compact and \(\R^3\) is Hausdorff.  The
locally finite invariant triangulation descends to a locally finite
triangulation of the compact surface \(M_\beta\), hence to a finite one.


\review{The term bend comes from my paper on the optimal paper Moebius band, which this paper should cite.}

In a clean triangle, call the boundary edge the ridge and the opposite
vertex the apex.  A bend is a segment in the triangle joining the apex to
a point of the ridge.



\begin{lemma}[bends lengthen]\label{lem:lengthen}
Let \(L\) be the affine map on one clean triangle.  If \(P\) is any point
of the ridge and \(C\) is the apex, then
\[
        |L(P)-L(C)|>|P-C|.
\]
Moreover every non-degenerate boundary subarc is strictly shortened by
\(F\).
\end{lemma}

\review{This proof can be done without calculus, but this here is still a decent proof.}



\begin{proof}
Let the ridge endpoints be \(A,B\), and put \(P_s=(1-s)A+sB\).  The
function
\[
 q(s)= |L(P_s)-L(C)|^2-|P_s-C|^2
\]
satisfies
\[
 q''(s)=2\big(|L(A)-L(B)|^2-|A-B|^2\big)<0,
\]
because the ridge is shortened.  Since the two side edges are lengthened,
\(q(0)>0\) and \(q(1)>0\).  Concavity gives \(q(s)>0\) for
\(0\le s\le1\).  On each boundary edge the affine map has constant
Lipschitz factor \(<1\); an arbitrary boundary subarc is split at the
finitely many boundary vertices it meets, and the strict inequalities add.
\end{proof}


\review{This is Lemma T in my paper on the optimal paper Moebius band.  That work should be cited, because the proof is extremely similar.}


\begin{lemma}[a polygonal \(T\)-pair]\label{lem:T}
For \(F(M_\beta)\) there are two bends whose image segments lie on
perpendicular intersecting lines.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The bends form a circle.  Indeed, every bend meets the center circle
\(\{y=1/2\}/\langle G_\beta\rangle\) once, and every point of the center
circle lies in a unique bend; on a common non-boundary edge the two
adjacent triangles give the same limiting bend.

Parametrize this bend circle by \(u\in\R/\Z\), lift to \(u\in\R\), and
choose the image direction \(e(u)\) from the lower side of the lifted
strip to the upper side.  Let \(m(u)\) be the image midpoint.  The
previous lemma makes every image bend non-degenerate, and the finite
polygonal structure gives continuous functions \(e,m\).  Going once
around the Mobius band reverses the chosen orientation, so
\[
        e(u+1)=-e(u),\qquad m(u+1)=m(u).
\]


\review{This proof seems too terse for me.  In particular, the functions are not motivated at all.  They work, but the reader might want some geometric intuition for what is going on.}

For \(0<r<1\) define
\[
 \Phi(u,r)=\Big(e(u)\!\cdot e(u+r),\,
 (m(u)-m(u+r))\!\cdot (e(u)\times e(u+r))\Big).
\]
It is periodic in \(u\).  As \(r\to0\) it tends to \((1,0)\), and as
\(r\to1\) it tends to \((-1,0)\).  Thus it extends continuously to the
two-point compactification of \(S^1\times(0,1)\), obtained by collapsing
the two boundary circles of \(S^1\times[0,1]\); this compactification is
\(S^2\).

The involution \(\iota(u,r)=(u+r,1-r)\) interchanges the two bends.  Under
the coordinates
\[
(\sin\pi r\cos 2\pi(u+r/2),\,\sin\pi r\sin 2\pi(u+r/2),\,\cos\pi r)
\]
on the compactified cylinder, \(\iota\) is the antipodal map.  From
\(e(u+1)=-e(u)\) and \(m(u+1)=m(u)\) one checks that
\(\Phi\circ\iota=-\Phi\).  Borsuk--Ulam gives a zero of \(\Phi\).

At such a zero the two direction vectors are perpendicular.  The second
coordinate says that the vector joining the two midpoints lies in the
plane spanned by those directions; hence the two supporting lines are
coplanar.  Since they are not parallel, they intersect.
\end{proof}


\review{This is the same as the main geometric argument in my paper.  The logic is very similar, and the labels $T,B,D,H$ are as in my paper. This should be cited.}


\begin{proposition}\label{prop:lower}
Every realized \(\beta\) satisfies \(\beta>\sqrt3\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Choose the two bends from Lemma \ref{lem:T}.  Their image segments have
disjoint relative interiors, because \(F\) is an embedding.  Let \(O\) be
the intersection of their supporting lines.  At least one image segment
does not contain \(O\) in its relative interior; call that bend \(B\), and
call the other one \(T\).

Lift \(T\) to a segment from \((0,0)\) to \((t,1)\).  Replacing \(x\) by
\(-x\), if necessary, we may assume \(t\ge0\).  In fact \(t<\beta\): if
\(t>\beta\), this lifted segment meets its \(G_\beta\)-translate in its
interior, and if \(t=\beta\), its endpoints are \(G_\beta\)-equivalent.
Either case contradicts the fact that the image of a bend is a
non-degenerate affine segment and that \(F\) is embedded on the quotient.

Cutting \(M_\beta\) along \(T\) gives two boundary arcs \(H,D\) of lengths
\[
        \ellg(H)=\beta-t,\qquad \ellg(D)=\beta+t.        \tag{1}
\]
Indeed the upper endpoint \((t,1)\) has boundary coordinate \(t+\beta\)
modulo \(2\beta\).  Let \(T^*=F(T)\).  Since \(H^*=F(H)\) joins the
endpoints of \(T^*\), while boundary arcs are shortened and bends are
lengthened,
\[
        \beta-t=\ellg(H)>\ellg(H^*)\ge |T^*|>\sqrt{1+t^2}.       \tag{2}
\]

The line of \(B^*\) is perpendicular to the line of \(T^*\).  Since \(O\)
is not in the relative interior of \(B^*\), one endpoint \(v^*\) of
\(B^*\) has distance from the line of \(T^*\) at least \(|B^*|\).  The
domain length of \(B\) is at least \(1\), so Lemma \ref{lem:lengthen}
gives \(|B^*|>1\).  Let \(A\) be the boundary arc, either \(H\) or \(D\),
which contains the corresponding boundary endpoint \(v\).

The image arc \(A^*\) goes from one endpoint of \(T^*\) to the other
through \(v^*\).  If a point has perpendicular distance \(h\) from a line
segment of length \(L\), the sum of its distances to the two endpoints is
at least \(\sqrt{L^2+4h^2}\), with equality only over the midpoint.  Here
\(L=|T^*|>\sqrt{1+t^2}\) and \(h>1\), so
\[
        \ellg(A)>\ellg(A^*)\ge
        \hbox{(that sum)}>\sqrt{5+t^2}.                  \tag{3}
\]


\review{This endgame seems a bit inefficient to me. It works, but is not maximally efficient.  The main point is to get $\beta>\max(\sqrt{1+t^2}+t,\sqrt{5+t^2}-t).$}

If \(A=H\), then \(D\ge H\) and (3) gives
\[
        2\beta=H+D>2\sqrt{5+t^2}>2\sqrt3.
\]
Thus assume \(A=D\).  From (1)--(3),
\[
 2\beta=H+D>\sqrt{1+t^2}+\sqrt{5+t^2},                         \tag{4}
\]
and also
\[
 2\beta=2D-2t>2\sqrt{5+t^2}-2t.                                \tag{5}
\]
Let
\[
 a(t)=\sqrt{1+t^2}+\sqrt{5+t^2},\qquad
 b(t)=2\sqrt{5+t^2}-2t .
\]
On \([0,\infty)\), \(a\) is increasing and \(b\) is decreasing, and
\[
        a(1/\sqrt3)=b(1/\sqrt3)=2\sqrt3 .
\]
Hence \(\max(a(t),b(t))\ge2\sqrt3\) for all \(t\ge0\).  Equations
(4)--(5) imply \(2\beta>2\sqrt3\).
\end{proof}

It remains to construct realizations for all larger \(\beta\).

\begin{lemma}[an embedded six-triangle model]\label{lem:model}
Let \(E_0E_1E_2\) be an equilateral triangle of side \(a\).  For every
sufficiently small \(\varepsilon>0\) there are points \(C_i(\varepsilon)\)
tending to the midpoint of \(E_iE_{i+1}\) (indices mod \(3\)) such that
the six affine triangles
\[
(E_i,C_i,E_{i+2}),\qquad (C_i,E_{i+1},E_{i+2}),\qquad i=0,1,2,
\]
form an embedded polyhedral Mobius band with boundary
\(E_0C_0E_1C_1E_2C_2E_0\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Embeddedness is preserved by nonsingular affine changes of coordinates,
so it is enough to verify one model.  Take
\[
\begin{array}{lll}
A_0=(0,0,0),& A_1=(1,0,0),& A_2=(0,1,0),\\
B_0=(1/2,0,\varepsilon),&
B_1=(1/2-\varepsilon,1/2,-2\varepsilon),&
B_2=(\varepsilon,1/2,0),
\end{array}
\]
where \(0<\varepsilon<1/4\), and take the six faces
\[
\Delta_0=A_0B_0A_2,\quad \Delta_1=B_0A_1A_2,\quad
\Delta_2=A_1B_1A_0,\quad \Delta_3=B_1A_2A_0,
\]
\[
\Delta_4=A_2B_2A_1,\quad \Delta_5=B_2A_0A_1 .
\]

The links of the vertices are intervals.  For instance
\[
 \operatorname{lk}(A_i)=
 B_i-A_{i+2}-B_{i+1}-A_{i+1}-B_{i+2},\qquad
 \operatorname{lk}(B_i)=A_i-A_{i+2}-A_{i+1}
\]




(indices mod \(3\), and reversal of a path is immaterial).  Thus the
abstract complex is a connected compact surface.  Its single boundary
cycle is \(A_0B_0A_1B_1A_2B_2A_0\), and

It remains to check embeddedness.  The face normals are
\[
\begin{array}{lll}
n_0=(-\varepsilon,0,1/2),&
n_1=(\varepsilon,\varepsilon,1/2),&
n_2=(0,2\varepsilon,1/2),\\

For the edge-sharing pairs
\((0,1),(0,3),(1,4),(2,3),(2,5)\) these normals are not proportional, so
the two planes meet in the line of the common edge and the two triangles
meet exactly in that edge.  The remaining edge-sharing pair is
\((4,5)\).  These two triangles are coplanar in \(z=0\), but their third
vertices lie on opposite sides of their common edge \(B_2A_1\), since
\[
 \det(A_1-B_2,A_2-B_2)=\frac{1-2\varepsilon}{2}>0,\qquad

Therefore \(\Delta_4\cap\Delta_5=B_2A_1\).

(0,4)&(-,0)&(-,+)\\

for \(0<\varepsilon<1/4\).  For example, for the pair \((0,2)\)

with cone coordinates

\begin{lemma}[realizations above \(\sqrt3\)]\label{lem:upper}
Every \(\beta>\sqrt3\) is realized.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Put \(\delta=\beta/3\).  Choose \(a\) with
\[
        \max\{2/\sqrt3,\sqrt{1+\delta^2}\}<a<2\delta ;
\]
such an \(a\) exists because \(\delta>1/\sqrt3\).

Triangulate \(M_\beta\) in the fundamental rectangle
\([0,\beta]\times[0,1]\), with \((0,y)\sim(\beta,1-y)\).  Put
\[
\begin{array}{c}
A_0=(0,0),\quad B_0=(\delta,0),\quad A_1=(2\delta,0),\quad B_1=(\beta,0),\\
B_1=(0,1),\quad A_2=(\delta,1),\quad B_2=(2\delta,1),\quad A_0=(\beta,1).
\end{array}
\]
Split the three rectangles of width \(\delta\) by the diagonals
\(A_0A_2\), \(A_1A_2\), and \(A_1A_0\), respectively.  Equivalently, the
six faces are
\[
(A_i,B_i,A_{i+2}),\qquad (B_i,A_{i+1},A_{i+2}),\qquad i=0,1,2
\]
(indices mod \(3\)).  This is a clean triangulation of \(M_\beta\), and
its lift to \(\Sigma\) is \(G_\beta\)-invariant.

Use Lemma \ref{lem:model} with side length \(a\), and choose
\(\varepsilon\) so small that the following strict inequalities, which
hold in the limit \(\varepsilon\to0\), still hold:
\[
 |E_iC_i|<\delta,\qquad |C_iE_{i+1}|<\delta,\qquad
 |C_iE_{i+2}|>1 .
\]
Also \(|E_iE_j|=a>\sqrt{1+\delta^2}\).  Map the six vertices
\(A_i,B_i\) to \(E_i,C_i\), respectively, and extend affinely over each
of the six triangles.  Lemma \ref{lem:model} gives an embedding
\(F:M_\beta\to\R^3\).

Each ridge in the domain has length \(\delta\), and its image has length
\(<\delta\).  Each non-boundary edge is of one of two types: either it has
domain length \(1\), and image length \(|C_iE_{i+2}|>1\), or it has domain
length \(\sqrt{1+\delta^2}\), and image length \(a>\sqrt{1+\delta^2}\).
Thus \(F\) is a squeeze map on every triangle.  Lifting \(F\) to
\(\Sigma\) gives a continuous map invariant under \(G_\beta\); since
\(F\) is an embedding of the quotient, the lifted map satisfies condition
(2).  Hence \(\beta\) is realized.
\end{proof}

Proposition \ref{prop:lower} shows that realized numbers are all
\(>\sqrt3\), while Lemma \ref{lem:upper} realizes every \(\beta>\sqrt3\).
Therefore the infimum of the set of realized numbers is \(\sqrt3\).
