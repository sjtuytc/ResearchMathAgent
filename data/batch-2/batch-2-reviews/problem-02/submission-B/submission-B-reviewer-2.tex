\section{Review}


\paragraph{Correctness.}
The solution provides a correct argument both for the lower bound $\beta \geq \sqrt{3}$ and for the realizability of every $\beta > \sqrt{3}$. It accomplishes the first goal by adapting Schwartz's argument, which shows that a smoothly isometrically embedded Moebius strip has aspect ratio greater than $\sqrt{3}$, to the PL setting in which the triangles in the triangulation satisfy the ``squeeze map'' hypothesis. This adaptation is carried out without any major logical flaws.

For the second goal, the solution starts with Sadowsky's family of $C^1$ embeddings of the flat Mobius strip, which have straight-line rulings and isometrically embedded boundary curves. It then approximates these embeddings by PL embeddings, which do not yet satisfy the squeeze property, and chooses scaling factors for the domain Mobius strip so that the resulting PL embedding does satisfy the squeeze property. The solution supports this part of the argument by justifying the choice of scaling constants and the size of the partition used in the PL approximation, as well as by verifying that the chosen scaling constants achieve the squeeze property on each triangle. However, the solution is unclear in its proof that the constructed PL embeddings are indeed embedded; see the inline comments for details. Nevertheless, I believe that the statement is still correct and can be salvaged by working directly with Sadowsky's construction.

\paragraph{Novelty.}
For both parts of the argument, the strategies implemented by the solution are essentially the same as the expected strategy for this problem, the strategy followed by the human solution, and the strategies pursued by some of the other AI models. Therefore, this approach should not be considered novel.

For the first part, the solution identifies that the proof strategy underlying Schwartz's theorem can be modified to prove the lower bound $\beta > \sqrt{3}$ in the setting of the problem statement. However, given access to Schwartz's result, adapting the argument to the PL category is a relatively routine task. In particular, Lemmas 6, 7, and 8 use proof strategies that are very similar to those of corresponding lemmas in Schwartz's paper. The solution however fails to cite Schwartz's work explicitly in this part of the argument.

For the second part, the solution uses PL approximations of a known construction in the smooth setting. This is also a standard strategy, provided the necessary background from the existing literature is available. 

\paragraph{Exposition.}

The solution gives lengthy proofs of some basic properties of the triangulation described in the problem statement; see the inline comments for Lemma 3 and 4. I believe that this part of the argument could be abbreviated. This issue does not affect the correctness of the argument.

\section{Recommendation}

  I recommend that the submission be accepted after minor revisions. The main revisions needed are to give proper attribution to Schwartz's theorem in the proofs of Lemmas 6, 7, and 8, and to clarify the confusion in Lemma 10. I consider these issues correctable.

\begin{theorem}[Headline theorem: original problem]\label{thm:headline}
\normalfont
Let \(\Z\) denote the integers and let \(\R\) denote the reals.  Let
\[
\Sigma=\R\times[0,1],\qquad \partial\Sigma=\R\times\{0,1\}.
\]
For \(\beta>0\), define \(G_\beta:\Sigma\to\Sigma\) by
\[
G_\beta(x,y)=(x+\beta,1-y).
\]
Let \(G_\beta^k\) denote the \(k\)-fold composition of \(G_\beta\) or
\(G_\beta^{-1}\), according as \(k>0\) or \(k<0\), and let
\(G_\beta^0\) be the identity.  A \emph{clean triangle} is the convex hull of
three non-collinear points in \(\partial\Sigma\).  A \emph{clean triangulation}
of \(\Sigma\) is a countable, locally finite collection of clean triangles
with pairwise disjoint interiors and union \(\Sigma\).  It is
\(G_\beta\)-\emph{invariant} if \(G_\beta\) preserves the triangulation.  A
\emph{squeeze map} of a clean triangle is an affine map into \(\R^3\) which
decreases the length of the edge contained in \(\partial\Sigma\) and increases
the lengths of the other two edges.  A number \(\beta>0\) is \emph{realized}
if there is a continuous map \(f:\Sigma\to\R^3\) such that
\begin{enumerate}
\item \(f\) restricts to a squeeze map on each triangle of a
\(G_\beta\)-invariant clean triangulation of \(\Sigma\);
\item \(f(p)=f(q)\) if and only if \(p=G_\beta^k(q)\) for some \(k\in\Z\).
\end{enumerate}
Then the infimum of the set of realized numbers is \(\sqrt3\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Established stronger claim]\label{thm:strong}
The realized numbers are exactly the interval \((\sqrt3,\infty)\).
\end{theorem}

\subsection*{Overview / Strategy}

The lower bound passes to the quotient
\(M_\beta=\Sigma/\langle G_\beta\rangle\), a flat M\"obius band with boundary
length \(2\beta\).  The map descends to an embedded piecewise-affine surface.
Inside each clean triangle the cevians from the apex to the boundary edge are
strictly lengthened.  These cevians form a leaf graph; in the quotient the leaf
graph is a circle, while upstairs it is a line, and the deck transformation
reverses the orientation of the image support line.  A topological lemma about
paths of oriented lines then forces two image support lines to be perpendicular
and intersecting.  Boundary shortening and a Euclidean estimate give
\(\beta>\sqrt3\).

For the upper bound we use one classical external input: the
Sadowsky--Wunderlich construction of embedded developable M\"obius bands, in a
quantitative form.  We approximate such a smooth ruled band by an invariant
piecewise-linear ruled band whose boundary edges are not expanded and whose
transverse edges are shortened by an arbitrarily small amount.  A final
anisotropic rescaling makes the boundary edges strictly shorter and all
transverse edges strictly longer, yielding a realization for every
\(\beta>\sqrt3\).

\subsection{The lower bound}

Throughout this section assume that \(\beta\) is realized by \(f\) on a
\(G_\beta\)-invariant clean triangulation \(\mathcal T\).  Write
\(G=G_\beta\).

\begin{lemma}[Face-to-face structure]\label{lem:facetoface}
The triangulation is face-to-face: distinct edges have disjoint relative
interiors, every transverse edge is incident to exactly two triangles, and
every boundary edge is incident to exactly one triangle.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
A clean triangle has exactly one boundary edge: among its three boundary
vertices, exactly two lie on one boundary component and the third lies on the
other.  The remaining two edges are transverse.

Two transverse edges cannot cross in the interior of \(\Sigma\), since near a
transverse crossing each of the two incident triangles occupies one open side
of its edge, and two such open half-disks always overlap in an open sector.  If
two transverse edges are collinear and overlap in more than one point, then
they lie on the same non-horizontal line; such a line meets \(y=0\) and
\(y=1\) once each, so the two edges are the same geometric segment.  Boundary
edges cannot overlap in a nontrivial interval, because at an interior point of
the overlap both adjacent triangles contain an inward half-neighborhood of the
boundary.  Finally, the endpoint of a transverse edge cannot lie in the
relative interior of a boundary edge: the triangle with the boundary edge
contains a small inward half-disk there, while the triangle with the transverse
edge contains a nonempty sector inside that half-disk.  Hence distinct
triangles meet only in common faces.

Let \(e\) be a transverse edge and \(z\) an interior point of \(e\).  A small
disk around \(z\) is divided by \(e\) into two half-disks.  At most one
triangle occupies each side, by disjointness of interiors, and at least one
triangle occupies each side, because the triangles cover \(\Sigma\).  Thus
\(e\) is incident to exactly two triangles.  The same half-disk argument at an
interior point of a boundary edge gives exactly one incident triangle.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[The finite quotient triangulation]\label{lem:quotient}
The quotient map \(f\) descends to an embedding
\(F:M_\beta\to\R^3\), where
\[
M_\beta=\Sigma/\langle G\rangle
      =[0,\beta]\times[0,1]/(0,y)\sim(\beta,1-y),
\]
and the quotient triangulation of \(M_\beta\) is finite and face-to-face.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since \(p=Gq\) implies \(f(p)=f(q)\), the map descends to a continuous
\(F\).  If \(F([p])=F([q])\), then \(f(p)=f(q)\), so by realization
\(p=G^kq\), hence \([p]=[q]\).  Thus \(F\) is injective; because \(M_\beta\)
is compact and \(\R^3\) is Hausdorff, \(F\) is an embedding.

The restriction of \(f\) to each triangle is injective.  Indeed, if an affine
restriction had rank \(<2\), then arbitrarily close distinct points
\(p,q\) in that triangle would satisfy \(f(p)=f(q)\).  Then \(p=G^kq\) for
some \(k\ne0\), impossible for sufficiently close \(p,q\), since
\(G^{2n}\) translates by \(2n\beta\) and \(G^{2n+1}\) changes the
\(x\)-coordinate by \((2n+1)\beta\), so every nontrivial \(G^k\) moves every
point by distance at least \(\beta\).

Therefore no closed triangle meets a nontrivial translate of itself.  If
\(z\in\tau\cap G^k\tau\), write \(z=G^kw\) with \(w\in\tau\).  Then
\(f(z)=f(w)\), and injectivity on \(\tau\) gives \(z=w\), contradicting
freeness of the \(G\)-action unless \(k=0\).  A compact fundamental rectangle,
for instance \([0,2\beta]\times[0,1]\), meets only finitely many closed
triangles by local finiteness and compactness, and every orbit of triangles has
a representative meeting it.  Hence the quotient triangulation is finite.
Lemma \ref{lem:facetoface} descends to the quotient.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Cevian lengthening]\label{lem:cevian}
Let \(\Delta=\operatorname{conv}\{a,r_0,r_1\}\) be a clean triangle whose
boundary edge is \([r_0,r_1]\), and let \(A:\Delta\to\R^3\) be a squeeze map.
For \(r(s)=(1-s)r_0+sr_1\), every cevian \([a,r(s)]\) is strictly lengthened:
\[
|A(a)-A(r(s))|>|a-r(s)|,\qquad 0\le s\le1.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Set \(q(s)=|A(a)-A(r(s))|^2-|a-r(s)|^2\).  The two transverse edges are
strictly lengthened, so \(q(0)>0\) and \(q(1)>0\).  Since \(A\) is affine,
\[
q''(s)=2\bigl(|A(r_1)-A(r_0)|^2-|r_1-r_0|^2\bigr)<0
\]
because the boundary edge is strictly shortened.  Thus \(q\) is strictly
concave and lies above its chord:
\(q(s)\ge(1-s)q(0)+sq(1)>0\).  Taking square roots gives the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Paths of oriented lines]\label{lem:lines}
Let \(L_s\), \(0\le s\le1\), be a continuous path of oriented lines in
\(\R^3\).  Suppose \(L_1\) is the same unoriented line as \(L_0\), but with the
opposite orientation.  Then two distinct parameters give unoriented lines that
are perpendicular and intersect.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Represent an oriented line by \((u,m)\), where \(u\in S^2\) is its unit
direction and \(m=p\times u\) for any point \(p\) on the line.  Reversing
orientation sends \((u,m)\) to \((-u,-m)\).  Choose continuous
\((u(s),m(s))\) and extend by \((u(s+1),m(s+1))=-(u(s),m(s))\).  For
\(s\in\R/\Z\) and \(0\le\delta\le1\), define
\[
a(s,\delta)=u(s)\cdot u(s+\delta),\qquad
b(s,\delta)=u(s)\cdot m(s+\delta)+u(s+\delta)\cdot m(s).
\]
Then \((a,b)(s,0)=(1,0)\), \((a,b)(s,1)=(-1,0)\), and
\((a,b)(s+\delta,1-\delta)=-(a,b)(s,\delta)\).

Assume \((a,b)\ne(0,0)\) everywhere.  Then
\(\Phi=(a,b)/\sqrt{a^2+b^2}:S^1\times[0,1]\to S^1\) is continuous and is
constant on \(\delta=0\).  On \(\delta=1/2\),
\[
\Phi(s+1/2,1/2)=-\Phi(s,1/2).
\]
If \(\psi:S^1\to S^1\) has \(\psi(s+1/2)=-\psi(s)\), then, after lifting to an
angle \(\theta\), the integer
\(\bigl(\theta(s+1/2)-\theta(s)-\pi\bigr)/(2\pi)\) is constant, so the degree
of \(\psi\) is odd.  But \(\Phi|_{\delta=1/2}\) is homotopic through
\(S^1\times[0,1/2]\) to the constant map \(\Phi|_{\delta=0}\), so its degree is
zero, a contradiction.  Hence \((a,b)=(0,0)\) somewhere, with
\(0<\delta<1\), so the two parameters are distinct.

At such a zero, \(u\cdot v=0\) for the two directions \(u,v\).  If the lines
are represented by \((u,p\times u)\) and \((v,q\times v)\), then
\[
b=u\cdot(q\times v)+v\cdot(p\times u)=(p-q)\cdot(u\times v).
\]
Thus, when \(u\perp v\), the condition \(b=0\) says the two nonparallel lines
are coplanar.  Nonparallel coplanar lines intersect.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Perpendicular intersecting leaves]\label{lem:perpleaves}
In \(M_\beta\) there are two distinct leaves whose image support lines under
\(F\) are perpendicular and intersect.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Inside each triangle take the cevians from the apex to the boundary edge and
call them leaves.  In the finite quotient triangulation, form a graph
\(\Lambda_M\) by taking one closed interval for the leaves of each triangle and
gluing endpoints corresponding to common transverse edges.  By
Lemma \ref{lem:facetoface}, every vertex has valence \(2\).

The graph is connected.  If \(C\) is a component and \(U_C\subset M_\beta\) is
the union of the corresponding leaves, then \(U_C\) is open: this is immediate
inside triangles, along boundary edges, and across transverse edges because the
two incident leaf intervals are glued.  At a boundary vertex, a small half-disk
meets finitely many incident triangle sectors; consecutive sectors share an
edge from the vertex, and all non-boundary shared edges are transverse, so all
incident triangles lie in the same component.  Thus the sets \(U_C\) are
disjoint open subsets covering the connected surface \(M_\beta\); hence there
is only one.  A finite connected graph with all vertices of valence \(2\) is a
circle, so \(\Lambda_M\cong S^1\).

The same openness argument upstairs gives a connected leaf graph
\(\Lambda_\Sigma\).  The action of \(G\) on leaves is free.  A lifted leaf has
endpoints \((x,0)\) and \((x+t,1)\).  If \(G^{2n}\) fixes it, then
\(2n\beta=0\).  If \(G^{2n+1}\) fixes it, then the two endpoint equations give
simultaneously \(t=(2n+1)\beta\) and \(t=-(2n+1)\beta\), impossible.  Hence
\(\Lambda_\Sigma\to\Lambda_M\) is a connected infinite covering of a circle,
so \(\Lambda_\Sigma\cong\R\).

Orient every lifted leaf from \(y=0\) to \(y=1\).  By
Lemma \ref{lem:cevian}, its image is a nondegenerate segment, hence determines
a continuous oriented support line along \(\Lambda_\Sigma\).  If \(\ell\) is a
leaf, then \(G\ell\), reoriented from bottom to top, has the same image segment
with the opposite orientation, because \(G\) swaps boundary components and
\(f\circ G=f\).  Applying Lemma \ref{lem:lines} to the compact interval in
\(\Lambda_\Sigma\) from \(\ell\) to \(G\ell\) gives two distinct quotient
leaves with perpendicular intersecting support lines.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[No \(\beta\le\sqrt3\) is realized]\label{prop:lower}
If \(\beta\) is realized, then \(\beta>\sqrt3\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(J\) be a lifted leaf with endpoints \((x,0)\) and \((x+t,1)\).  Its flat
length is \(\sqrt{1+t^2}\).  We first show
\[
|t|<\beta. \tag{1}
\]
If \(t=\beta\) or \(t=-\beta\), the endpoints are identified by \(G\) or
\(G^{-1}\), so their images coincide, contradicting Lemma \ref{lem:cevian}.  If
\(t>\beta\), translate so \(J\) has endpoints \((0,0)\), \((t,1)\).  Then
\(GJ\) has endpoints \((\beta,1)\), \((t+\beta,0)\), and the two segments meet
at height \((t+\beta)/(2t)\in(0,1)\).  Thus some distinct \(p,q\in J\) satisfy
\(p=Gq\), so \(f(p)=f(q)\), contradicting injectivity on the nondegenerate
image segment.  The case \(t<-\beta\) is identical using \(G^{-1}\).

Parametrize the boundary circle of \(M_\beta\) modulo \(2\beta\) by assigning
coordinate \(u\) to \((u,0)\) and coordinate \(v-\beta\) to \((v,1)\).  Hence
the boundary arcs between the endpoints of a leaf of span \(t\) have lengths
\[
\beta-t,\qquad \beta+t, \tag{2}
\]
both positive by (1).  Also every nonconstant boundary arc is strictly
shortened by \(F\): the quotient boundary is subdivided into finitely many
boundary edges, and on each such edge \(F\) has constant length factor 
\(\lambda_e<1\); summing over subsegments gives
\[
\ell_F(\gamma)<\ell_0(\gamma). \tag{3}
\]
\review{(Minor comment) The symbol $\gamma$ in Equation (3) is not defined before it is used. Its meaning is therefore somewhat unclear until one reads the later parts of this section.}

Choose the two leaves \(T,B\) from Lemma \ref{lem:perpleaves}, and let their
support lines meet perpendicularly at \(P\).  Since distinct leaves have
disjoint relative interiors and \(F\) is embedded, \(P\) cannot lie in the
relative interiors of both image segments.  Rename so that \(F(B)\) lies in a
closed ray from \(P\).  Let \(q\) be the endpoint of \(B\) farther from \(P\),
and put \(Q=F(q)\).  Then
\[
y:=\operatorname{dist}(Q,\operatorname{line}F(T))=|Q-P|\ge |F(B)|>|B|_0\ge1,
\]
so in fact
\[
y>1. \tag{4}
\]

Lift \(T\) with endpoints \((x,0)\), \((x+t,1)\).  Replacing this lift by its
\(G\)-translate changes \(t\) to \(-t\), so it interchanges the two lengths in
(2).  Choose the lift so that the boundary arc \(D\) containing \(q\) has
length \(\beta+t\); let the other arc be \(H\), of length \(\beta-t\).  Let
\(A,C\) be the endpoints of \(F(T)\), and set
\[
L=|A-C|=|F(T)|>\sqrt{1+t^2}. \tag{5}
\]
The arc \(H\) connects \(A\) to \(C\), so by (3),
\[
\beta-t=\ell_0(H)>\ell_F(H)\ge L>\sqrt{1+t^2},
\]
hence
\[
\beta>\sqrt{1+t^2}+t. \tag{6}
\]
The arc \(D\) connects \(A\) to \(C\) and passes through \(Q\).  Therefore
\(\ell_F(D)\ge |A-Q|+|Q-C|\).  Among points at distance \(y\) from the line
\(AC\), the sum of distances to \(A\) and \(C\) is minimized when the
projection is the midpoint of \(AC\), giving
\[
|A-Q|+|Q-C|\ge \sqrt{L^2+4y^2}.
\]
Using (4) and (5),
\[
\ell_F(D)>\sqrt{5+t^2}.
\]
By (3),
\[
\beta+t=\ell_0(D)>\ell_F(D)>\sqrt{5+t^2},
\]
so
\[
\beta>\sqrt{5+t^2}-t. \tag{7}
\]
Combining (6) and (7),
\[
\beta>
\max\{\sqrt{1+t^2}+t,\ \sqrt{5+t^2}-t\}.
\]
For every real \(t\), this maximum is at least \(\sqrt3\).  Indeed, if both
terms were \(<\sqrt3\), then squaring
\(\sqrt{1+t^2}+t<\sqrt3\) gives \(t<1/\sqrt3\), while squaring
\(\sqrt{5+t^2}-t<\sqrt3\) gives \(t>1/\sqrt3\), a contradiction.  Hence
\(\beta>\sqrt3\).
\end{proof}

\subsection{The upper bound}

We quote the classical Sadowsky--Wunderlich construction in the precise form
needed.

\begin{theorem}[Sadowsky--Wunderlich input]\label{thm:sadowsky}
For every \(\mu>\sqrt3\), there is a map
\(E_\mu:\R\times[0,1]\to\R^3\) such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \(E_\mu(X+\mu,1-Y)=E_\mu(X,Y)\);
\item the induced quotient M\"obius band is embedded;
\item the quotient surface is \(C^{1,1}\), piecewise \(C^\infty\), and
developable;
\item it is foliated by full-width straight rulings, and \(E_\mu\) maps each
ruling affinely and isometrically to a segment;
\item the boundary lifts are parametrized by arclength in the \(X\)-coordinate;
\item the construction remains embedded on a slightly wider strip.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
This is the three-cylinder construction of Sadowsky, with Wunderlich's
analysis of the developable M\"obius band; see \cite{Sad30,Wun62}.  In that
construction the band consists of three planar pieces and three cylindrical
pieces joined along generators with matching tangent planes, and the bending is
isometric.  The standard separation computation gives maximum half-width
\[
b_{\max}=\frac{\mu}{2\sqrt3}-\frac{3\pi d}{4}
\]
for auxiliary cylinder diameter \(d>0\).  Since \(\mu>\sqrt3\), choosing
\(0<d<2(\mu-\sqrt3)/(3\pi\sqrt3)\) gives \(b_{\max}>1/2\), hence a width-one
embedded strip, and the strict inequality gives the slightly wider embedded
extension.
\end{proof}

For \(L,c>0\), write
\[
H_{L,c}(X,Y)=(X+L,c-Y)\qquad\text{on }\R\times[0,c].
\]

\begin{lemma}[Invariant PL approximation]\label{lem:PL}
Let \(E:\R\times[0,c]\to\R^3\) be a scaled Sadowsky band invariant under
\(H_{L,c}\), with full-width rulings
\[
R_x=[(x,0),(h(x),c)].
\]
Fix \(x_0\), put \(\phi(x)=h(x)+L\), and set
\[
S=\max_{x\in[x_0,\phi(x_0)]}|h(x)-x|.
\]
For every \(D>S\) and every \(\varepsilon>0\), there are an
\(H_{L,c}\)-invariant clean triangulation \(\mathcal T_0\) and an
\(H_{L,c}\)-invariant PL map \(F_0\) such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \(F_0(P)=F_0(Q)\) iff \(P=H_{L,c}^k(Q)\) for some \(k\in\Z\);
\item every boundary edge is nonexpanded;
\item every transverse edge \(PQ\) satisfies
\[
|F_0(P)-F_0(Q)|\ge |P-Q|-\varepsilon;
\]
\item every transverse edge has horizontal span \(<D\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The noncrossing full-width rulings imply that \(h\) is a strictly increasing
homeomorphism.  The map \(H=H_{L,c}\) sends \(R_x\) to
\(R_{\phi(x)}\), so
\[
h(\phi(x))=x+L,\qquad \phi^2(x)=x+2L. \tag{8}
\]
Also
\[
|h(x)-x|<L. \tag{9}
\]
If \(h(x)-x=L\), the endpoints of \(R_x\) are identified by \(H\), contradicting
the isometric nondegenerate image of \(R_x\).  If \(h(x)-x>L\), then \(R_x\)
meets \(H(R_x)\) in the interior of the strip; hence two distinct points of
\(R_x\) have the same image under \(E\), again impossible.  The case
\(h(x)-x<-L\) follows using \(H^{-1}\).  Thus \(\phi(x)>x\).

Choose \(\rho>0\) with \(S+\rho<D\) and \(2\rho<\varepsilon\).  Choose a
partition
\[
x_0<x_1<\cdots<x_N=\phi(x_0)
\]
so fine that \(x_{i+1}-x_i<\rho\), \(h(x_{i+1})-h(x_i)<\rho\), all seams of the
piecewise smooth Sadowsky band occur among the rulings \(R_{x_i}\), and the
embeddedness condition proved below holds.  Extend by \(x_{i+N}=\phi(x_i)\).
Then \(x_{i+2N}=x_i+2L\).  Let
\[
B_i=(x_i,0),\qquad T_i=(h(x_i),c).
\]
The quadrilaterals \(B_iB_{i+1}T_{i+1}T_i\) tile the strip.  For
\(0\le i<N\), triangulate by the diagonal \(B_iT_{i+1}\), and extend
\(H\)-invariantly.  Define \(F_0\) on vertices by \(F_0(B_i)=E(B_i)\),
\(F_0(T_i)=E(T_i)\), and extend affinely on triangles.  Then \(F_0\) is
\(H\)-invariant.

Boundary edges are nonexpanded because their images are chords of boundary
arcs parametrized by arclength.  A ruling edge is mapped isometrically.  For a
diagonal \(B_iT_{i+1}\), set
\(d=|B_i-T_{i+1}|\) and \(r=|B_i-T_i|\).  Since
\(|T_i-T_{i+1}|<\rho\), one has \(|d-r|<\rho\), and
\[
|F_0(B_i)-F_0(T_{i+1})|
\ge |E(B_i)-E(T_i)|-|E(T_i)-E(T_{i+1})|
>r-\rho>d-2\rho>d-\varepsilon.
\]
The other diagonal type is identical, using \(x_{i+1}-x_i<\rho\).  By (8),
the absolute span \(|h(x)-x|\) is preserved under \(\phi\), so all ruling spans
are at most \(S\); diagonal spans are \(<S+\rho<D\).

It remains to justify the promised embeddedness for sufficiently fine
partition.  Let
\(N=(\R\times[0,c])/\langle H\rangle\).  By Theorem \ref{thm:sadowsky}, \(E\)
extends to a slightly wider compact embedded \(C^{1,1}\) surface
\(\widehat N\), so there is a tubular neighborhood \(U\) with nearest-point
projection \(\pi:U\to \widehat E(\widehat N)\).  The secant PL maps converge
uniformly to \(E\); because \(E\) is affine on rulings, \(C^{1,1}\) on each
piece, and the seams are included among the ruling edges with matching tangent
planes, the maps
\[
\Psi=\widehat E^{-1}\circ\pi\circ F_0:N\to\widehat N
\]
converge to the inclusion \(N\hookrightarrow\widehat N\) in \(C^1\) on each
piece.

\review{(Major comment) First, the map $\hat E$ is not defined before its first appearance in the phrase ``nearest-point projection $\pi: U \rightarrow \hat E(\widehat N)$.'' I suspect that the solution intends $\hat E$ to denote the embedding of the wider Moebius strip guaranteed by statement 6 of Theorem 9, but this should be made explicit.
Second, the solution claims that the map $\Phi$ converges to the inclusion $N \hookrightarrow \widehat N$ in $C^1$. However, I suspect that $\Phi$ itself is only PL, rather than $C^1$, so it is unclear what kind of convergence is intended here. This point should be clarified.}


For sufficiently small \(C^1\)-error, \(\Psi\) is injective.  Choose finitely
many coordinate disks with convex coordinate images and \(\delta>0\) so that
points of \(N\) at distance \(<\delta\) lie in a common disk.  In such a disk,
if the derivative of \(\Psi\) differs from the identity by \(<1/2\), then
integration along the coordinate segment gives
\(|\Psi(p)-\Psi(q)|\ge \frac12|p-q|\).  For pairs with distance \(\ge\delta\),
compactness and injectivity of the inclusion give a positive minimum
separation; sufficiently small \(C^0\)-error preserves this separation.  Hence
\(\Psi\) is injective.  If the quotient map induced by \(F_0\) identified two
points, then \(\Psi\) would identify them, contradiction.  Thus the quotient
PL map is embedded, which is equivalent to assertion (1) after lifting.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Every \(\beta>\sqrt3\) is realized]\label{prop:upper}
For every \(\beta>\sqrt3\), there is a realization of \(\beta\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Choose \(\mu\) with \(\sqrt3<\mu<\beta\), and take the Sadowsky band
\(E_\mu\).  Let \(S_\mu=\max |h(x)-x|\) over one fundamental interval of
rulings, and choose \(D_\mu>S_\mu\).  Choose \(a<1\), close to \(1\), with
\(a>\mu/\beta\), and set
\[
c=\frac{a\beta}{\mu},\qquad L=a\beta.
\]
Then \(c>1\) and \(L=c\mu\).  Scale \(E_\mu\) by \(c\), giving a width-\(c\),
length-\(L\) band invariant under \(H_{L,c}\).  Its span bound scales to
\(cS_\mu\).

Choose \(a\) close enough to \(1\) so that
\[
m:=\min_{0\le u\le D_\mu}
\left(c\sqrt{1+u^2}-\sqrt{\left(\frac{cu}{a}\right)^2+1}\right)>0. \tag{10}
\]
This is possible because at \(a=1\) one has \(c=\beta/\mu>1\), and
\(c\sqrt{1+u^2}>\sqrt{c^2u^2+1}\) on the compact interval
\([0,D_\mu]\).  Choose \(0<\varepsilon<m\) and apply Lemma \ref{lem:PL} to the
scaled band with \(D=cD_\mu\).  We get \(\mathcal T_0\) and \(F_0\).

Define
\[
A:\Sigma\to\R\times[0,c],\qquad A(x,y)=(ax,cy).
\]
Because \(L=a\beta\),
\[
A(G_\beta(x,y))=(ax+a\beta,c-cy)=H_{L,c}(A(x,y)).
\]
Thus \(A\) conjugates \(G_\beta\) to \(H_{L,c}\).  Put
\[
f=F_0\circ A,\qquad \mathcal T=A^{-1}(\mathcal T_0).
\]
Then \(\mathcal T\) is a countable locally finite \(G_\beta\)-invariant clean
triangulation, and \(f\) is affine on every triangle.

If \(pq\) is a boundary edge, then
\[
|f(p)-f(q)|\le |A p-A q|=a|p-q|<|p-q|.
\]
If \(pq\) is a transverse edge, let \(s\) be the horizontal span of
\(A p\,A q\).  By Lemma \ref{lem:PL}, \(s<cD_\mu\); write \(s=cu\) with
\(0\le u<D_\mu\).  Then
\[
|Ap-Aq|=c\sqrt{1+u^2},\qquad
|p-q|=\sqrt{\left(\frac{cu}{a}\right)^2+1}.
\]
Using Lemma \ref{lem:PL} and \(\varepsilon<m\), (10) gives
\[
|f(p)-f(q)|
\ge |Ap-Aq|-\varepsilon
> |p-q|.
\]
Thus \(f\) is a squeeze map on every triangle.  Finally, for all
\(p,q\in\Sigma\),
\[
f(p)=f(q)
\Longleftrightarrow
F_0(Ap)=F_0(Aq)
\Longleftrightarrow
Ap=H_{L,c}^k(Aq)\text{ for some }k\in\Z
\Longleftrightarrow
p=G_\beta^k(q)\text{ for some }k\in\Z.
\]
Therefore \(\beta\) is realized.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:strong}]
Proposition \ref{prop:lower} shows that every realized number is \(>\sqrt3\).
Proposition \ref{prop:upper} shows that every number \(>\sqrt3\) is realized.
Hence the realized numbers are exactly \((\sqrt3,\infty)\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:headline}]
By Theorem \ref{thm:strong}, the set of realized numbers is
\((\sqrt3,\infty)\), whose infimum is \(\sqrt3\).
\end{proof}
