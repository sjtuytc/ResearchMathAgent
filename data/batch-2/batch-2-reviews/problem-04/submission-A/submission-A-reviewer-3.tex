{\bf Partial proof: the first alternative and the remaining hard estimate}
\end{center}

\subsection*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I write the small universal constant in the problem as \(\kappa\), reserving
\(k\) for \(k\)-dilation.  Rectangles are oriented Euclidean products and
degree \(1\) means relative degree \(1\) on
\(H_4(R,\partial R;\mathbb Z)\to H_4(S,\partial S;\mathbb Z)\).

The current proof is complete through the first alternative and through a
conditional reduction of the hard case.  It is not a complete proof of the
stated dichotomy, because the averaged tightening estimate \((\mathrm{EAT})\)
below remains unproved.  The arguments are written for smooth transverse maps;
piecewise smooth maps may be treated by the same current-slicing argument, or
by smoothing on strata with an arbitrarily small \(C^1\)-loss and then
absorbing that loss into the universal constants.

\subsection*{Standard inputs}

All constants are positive and universal.  If \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), then
\(\Dil_3(f),\Dil_4(f)\le1\): for singular values
\(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\), the inequality
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\) implies \(\sigma_2\le1\) and
\(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le\sigma_1\sigma_2\) for \(j\ge2\).

We use Guth's map-of-pairs estimate \cite[Estimate 1]{GuthExpanding}.  For
\(n=4,k=2,j=1,l=4\) it gives
\begin{equation}\label{guth14}
        R_1^2\,\Vol(R)\ge c_0\,S_1^3S_2S_3S_4 .
\end{equation}
We also use the small-volume relative isoperimetric estimate in rectangles
\cite[Theorem 3]{GuthExpanding}: if \(Z\) is a relative \(2\)-cycle in \(R\)
with \(A=\Mass Z\le c_*R_1R_2\), then
\[
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C_*\max\{A^{3/2},A^2/R_1\}.
\]
Consequently
\begin{equation}\label{profile-inverse}
\Fill_R(Z)\ge L\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\Mass Z\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,L^{2/3},(R_1L)^{1/2}\},
\end{equation}
and, after decreasing \(c_*\),
\begin{equation}\label{iso}
        \Mass Z<c_*R_1R_2\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C R_1R_2^2 .
\end{equation}

\subsection*{The first alternative}

Let \(Q\) be the concentric subrectangle of the \((x_3,x_4)\)-face of \(S\)
with side lengths \(S_3/2,S_4/2\), and set
\[
        P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\},\qquad y\in Q.
\]

\begin{lemma}[central target plane]\label{central}
Every relative \(3\)-chain in \(S\) with boundary \(P_y\), \(y\in Q\), has
mass at least \(cS_1S_2S_3\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a \(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the
\((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle which vanishes on its boundary and satisfies
\(\psi\ge cS_3\) on \(Q\).  Put \(\beta=\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2\).  Then
\(d\beta\) has comass at most \(1\), and Stokes' theorem gives
\[
\Mass Y\ge\left|\int_Yd\beta\right|
 =\left|\int_{P_y}\beta\right|\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
The relative boundary terms vanish because \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\) on the
\(x_1,x_2\)-faces and \(\psi=0\) on the \(x_3,x_4\)-faces.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{firstalt}
There is \(c_2>0\) such that, under the hypotheses of the problem, if
\[
        R_1R_2^2\le c_2S_1S_2S_3 ,
\]
then \(R_3R_4\ge c_2S_3S_4\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(T=[[R]]\) be the fundamental relative current and put \(F=(f_3,f_4)\).
Since \(f\) has relative degree \(1\), \(f_\#T=[[S]]\) as a relative
\(4\)-current.  For a.e. \(y\in Q\), slicing naturality gives
\[
        f_\#\langle T,F,y\rangle
        =\langle [[S]],(x_3,x_4),y\rangle=\pm P_y .
\]
Write \(Z_y=\langle T,F,y\rangle\).  Any relative filling of \(Z_y\) in \(R\)
pushes forward, using \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), to a filling of \(P_y\) in \(S\).
Lemma \ref{central} therefore gives
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
If \(c_2\) is sufficiently small, \eqref{iso} forces
\(\Mass Z_y\ge c_*R_1R_2\).  Since \(J_2F\le\Dil_2(f)\le1\), the coarea
formula yields
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge\int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
        \ge cR_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Cancelling \(R_1R_2\) proves the proposition.
\end{proof}

\subsection*{The unconditional partial hard-case bound}

Assume \(R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1\) and that the first conclusion fails with a
small constant.  Proposition \ref{firstalt} gives
\begin{equation}\label{r12large}
        R_1R_2^2\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\end{equation}
For the slices above, \eqref{profile-inverse} with
\(L=cS_1S_2S_3\) gives
\[
\Mass Z_y\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,(S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},
                         (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\}.
\]
Using \eqref{r12large} and \(R_1=\alpha S_1\), every term in the minimum is
at least \(c\alpha^{1/2}S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\).  Hence
\begin{equation}\label{slicevol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\alpha^{1/2}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .
\end{equation}
On the other hand, \eqref{guth14} gives
\begin{equation}\label{monovol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .
\end{equation}

\subsection*{A sufficient averaged hard-case estimate}

Let \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), keep \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), and define
\[
E_y=\int_{Z_y}\|dG|_{TZ_y}\|_{HS}^2\,d\|Z_y\|,\qquad
I=\int_QE_y\,dy,\qquad q=|Q|.
\]
At a regular point of \(F\), let \(K=\ker dF\), and let \(a\ge b\) be the
singular values of \(dF|_{K^\perp}\); then \(ab=J_2F\).  For any unit
\(w\in K\), the mixed two-vector \(df(w)\wedge df(v)\), where \(v\) is a
unit vector with \(|dF(v)|=a\), has a component of norm
\(|dG(w)|a\).  Since \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), \(|dG(w)|a\le1\), and hence
\(|dG(w)|^2J_2F\le b/a\le1\).  Summing over an orthonormal basis of \(K\)
gives
\[
        \|dG|_{\ker dF}\|_{HS}^2J_2F\le2 .
\]
By coarea,
\begin{equation}\label{Icoarea}
        I\le C\Vol(R).
\end{equation}
Let \(\Omega=\{y\in Q:E_y\le4I/q\}\), so \(|\Omega|\ge3q/4\).

For real normal currents define
\[
\EFill_f(y)=\inf\{\Mass C:\ C\in N_3(R,\partial R;\mathbb R),\
        f_\#(\partial C)=\pm P_y\}.
\]
This class is nonempty by filling the actual slice \(Z_y\).  If \(C\) is
admissible, then \(f_\#C\) fills \(P_y\) and \(\Mass(f_\#C)\le\Mass C\);
Lemma \ref{central} gives
\begin{equation}\label{EFilllower}
        \EFill_f(y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\end{equation}
Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{AElower}
A_\Omega:=\int_\Omega\EFill_f(y)\,dy
        \ge cS_1S_2S_3\,q
        \ge cS_1S_2S_3^2S_4 .
\end{equation}

For \(z\in Q\), let \({\mathcal B}_z\) be the set of \(x\in R\) such that
\(z\) belongs to the \(F\)-image of at least one coordinate \(2\)-plaque
through \(x\).  For fixed \(x\), the set of such \(z\)'s has measure at most
\(CR_3R_4\), because there are six plaques, each has area at most \(R_3R_4\),
and \(J_2F\le1\) on each plaque.  Hence, for every normal \(3\)-current \(B\),
\begin{equation}\label{badfubini}
 \frac1q\int_Q\Mass(B\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dz
 \le C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\Mass B .
\end{equation}

The following estimate is sufficient for the desired theorem.  Take measurable
near-minimizers \(C_y^0\) with
\(\Mass C_y^0\le2\EFill_f(y)\); standard measurable selection in the
separable flat topology, or equivalently outer integration with
\(\varepsilon\)-minimizers, is enough for this formal step.  One needs
\begin{equation}\tag{EAT}\label{EAT}
 A_\Omega
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right)
 +\frac{C}{q}\int_Q\int_\Omega
       \Mass(C_y^0\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dy\,dz .
\end{equation}

Assuming \eqref{EAT}, \eqref{badfubini} and
\(\Mass C_y^0\le2\EFill_f(y)\) give
\[
A_\Omega\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right)
  +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}A_\Omega .
\]
If \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), the last term is absorbed by taking
\(\kappa\) small.  If also \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and
\(I\le\delta S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), then, since \(q\sim S_3S_4\),
\[
R_1I\le C\kappa\delta S_1S_2S_3^2S_4,\qquad
\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\le C\delta^2S_1S_2S_3^2S_4 .
\]
For small \(\delta,\kappa\) this contradicts \eqref{AElower}.  Thus
\(I\ge cS_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), and \eqref{Icoarea} gives the desired
second alternative.

\subsection*{Why the remaining estimate is non-routine}

The estimate \eqref{EAT} cannot be obtained by a standard source-local
cellular deformation in the quotient by \(f\)-null chains without a new idea.
The obstruction is already visible in a two-cell model.  Let \(A,A'\) be two
oriented \(2\)-cells with \(f_\#A=f_\#A'=P\).  Then \(N=A-A'\in\ker f_\#\).
If an exact-plaque bad set contains \(A\) but not \(A'\), the source-local
projection \(P_{\rm bad}\) with \(P_{\rm bad}A=A\), \(P_{\rm bad}A'=0\) gives
\(f_\#P_{\rm bad}N=P\ne0\).  Dually, a quotient cochain has equal values on
\(A\) and \(A'\), but restricting it to the nonsaturated bad set destroys that
property.  Thus exact source localization and quotient invariance are in
tension.

This does not disprove \eqref{EAT}; it identifies what is missing.  A proof
would need an energy-sensitive, null-respecting carrier or a dual target-side
cochain homotopy whose residual term is nevertheless controlled by the
unsaturated exact-plaque mass in \eqref{badfubini}.

\subsection*{Remaining open issues}

The document is therefore a conditional reduction, not a complete solution.

\begin{enumerate}
\item The essential mathematical gap is \eqref{EAT}, an averaged tightening
estimate for the degree-carrying part of the slices \(Z_y\).  It is used only
in the last section, but it is indispensable for the high-aspect hard case.

\item The first alternative, the single-slice bound, the weighted coarea
estimate, the essential filling lower bound, the exact-plaque Fubini estimate,
and the absorption algebra are proved above.

\item Standard approaches tried so far do not close the gap: monomial
degree estimates give only \eqref{guth14}; ordinary source deformations create
uncontrolled \(f\)-null boundary errors; quotient cochains are not compatible
with nonsaturated exact-plaque localization as shown above.

\item A complete proof would require either a proof of \eqref{EAT}, an
equivalent dual formulation using only target-pullback cochains, or a different
hard-regime argument exploiting the full two-parameter family of slices and the
ambient mixed \(2\)-Jacobian bounds.
\end{enumerate}
