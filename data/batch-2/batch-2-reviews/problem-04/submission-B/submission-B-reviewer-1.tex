\section{Review}
{  Instead of the theorem in the problem, this submission wrote down a different theorem and then proved it.  This different theorem follows from standard facts in a fairly easy way.  It does not require the main mathematical ideas in the solution of the actual problem.

The submission does correctly explain some standard facts that are relevant to the problem.  But it does not make real progress on the question asked.

}


\paragraph{Correctness.} The submission states a different theorem than the one in the problem.  The proof of the different theorem is correct.  There is one place where there is either a small error or some bad writing, but it can be fixed easily.

There are also some issues with the organization of the writing.  The statement of the theorem is very long, and some of the statement should really be moved to the proof.  Some parts of the argument are never used and could be omitted.  On the other hand, on a line-by-line basis, the writing is clear.

\paragraph{Novelty.} 
I don't see anything novel in the proof.  The ingredients are standard.
\section{Recommendation}

\subsection*{Theorem (fiber-filling and coarea reduction)}

Let
\[
R=\prod_{i=1}^4[0,R_i],\qquad S=\prod_{i=1}^4[0,S_i],
\]
with \(0<R_1\le R_2\le R_3\le R_4\) and \(0<S_1\le S_2\le S_3\le S_4\). Orient \(S\) by
\[
dx_1\wedge dx_2\wedge dx_3\wedge dx_4.
\]
Write
\[
Q=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2],\qquad B=[0,S_3]\times[0,S_4],
\]
so \(S=Q\times B\), and orient \(Q\) and \(B\) by \(dx_1\wedge dx_2\) and \(dx_3\wedge dx_4\), respectively. Let
\[
\pi:S\to B,\qquad \pi(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)=(x_3,x_4),
\]
and suppose
\[
f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)
\]
is a Lipschitz finite piecewise smooth map of degree \(1\) with \(\operatorname{Dil}_2(f)\le 1\). Put
\[
F=\pi\circ f=(f_3,f_4),\qquad G=(f_1,f_2).
\]

For \(y\in B\), set
\[
P_y=Q\times\{y\}.
\]
Use the slicing convention normalized by
\[
\langle \llbracket Q\times B\rrbracket,\pi,y\rangle
=
\llbracket Q\times\{y\}\rrbracket .
\]

Then for a.e. \(y\in \operatorname{int}B\), the slice
\[
z_y=\langle \llbracket R\rrbracket,F,y\rangle
\]
is a relative integral \(2\)-cycle in \((R,\partial R)\), and
\[
f_\# z_y=\llbracket P_y\rrbracket .
\]
Consequently,
\[
\operatorname{FillVol}_R(z_y)\ge \operatorname{FillVol}_S(P_y)
=S_1S_2\,\operatorname{dist}(y,\partial B).
\]

Moreover,
\[
\int_B \operatorname{Area}(z_y)\,dy\le \operatorname{Vol}(R).
\]

Finally, let
\[
B_0=\left[\frac{S_3}{3},\frac{2S_3}{3}\right]\times
\left[\frac{S_4}{3},\frac{2S_4}{3}\right].
\]
At points where \(dF\) has rank \(2\), let
\[
\sigma_1(x)\ge \sigma_2(x)>0
\]
be the two nonzero singular values of \(dF_x\). Define the measure
\[
d\mu=\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1}\,dx
\]
on the rank-two set of \(dF\), and \(d\mu=0\) where \(\operatorname{rank}dF<2\). Then
\[
\mu(F^{-1}(B_0))\ge \frac19 S_1S_2S_3S_4
\]
and
\[
\int_{B_0}\operatorname{Area}(z_y)\,dy
=
\int_{F^{-1}(B_0)}\sigma_1^2\,d\mu.
\]

Thus, if in addition one has an estimate
\[
\int_{F^{-1}(B_0)}\sigma_1^2\,d\mu
\ge
c_d\sqrt{\frac{S_3}{S_2}}\,
\mu(F^{-1}(B_0))
\]

[Reviewer comment: This last hypothesis is not like anything in the original problem.  Adding this hypothesis makes the conclusion more-or-less immediate.]

for some constant \(c_d>0\), then
\[
R_1R_2R_3R_4
\ge
\frac{c_d}{9}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]

\subsection*{Proof}

Write \(\mathbf M(T)\) for the mass of an integral current \(T\), and write \(\llbracket X\rrbracket\) for the integral current associated to an oriented Lipschitz manifold or rectangle \(X\).

For a compact rectangle \(X\), if \(Z\in \mathbf I_m(X)\) satisfies \(\operatorname{spt}\partial Z\subset \partial X\), define its relative filling volume by
\[
\operatorname{FillVol}_X(Z)
=
\inf\left\{
\mathbf M(Y):
Y\in \mathbf I_{m+1}(X),\
\operatorname{spt}(\partial Y-Z)\subset \partial X
\right\}.
\]

We use the standard slicing theorem for integral currents: if \(T\in \mathbf I_m\) and \(\Phi\) is Lipschitz into \(\mathbb R^k\), then for a.e. \(a\), the slice \(\langle T,\Phi,a\rangle\in \mathbf I_{m-k}\) exists and
\[
\partial\langle T,\Phi,a\rangle
=
\pm \langle \partial T,\Phi,a\rangle.
\]
We also use slicing naturality: for Lipschitz \(H\),
\[
H_\#\langle T,\Phi\circ H,a\rangle
=
\langle H_\#T,\Phi,a\rangle
\]
for a.e. \(a\). Finally, we use the rectifiable-current area inequality
\[
\mathbf M(H_\#T)
\le
\int J_k(dH|_{\operatorname{Tan}T})\,d\|T\|
\]
for \(T\in\mathbf I_k\).

First prove the fundamental current identity
\[
f_\#\llbracket R\rrbracket=\llbracket S\rrbracket .
\]
Let
\[
T=f_\#\llbracket R\rrbracket.
\]
Since \(f\) is Lipschitz, \(T\in\mathbf I_4(S)\), and
\[
\partial T=f_\#\partial\llbracket R\rrbracket
\]
is supported in \(f(\partial R)\subset \partial S\). Hence, in \(\operatorname{int}S\),
\[
\partial(T\llcorner \operatorname{int}S)=0.
\]
By the constancy theorem on the connected open set \(\operatorname{int}S\),
\[
T\llcorner \operatorname{int}S
=
m\,\llbracket \operatorname{int}S\rrbracket
\]
for some \(m\in\mathbb Z\). Therefore \(T-m\llbracket S\rrbracket\) is supported in \(\partial S\). Since \(\partial S\) has \(\mathcal H^4\)-measure zero, no nonzero integral \(4\)-current can be supported there, so
\[
T=m\llbracket S\rrbracket.
\]
Because \(f\) has degree \(1\), \(T\) represents the relative fundamental class \([S,\partial S]\), hence \(m=1\). Thus
\[
f_\#\llbracket R\rrbracket=\llbracket S\rrbracket .
\]

Now apply slicing to \(F=\pi\circ f\). For a.e. \(y\in\operatorname{int}B\),
\[
z_y=\langle \llbracket R\rrbracket,F,y\rangle
\]
is an integral \(2\)-current, and
\[
\partial z_y
=
\pm \langle \partial\llbracket R\rrbracket,F,y\rangle,
\]
so \(\partial z_y\) is supported in \(\partial R\). Hence \(z_y\) is a relative integral \(2\)-cycle in \((R,\partial R)\).

By slicing naturality,
\[
f_\# z_y
=
f_\#\langle \llbracket R\rrbracket,F,y\rangle
=
\langle f_\#\llbracket R\rrbracket,\pi,y\rangle
=
\langle \llbracket S\rrbracket,\pi,y\rangle .
\]
With the stated product orientation and slice convention,
\[
\langle \llbracket S\rrbracket,\pi,y\rangle
=
\llbracket Q\times\{y\}\rrbracket
=
\llbracket P_y\rrbracket.
\]
Thus
\[
f_\#z_y=\llbracket P_y\rrbracket.
\]

Next, \(\operatorname{Dil}_2(f)\le 1\) implies \(\operatorname{Dil}_3(f)\le 1\). Indeed, at a differentiability point of \(f\), let
\[
a_1\ge a_2\ge a_3\ge a_4\ge 0
\]
be the singular values of \(df\). The hypothesis gives \(a_1a_2\le 1\). Hence \(a_2\le 1\), so \(a_3\le 1\), and therefore
\[
a_1a_2a_3\le a_1a_2\le 1.
\]
Thus \(\|\Lambda^3df\|\le 1\) a.e.

Let \(Y\in \mathbf I_3(R)\) be a relative filling of \(z_y\), so
\[
\operatorname{spt}(\partial Y-z_y)\subset \partial R.
\]
Then
\[
\partial(f_\#Y)-f_\#z_y
=
f_\#(\partial Y-z_y)
\]
is supported in \(f(\partial R)\subset \partial S\). Since \(f_\#z_y=\llbracket P_y\rrbracket\), the current \(f_\#Y\) is a relative filling of \(P_y\) in \(S\). Also,
\[
\mathbf M(f_\#Y)\le \int \|\Lambda^3df\|\,d\|Y\|\le \mathbf M(Y).
\]
Taking the infimum over all such \(Y\) gives
\[
\operatorname{FillVol}_R(z_y)\ge \operatorname{FillVol}_S(P_y).
\]

It remains to compute \(\operatorname{FillVol}_S(P_y)\). Let
\[
\delta(y)=\operatorname{dist}(y,\partial B).
\]
For the upper bound, choose a shortest segment \(\gamma\subset B\) from \(\partial B\) to \(y\), oriented toward \(y\). Then
\[
C=\llbracket Q\rrbracket\times \llbracket \gamma\rrbracket
\]
is a relative filling of \(P_y\) in \(S\), and
\[
\mathbf M(C)=S_1S_2\,\delta(y).
\]
Hence
\[
\operatorname{FillVol}_S(P_y)\le S_1S_2\,\delta(y).
\]

For the lower bound, define
\[
\delta(q)=\operatorname{dist}(q,\partial B)
=
\min\{q_3,S_3-q_3,q_4,S_4-q_4\}.
\]
For \(\eta>0\), set
\[
m_\eta(q)
=
-\eta\log\left(
e^{-q_3/\eta}
+
e^{-(S_3-q_3)/\eta}
+
e^{-q_4/\eta}
+
e^{-(S_4-q_4)/\eta}
\right).
\]
Then
\[
\delta(q)-\eta\log 4\le m_\eta(q)\le \delta(q),
\qquad
|\nabla m_\eta(q)|\le 1.
\]
Choose a smooth function \(\chi_\eta:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R\) such that
\[
\chi_\eta(t)=0\quad\text{for }t\le 0,\qquad
0\le \chi_\eta'(t)\le 1,\qquad
\chi_\eta(t)\ge t-\eta\quad\text{for }t\ge 0.
\]
Set
\[
\rho_\eta(q)=\chi_\eta(m_\eta(q)).
\]
Then \(\rho_\eta\) is smooth,
\[
\rho_\eta=0\quad\text{on }\partial B,\qquad |d\rho_\eta|\le 1,
\]
and for every fixed \(y\in\operatorname{int}B\),
\[
\rho_\eta(y)\to \delta(y)
\qquad\text{as }\eta\to 0.
\]

Define the smooth \(2\)-form
\[
\alpha_\eta=\rho_\eta(x_3,x_4)\,dx_1\wedge dx_2.
\]
It restricts to zero on \(\partial S\): on the \(x_1\)- and \(x_2\)-faces because \(dx_1\wedge dx_2\) vanishes there, and on the \(x_3\)- and \(x_4\)-faces because \(\rho_\eta=0\) on \(\partial B\).

Let \(Y\in\mathbf I_3(S)\) be any relative filling of \(P_y\), so
\[
\operatorname{spt}(\partial Y-\llbracket P_y\rrbracket)\subset \partial S.
\]
Since \(\alpha_\eta\) vanishes on \(\partial S\),
\[
\partial Y(\alpha_\eta)
=
\llbracket P_y\rrbracket(\alpha_\eta)
=
\rho_\eta(y)S_1S_2.
\]
By Stokes’ theorem for currents,
\[
Y(d\alpha_\eta)=\rho_\eta(y)S_1S_2.
\]
But
\[
d\alpha_\eta=d\rho_\eta\wedge dx_1\wedge dx_2,
\]
whose comass is at most \(|d\rho_\eta|\le 1\). Therefore
\[
\mathbf M(Y)\ge \rho_\eta(y)S_1S_2.
\]
Letting \(\eta\to 0\) gives
\[
\mathbf M(Y)\ge S_1S_2\,\delta(y).
\]
Taking the infimum over \(Y\),
\[
\operatorname{FillVol}_S(P_y)\ge S_1S_2\,\delta(y).
\]
Thus
\[
\operatorname{FillVol}_S(P_y)=S_1S_2\,\operatorname{dist}(y,\partial B).
\]

In particular, for \(y\in B_0\),
\[
\operatorname{dist}(y,\partial B)\ge \frac{S_3}{3},
\]
because \(S_4\ge S_3\). Hence
\[
\operatorname{FillVol}_R(z_y)\ge \frac13 S_1S_2S_3.
\]

Now apply the coarea formula to the Lipschitz map \(F:R\to B\). For every nonnegative Borel function \(g\),
\[
\int_B\int_{F^{-1}(y)} g\,d\mathcal H^2\,dy
=
\int_R g\,J_2F\,dx,
\]
where
\[
J_2F=\|\Lambda^2 dF\|.
\]
For a.e. \(y\), the mass of the slice \(z_y\) is \(\operatorname{Area}(z_y)\), so with \(g=1\),
\[
\int_B \operatorname{Area}(z_y)\,dy
=
\int_R J_2F\,dx.
\]
Since \(F=\pi\circ f\) and \(\pi\) is an orthogonal projection,
\[
J_2F\le \|\Lambda^2df\|\le 1.
\]
Therefore
\[
\int_B \operatorname{Area}(z_y)\,dy
\le
\operatorname{Vol}(R).
\]

It remains to prove the two estimates involving \(\mu\). Fix a regular value \(y\in B_0\), and let \(x\in z_y\) be a point where \(f\) is differentiable and \(dF_x\) has rank \(2\). Then
\[
T_xz_y=\ker dF_x.
\]
Let
\[
N_x=(T_xz_y)^\perp.
\]
The restriction \(dF_x|_{N_x}:N_x\to\mathbb R^2\) has singular values \(\sigma_1(x)\ge \sigma_2(x)>0\).

Choose a unit vector \(n\in N_x\) such that
\[
|dF_x(n)|=\sigma_1(x).
\]
For any unit vector \(v\in T_xz_y\), one has \(dF_x(v)=0\). Since
\[
df_x=(dG_x,dF_x),
\]
we get
\[
df_x(v)=(dG_x(v),0),
\qquad
df_x(n)=(dG_x(n),dF_x(n)).
\]
In the orthogonal splitting \(\mathbb R^4=\mathbb R^2_Q\oplus\mathbb R^2_B\), the mixed \(Q\wedge B\) component of
\[
df_x(v)\wedge df_x(n)
\]
is
\[
dG_x(v)\wedge dF_x(n),
\]
whose norm is
\[
|dG_x(v)|\,|dF_x(n)|
=
|dG_x(v)|\,\sigma_1(x).
\]
This mixed component has norm at most the norm of the full wedge. Therefore, using \(\operatorname{Dil}_2(f)\le 1\),
\[
|dG_x(v)|\,\sigma_1(x)
\le
|df_x(v)\wedge df_x(n)|
\le 1.
\]
Hence
\[
|dG_x(v)|\le \sigma_1(x)^{-1}.
\]
Taking the supremum over unit \(v\in T_xz_y\),
\[
\|dG_x|_{T_xz_y}\|\le \sigma_1(x)^{-1}.
\]
Since \(T_xz_y\) is two-dimensional,
\[
J_2(G|_{T_xz_y})\le \sigma_1(x)^{-2}.
\]

Let \(p_Q:S\to Q\) be the projection onto the first two coordinates. Since \(G=p_Q\circ f\), for a.e. \(y\in B_0\),
\[
G_\#z_y
=
(p_Q)_\# f_\#z_y
=
(p_Q)_\#\llbracket P_y\rrbracket
=
\llbracket Q\rrbracket.
\]
Therefore
\[
S_1S_2
=
\mathbf M(\llbracket Q\rrbracket)
=
\mathbf M(G_\#z_y)
\le
\int_{z_y}J_2(G|_{Tz_y})\,dA
\le
\int_{z_y}\sigma_1^{-2}\,dA.
\]
Integrating over \(B_0\),
\[
\int_{B_0}\int_{z_y}\sigma_1^{-2}\,dA\,dy
\ge
S_1S_2\,|B_0|
=
\frac19 S_1S_2S_3S_4.
\]

Now define
\[
\widetilde g(x)
=
\sigma_1(x)^{-2}\,
\mathbf 1_{F^{-1}(B_0)\cap\{\operatorname{rank}dF=2\}}(x).
\]
For \(M>0\), set \(g_M=\min\{\widetilde g,M\}\). By the coarea formula,
\[
\int_{B_0}\int_{z_y} g_M\,dA\,dy
=
\int_{F^{-1}(B_0)}g_M\,J_2F\,dx.
\]
Letting \(M\to\infty\) and using monotone convergence,
\[
\int_{B_0}\int_{z_y}\sigma_1^{-2}\,dA\,dy
=
\int_{F^{-1}(B_0)\cap\{\operatorname{rank}dF=2\}}
\sigma_1^{-2}J_2F\,dx.
\]
On the rank-two set,
\[
J_2F=\sigma_1\sigma_2.
\]
Thus
\[
\sigma_1^{-2}J_2F
=
\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1}.
\]
Hence
\[
\int_{B_0}\int_{z_y}\sigma_1^{-2}\,dA\,dy
=
\int_{F^{-1}(B_0)}\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1}\,dx
=
\mu(F^{-1}(B_0)).
\]
Combining this with the previous inequality gives
\[
\mu(F^{-1}(B_0))
\ge
\frac19 S_1S_2S_3S_4.
\]

Finally, applying the ordinary coarea formula over \(B_0\),
\[
\int_{B_0}\operatorname{Area}(z_y)\,dy
=
\int_{F^{-1}(B_0)}J_2F\,dx.
\]
On the rank-two set,
\[
J_2F=\sigma_1\sigma_2
=
\sigma_1^2\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1},
\]
while both sides vanish on the rank \(<2\) set. Therefore
\[
\int_{B_0}\operatorname{Area}(z_y)\,dy
=
\int_{F^{-1}(B_0)}\sigma_1^2\,d\mu.
\]

Assume now that
\[
\int_{F^{-1}(B_0)}\sigma_1^2\,d\mu
\ge
c_d\sqrt{\frac{S_3}{S_2}}\,
\mu(F^{-1}(B_0)).
\]
Then
\[
\int_{B_0}\operatorname{Area}(z_y)\,dy
\ge
c_d\sqrt{\frac{S_3}{S_2}}\,
\mu(F^{-1}(B_0))
\ge
\frac{c_d}{9}
S_1S_2S_3S_4
\sqrt{\frac{S_3}{S_2}}.
\]
Since
\[
\operatorname{Vol}(R)
\ge
\int_B\operatorname{Area}(z_y)\,dy
\ge
\int_{B_0}\operatorname{Area}(z_y)\,dy,
\]
we obtain
\[
R_1R_2R_3R_4
=
\operatorname{Vol}(R)
\ge
\frac{c_d}{9}
S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]

This completes the proof.
