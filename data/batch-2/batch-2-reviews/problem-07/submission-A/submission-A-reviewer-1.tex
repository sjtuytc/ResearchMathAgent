\begin{center}
\textbf{A counterexample}
\end{center}

The answer is no.  We give a counterexample in
\(G=\langle a,b,c\rangle\).  Write \(\ol x=x^{-1}\), and set
\[
        w=\ol a\,a\, b\,\ol b\,\ol a\,a\, c\,\ol c\,\ol a\,a .
\]
This word is freely trivial, by deleting the adjacent inverse pairs
\[
        \ol a a,\qquad b\ol b,\qquad \ol a a,\qquad c\ol c,\qquad
        \ol a a .
\]
It is quasi-reduced: the only adjacent inverse pairs are the five
displayed ones, and in each case the following letter, if there is one,
is not the first letter of that pair.

We shall use two elementary consequences of the face condition in the
definition.  Compactify the upper half-plane to a disk and put the
marked boundary points in cyclic order.  For a matching \(M\), let
\(\Gamma(M)\) be the graph obtained from \(M\) by making each crossing a
vertex; the original boundary endpoints are leaves.

\begin{lemma}
For every admissible matching \(M\), the graph \(\Gamma(M)\) is a
forest.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Suppose that \(\Gamma(M)\) contains a cycle.  Boundary vertices have
valence one, so every cycle is contained in the interior of the disk.
Choose one which is innermost: the disk bounded by it contains no other
cycle.  It in fact contains no edge of \(\Gamma(M)\).  Indeed, any
component of the graph lying in that disk either contains a cycle, which
would be smaller, or is a tree.  Such a tree cannot be wholly interior,
because it would have an interior leaf; and if it attaches to the chosen
cycle in two places, the path between the attachment points gives a
smaller cycle.  Thus it would have to attach in exactly one place, again
forcing an interior leaf.  This is impossible since all interior vertices
have valence four and all leaves of \(\Gamma(M)\) lie on the boundary of
the disk.

Thus one complementary region has this cycle as a boundary component.
The corresponding component of
\(M\cap\partial\overline R\) is closed and has no boundary endpoint.
This contradicts the defining requirement that every such component
have boundary consisting of two marked boundary points with inverse
labels.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
Suppose that a letter \(x\) occurs in the word exactly once and
\(x^{-1}\) occurs exactly once.  Then, in every admissible matching, the
two corresponding endpoints are joined by an uncrossed interval.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let the two endpoints be \(p\) and \(q\).  At \(p\), the two local sides
of the incident edge belong to two components of face boundary.\review{the term `face boundary' has not been defined}  By the
label condition, both of these components must end at \(q\), since
\(q\) is the only endpoint with the inverse label.

By the preceding lemma the component of \(\Gamma(M)\) containing \(p\)
is a tree, and hence there is a unique graph path from \(p\) to \(q\).
If this path met a crossing, let \(c\) be the first crossing encountered
when travelling from \(p\).  At \(c\), the two local face-boundary sides
of the incoming edge leave along two different half-edges.  Removing
\(c\) from the tree separates those two half-edges into different
components, so at most one of the two resulting face-boundary arcs can
continue to \(q\).  This contradicts the previous paragraph.  Therefore
the path from \(p\) to \(q\) has no crossing, and is a single uncrossed
interval.
\end{proof}

In the word \(w\), the letters \(b,\ol b,c,\ol c\) each occur only in
the displayed adjacent inverse pairs.  Hence every admissible matching
contains the uncrossed intervals \((3,4)\) and \((7,8)\).  Since their
endpoints are adjacent on the boundary, these intervals cut off empty
boundary disks.  Deleting them and renumbering the remaining endpoints
\(1,2,5,6,9,10\) gives a cell-preserving isomorphism from \(F_w\) to the
analogous six-endpoint complex for
\[
        u=\ol a\,a\,\ol a\,a\,\ol a\,a .
\]
Here \(u\) is only an auxiliary labelled boundary word; the same matching
definition is being used after the forced intervals have been removed.

We now compute this six-endpoint complex.  For \(u\), odd endpoints have
label \(\ol a\) and even endpoints have label \(a\).  Write
\[
        ij|kl|mn
\]
for the pairing \(\{i,j\},\{k,l\},\{m,n\}\).  By Lemma 1, two intervals
cannot cross twice, since two crossings between them would make a bigon
cycle.  Also, all three pairs of intervals cannot cross, since the
three crossings would form a triangular cycle.  Hence the crossing
pattern is determined by the endpoint pairing: two pairs cross exactly
when their endpoints interleave around the boundary.  There is no
further isotopy datum here; a regular neighbourho
od of the resulting
planar forest is unique once the cyclic order of its six boundary leaves
is fixed.\review{this needs further justification}

Conversely, take any pairing for which the interleaving graph\review{interleaving graph has not been defined} is a
forest, and draw it with one transverse crossing for each interleaving
pair and no other crossings.  Then \(\Gamma(M)\) is a forest.  Every
component of \(M\cap\partial\overline R\) is therefore an arc joining
two boundary endpoints.  Push such an arc slightly into the adjacent
face.  The pushed-off arc is disjoint from the diagram and cuts the disk
into two pieces.  No interval can have its endpoints on different sides,
so each side contains an even number of marked endpoints.  Therefore
the two endpoints of the face-boundary arc have opposite parity.  For
the alternating word \(u\), opposite parity is exactly the inverse-label
condition.  Thus precisely the pairings with forest interleaving graph
are admissible.  Among the fifteen pairings of six points, the only
excluded one is the all-crossing pairing \(14|25|36\).

The resulting cells are the following.  The five vertices are
\[
\begin{array}{lll}
v_0=12|34|56, & v_1=12|36|45, & v_2=14|23|56,\\
v_3=16|23|45, & v_4=16|25|34. &
\end{array}
\]
The six edges are
\[
\begin{array}{lll}
e_{01}=12|35|46, & e_{02}=13|24|56, & e_{13}=13|26|45,\\
e_{23}=15|23|46, & e_{04}=15|26|34, & e_{34}=16|24|35,
\end{array}
\]
where the subscripts indicate the two endpoint vertices.  The three
squares are
\[
        Q_{12}=13|25|46,\qquad
        Q_{14}=14|26|35,\qquad
        Q_{24}=15|24|36,
\]
and their boundaries, obtained by the two local resolutions of each
crossing, are
\[
\begin{aligned}
\partial Q_{12}&=e_{01}\cup e_{13}\cup e_{23}\cup e_{02},\\
\partial Q_{14}&=e_{01}\cup e_{13}\cup e_{34}\cup e_{04},\\
\partial Q_{24}&=e_{02}\cup e_{23}\cup e_{34}\cup e_{04}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Thus the one-skeleton is the theta graph with poles \(v_0\) and \(v_3\)
and with three length-two paths through \(v_1,v_2,v_4\).  The square
\(Q_{12}\) fills the cycle formed by the paths through \(v_1\) and
\(v_2\).  The square \(Q_{14}\) fills the cycle formed by the paths
through \(v_1\) and \(v_4\).  These two squares are glued along the
common path through \(v_1\), so their union is a disk whose boundary is
the remaining cycle, the one through \(v_2\) and \(v_4\).  Finally
\(Q_{24}\) is glued along exactly this boundary.  Hence the whole
complex is a 2-sphere:
\[
        F_w \cong S^2 .
\]
In particular \(F_w\) is not contractible.
