\section{Review}

\paragraph{Correctness.}
I think that this work could be \textbf{publishable with minor revisions}. The main conceptual contribution is Proposition~\ref{prop:criterion}, which introduces a coloring criterion guaranteeing that a rational number satisfies the required condition. This criterion correctly identifies a nontrivial family of values of $p$ for which the problem holds.

One missing connection is that after trivial renormalization, the coloring criterion of Proposition~\ref{prop:criterion} is a well-studied object in extremal combinatorics. The \emph{Manickam--Mikl{\'o}s--Singhi (MMS) conjecture} asserts that, for any real numbers $x_1,\ldots,x_b$ satisfying
\[
x_1+\cdots+x_b=0,
\]
at least $\binom{b-1}{a-1}$ of the $a$-element subsets $S\subset [n]$ have nonnegative sum, provided that $b \geq 4a$. If the MMS conjecture were to hold, then the original problem would be resolved for all $p \leq 1/4.$ 

The best known result in this direction is due to Pokrovskiy, who proved the conjectured conclusion whenever $b\ge 10^{46}a$. Combined with the closure property established in Proposition~\ref{prop:closure}, this immediately implies that the required condition holds for all $p\le 10^{-46}$. 

\paragraph{Novelty.}
I would say that the novelty of the paper is moderate. The main contribution is Proposition~\ref{prop:criterion}, which correctly generalizes an idea of Alon, Emek, Feldman, and Tennenholtz, but fails to identify it as a known object of study. Notably, this proposition provides what is essentially the human proof of the result.

\subsection*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I interpret the question in the universal sense: determine those \(p\in[0,1]\)
for which
\[
        \Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^m w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p                  \tag{P}
\]
holds for every \(m\ge1\) and every non-negative probability vector
\((w_1,\ldots,w_m)\), with \(v_i\) i.i.d.\ \(\Ber(p)\).

The counterexamples in Proposition~\ref{prop:negative} show that no value
outside
\[
        [0,1/3]\cup\{1/2,1\}
\]
can work.  Conversely, a full classification would follow from the
small-\(p\) assertion (Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing}) that all
\(0\le p\le1/3\) work.  That assertion remains the only essential gap in this
draft.  What is proved unconditionally below is the complete negative direction,
several infinite positive families, closure properties, equivalent forms of the
remaining assertion, and a coalescence route that is ruled out by explicit
examples.

\subsection*{Positive values proved here}

Let \(\mathcal G\) be the set of probabilities for which (P) holds universally.

\begin{proposition}[a coloring criterion]\label{prop:criterion}
Let \(p=a/b\), where \(1\le a\le b\) are integers.  Suppose that every
non-negative \(b\)-tuple \(x_1,\ldots,x_b\) with \(\sum_j x_j=1\) has at least
\[
        \frac{a}{b}\binom ba=\binom{b-1}{a-1}
\]
\(a\)-subsets \(J\subseteq[b]\) satisfying \(\sum_{j\in J}x_j\ge a/b\).  Then
\(a/b\in\mathcal G\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Randomly color each original index \(i\) independently and uniformly with one
of \(b\) colors, and let \(X_j\) be the total weight of color \(j\).  For every
coloring outcome, the hypothesis gives at least \(\binom{b-1}{a-1}\) good
\(a\)-subsets of colors.  Hence, for a uniformly random \(a\)-subset \(J\) of
colors,
\[
       \Pr\!\left[\sum_{j\in J}X_j\ge a/b\right]\ge
       \frac{\binom{b-1}{a-1}}{\binom ba}=\frac ab .
\]
By symmetry the same lower bound holds for any fixed \(a\)-subset \(J\).  For
such a fixed \(J\), membership of an original index in \(J\) is an independent
\(\Ber(a/b)\) event, which is exactly (P).
\end{proof}

\review{Observe that, after the change of variables \(y_i=x_i-1/b\), the criterion becomes an instance of the \emph{Manickam--Mikl{\'o}s--Singhi} (MMS) conjecture. Under this reduction, the MMS conjecture is equivalent to the statement that the criterion holds whenever \(b\ge 4a\). The best currently known result in this direction is due to Pokrovskiy, who proved that the criterion is satisfied whenever \(b\ge 10^{46}a\).}

The criterion immediately gives the reciprocal points \(p=1/k\), \(k\ge3\):
among \(k\) color classes at least one has weight at least \(1/k\).

It also gives the following second family.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:pairs}
Let \(b\ge6\), and let \(x_1,\ldots,x_b\ge0\) with \(\sum_i x_i=1\).  Then at
least \(b-1\) unordered pairs \(\{i,j\}\) satisfy
\[
       x_i+x_j\ge \frac2b .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Put \(z_i=bx_i/2\).  Then \(\sum_i z_i=b/2\), and we must find at least
\(b-1\) pairs with \(z_i+z_j\ge1\).

Maintain a remaining set \(R\) of size \(s\) and sum at least \(s/2\).  Let
\(u\) and \(a\) be the smallest and largest elements of \(R\).  Since the
average of the elements of \(R\) is at least \(1/2\), its largest element
satisfies \(a\ge1/2\).  If \(u+a\ge1\), then \(a\) makes good pairs with all
other \(s-1\) elements and we stop.  Otherwise \(u+a<1\).  Let \(h\) be the
number of other elements making a good pair with \(a\).  If \(h\le(s-2)/2\),
then the \(h\) partners of \(a\) are at most \(a\), while the other \(s-1-h\)
elements are \(<1-a\), so
\[
 \sum_{z\in R}z
   < a+ha+(s-1-h)(1-a)
   =s-1-h+a(2h-s+2)\le s/2.
\]
The last inequality uses \(a\ge1/2\) and \(2h-s+2\le0\).  This contradiction
shows that \(h\ge\lfloor s/2\rfloor\).  Count these \(h\) pairs, delete \(u\)
and \(a\), and continue.  The remaining sum is
\(>s/2-1=(s-2)/2\), so the invariant is preserved.  Pairs counted at different
stages are distinct.  If the process reaches two remaining elements, their sum is
at least \(1\) by the invariant, so this final pair is counted and the process
stops; if one element remains, no further pair is available.  These terminal
possibilities are included in the ``never stops early'' count below.

If the procedure never stops before the terminal case, then for \(b=2r\) it
counts
\(r+(r-1)+\cdots+1\ge2r-1=b-1\), and for \(b=2r+1\) it counts
\(r+(r-1)+\cdots+1\ge2r=b-1\), using \(r\ge3\).

If it stops after \(t\) deletion steps, then \(s=b-2t\) elements remain and the
number counted is
\[
       \sum_{i=0}^{t-1}\left\lfloor \frac{b-2i}{2}\right\rfloor+(b-2t-1).
\]
For \(b=2r\) this exceeds \(b-1\) by
\[
       \frac{t(2r-t-3)}{2}\ge0\qquad(0\le t\le r-1,\ r\ge3),
\]
and for \(b=2r+1\) the same displayed expression is the excess over \(b-1\).
Hence at least \(b-1\) good pairs are always counted.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:positive}
The desired inequality holds for \(p=0\), for \(p=1/k\) with every integer
\(k\ge3\), for \(p=2/b\) with every integer \(b\ge6\), for \(p=1/2\), and for
\(p=1\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The cases \(p=0,1\) are immediate.  The reciprocal cases follow from
Proposition~\ref{prop:criterion} with \(a=1\), and Lemma~\ref{lem:pairs} plus
Proposition~\ref{prop:criterion} gives \(p=2/b\), \(b\ge6\).

For \(p=1/2\), put \(X=\sum_i w_i v_i\).  Since \(1-v_i\) has the same law as
\(v_i\), \(X\) and \(1-X\) have the same distribution.  Hence
\(\Pr[X<1/2]=\Pr[X>1/2]\), and so \(\Pr[X\ge1/2]\ge1/2\).
\end{proof}

A possible pointwise verification of the hypothesis of
Proposition~\ref{prop:criterion} would ask that, for every non-negative
\(b\)-tuple of total \(1\), at least \(\binom{b-1}{a-1}\) of the
\(a\)-subsets have load at least \(a/b\).  This deterministic statement is
false already at \(a/b=3/10\): take seven
loads equal to \(1/7\) and three loads equal to \(0\).  A triple is then heavy
only if all three of its points come from the seven positive positions, giving
\(\binom73=35<36=\binom92\).  Thus the missing rational cases require genuinely
averaged information about the random coloring, not just a pointwise
Manickam--Miklos--Singhi type count.

\begin{proposition}[multiplicative closure]\label{prop:closure}
If \(p,q\in\mathcal G\), then \(pq\in\mathcal G\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \(pq=0\), the conclusion is trivial.  Otherwise generate independent
\(\Ber(pq)\) variables as \(v_i=x_i y_i\), where
\(x_i\) are i.i.d.\ \(\Ber(p)\), \(y_i\) are i.i.d.\ \(\Ber(q)\), and the two
families are independent.  Put \(T=\sum_i w_i x_i\).  With probability at least
\(p\), \(T\ge p\).  Conditional on such an outcome, the normalized weights
\(w_i x_i/T\) form a probability vector, and the \(q\)-inequality gives
\[
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i \frac{w_i x_i}{T}y_i\ge q\,\middle|\,x_1,\ldots,x_m\right]
 \ge q .
\]
On this event \(\sum_i w_i x_i y_i\ge qT\ge pq\), proving the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[closure of the set of good probabilities]\label{prop:closed}
The set \(\mathcal G\) is closed in \([0,1]\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(p_n\in\mathcal G\) and \(p_n\to p\).  Fix a finite weight vector \(w\).
For \(r\in[0,1]\) put
\[
        F(r)=\Pr_r\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge r\right],
\]
where the subscript denotes the Bernoulli parameter.  There are only finitely
many subset sums \(w(A)\).

If infinitely many \(p_n\le p\), pass to that subsequence.  Among the finitely
many subset sums strictly smaller than \(p\), let \(p-\delta\) be the largest
one, if it exists; otherwise take any \(\delta>0\).  For all large \(n\),
\(p_n>p-\delta\), so no subset sum lies in \([p_n,p)\).  Hence the
event \(\{w(A)\ge p_n\}\) is eventually the same family of subsets as
\(\{w(A)\ge p\}\).  Therefore \(F(p)=\lim F(p_n)\ge\lim p_n=p\).

If instead infinitely many \(p_n>p\), pass to that subsequence.  Let \(p+\delta\)
be the smallest subset sum strictly larger than \(p\), if it exists; otherwise
take any \(\delta>0\).  For all large \(n\), \(p_n<p+\delta\), so the event
\(\{w(A)\ge p_n\}\) is the fixed family \(\{w(A)>p\}\), and
\[
       \mu_p\{A:w(A)>p\}=\lim F(p_n)\ge p .
\]
Since \(\{w(A)>p\}\subseteq\{w(A)\ge p\}\), again \(F(p)\ge p\).  As the weight
vector was arbitrary, \(p\in\mathcal G\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:base}
If the inequality can be proved for all \(p\in[1/6,1/3]\), then it holds for
all \(p\in[0,1/3]\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
For \(0<p\le1/3\), choose \(n\ge0\) such that \(2^n p\in[1/6,1/3]\).  Since
\(1/2\in\mathcal G\), repeated use of Proposition~\ref{prop:closure} gives
\(p=(2^n p)2^{-n}\in\mathcal G\).  The point \(p=0\) is trivial.
\end{proof}

\subsection*{Values that cannot work}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:negative}
The desired universal inequality fails for every
\(p\in(1/3,1/2)\cup(1/2,1)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \(1/3<p<1/2\), take \(m=3\) and \(w_1=w_2=w_3=1/3\).  At least two
successes are needed, so
\[
\Pr\!\left[\frac{v_1+v_2+v_3}{3}\ge p\right]
  =3p^2(1-p)+p^3
  =3p^2-2p^3 .
\]
But \(3p^2-2p^3-p=p(1-p)(2p-1)<0\) on \(1/3<p<1/2\).

If \(1/2<p<1\), take \(m=2\) and \(w_1=w_2=1/2\).  The event requires two
successes and has probability \(p^2<p\).
\end{proof}

\subsection*{Equivalent forms of the remaining small-\(p\) assertion}

\begin{conjecture}[small-\(p\) assertion]\label{conj:missing}
If \(0\le p\le1/3\), \(w_i\ge0\), \(\sum_iw_i=1\), and the \(v_i\)'s are
i.i.d.\ \(\Ber(p)\), then
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p .
\]
\end{conjecture}

The assertion is sharp even in finite examples: if one weight is at least \(p\) and
the sum of all remaining weights is \(<p\), then the event in (P) is exactly
the event that this large coordinate succeeds, and its probability is \(p\).

The nontrivial sharpness mechanism with all weights below \(p\) is a pivot plus
dust.  Fix \(0<p\le1/3\), choose \(0<\varepsilon<p\), put
\(a=p-\varepsilon\), and take
\[
   w_0=a,\qquad
   w_1=\cdots=w_N=\frac{1-a}{N}.
\]
For all sufficiently large \(N\), the dust weights \((1-a)/N\), as well as the
pivot \(a\), are below \(p\).
If \(Y_N=(1-a)N^{-1}{\rm Bin}(N,p)\), then
\[
 \Pr\!\left[w_0v_0+Y_N\ge p\right]
 =
 p\,\Pr[Y_N\ge\varepsilon]+(1-p)\Pr[Y_N\ge p].
\]
Since \(Y_N\to p(1-a)=p(1-p+\varepsilon)\) in probability and
\(\varepsilon<p(1-p+\varepsilon)<p\), this probability tends to \(p\).

A particularly useful normalization is the following ``unit-gap'' form.

\begin{proposition}[unit-gap form]\label{prop:unitgap}
For a fixed \(0<p\le1/3\), Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing} is equivalent to the
following assertion.  Let \(q=1-p\), let \(0\le b_i\le1\),
\(B=\sum_i b_i\), and let \(\zeta_i\) be i.i.d.\ \(\Ber(p)\).  Then
\[
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i b_i\zeta_i\ge p(B+1)\right]
 \ge
 \frac{p}{q}\,
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i b_i\zeta_i<p(B+1)-1\right].            \tag{UG}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Assume Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing}.  Apply it to the normalized coefficient vector
\((1,b_1,\ldots,b_n)/(B+1)\), and condition on the
Bernoulli variable multiplying the coefficient \(1\).  With
\(S=\sum_i b_i\zeta_i\),
\[
 p\,\Pr[S\ge p(B+1)-1]+q\,\Pr[S\ge p(B+1)]\ge p,
\]
which rearranges to (UG).

Conversely, suppose (UG) is known.  If an original weight is at least \(p\),
then that coordinate alone proves (P).  Otherwise choose a pivot of maximal
weight \(a\in(0,p)\).  Denote the residual weights by \(u_i\), and write
\(u_i=a b_i\).  Then \(0\le b_i\le1\) and \(a(B+1)=1\).  Multiplying (UG) by \(a\) in the thresholds
gives
\[
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i u_i v_i\ge p\right]
 \ge \frac pq
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i u_i v_i<p-a\right].
\]
Conditioning on the pivot variable then yields
\[
 p\,\Pr\!\left[\sum_i u_i v_i\ge p-a\right]
 +q\,\Pr\!\left[\sum_i u_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p,
\]
which is (P).
\end{proof}

The same statement will sometimes be more convenient in the following
homogeneous form.  For every \(h>0\), every \(0\le b_i\le h\), and
\(B=\sum_i b_i\),
\[
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i b_i\zeta_i\ge p(B+h)\right]
 \ge
 \frac{p}{q}\,
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i b_i\zeta_i<p(B+h)-h\right].            \tag{HUG}
\]
Indeed, dividing all coefficients by \(h\) reduces (HUG) to (UG), and \(h=1\)
recovers (UG).  Thus Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing}, (UG), and (HUG) are
equivalent for each fixed \(0<p\le1/3\), with \(p=0\) trivial.

The following shifted version is one rigorous piece of the natural induction.
It does not cover the pivot normalization needed for the full conjecture, but it
identifies exactly where the one-coordinate induction breaks.

\begin{proposition}[subcritical shifted unit-gap inequality]\label{prop:subcritical}
Let \(0<p\le1/3\), \(q=1-p\), \(h>0\), \(0\le b_i\le h\), \(B=\sum_i b_i\),
and \(S=\sum_i b_i\zeta_i\), where the \(\zeta_i\)'s are i.i.d.\ \(\Ber(p)\).
For
\[
        0\le \delta\le qh-pB
\]
one has
\[
 q\,\Pr\!\left[S\ge p(B+h)+\delta\right]
 \ge
 p\,\Pr\!\left[S<p(B+h)+\delta-h\right].                 \tag{6}
\]
In particular (HUG) is proved whenever \(B\le qh/p\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We induct on the number of coefficients.  If there are none, then
\(B=0\), and for \(0\le\delta\le qh\) both events in (6) are empty (with the
left threshold positive and the right threshold non-positive).

Write \(S=R+c\zeta\), \(B=B'+c\), with \(0\le c\le h\).  Put
\(T=p(B+h)+\delta\).  The difference between the two sides of (6) is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
& q\Pr[S\ge T]-p\Pr[S<T-h]                                      \\
&\quad =
q\Big(q\Pr[R\ge T]-p\Pr[R<T-h]\Big)
+p\Big(q\Pr[R\ge T-c]-p\Pr[R<T-h-c]\Big).
\end{aligned}                                                   \tag{7}
\end{equation}
For the first bracket, observe that
\[
        T=p(B'+h)+(\delta+pc),
\]
and
\[
        0\le\delta+pc\le qh-pB+pc=qh-pB'.
\]
Thus the induction hypothesis applies to \(R\) with shift \(\delta+pc\).

For the second bracket,
\[
        T-c=p(B'+h)+(\delta-qc).
\]
If \(\delta-qc\ge0\), then
\[
        \delta-qc\le\delta\le qh-pB\le qh-pB',
\]
so induction applies again.  If \(\delta-qc<0\), then
\(T-c<p(B'+h)\) and \(T-h-c<p(B'+h)-h\).  Hence
\[
 \Pr[R\ge T-c]\ge \Pr[R\ge p(B'+h)]
\]
and
\[
 \Pr[R<T-h-c]\le \Pr[R<p(B'+h)-h].
\]
The induction hypothesis at shift \(0\) therefore makes the second bracket
non-negative.  Both brackets in (7) are non-negative, completing the induction.
\end{proof}

For rational parameters there is also a useful exact cyclic formulation.  The
case \(p=0\) is trivial, so let \(p=r/b\le1/3\) with \(1\le r\le b\), in
lowest or non-lowest terms.  Assign each coefficient \(w_i\)
independently and uniformly to one of the \(b\) cyclic positions
\(\mathbb Z/b\mathbb Z\), and let \(L_j\) be the total load in the cyclic
interval \([j,j+r)\).  Then
\[
        \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i<p\right]
        =\frac1b\,\mathbb E\,\#\{j:L_j<r/b\}.              \tag{8}
\]
Consequently the rational case of Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing} is equivalent
to the averaged cyclic inequality
\[
        \mathbb E\,\#\{j:L_j<r/b\}\le b-r .                \tag{9}
\]
The corresponding deterministic assertion for each fixed load vector is false.
For \(r/b=2/7\), the cyclic load vector
\[
   \left(\frac14,\frac14,0,\frac14,0,\frac14,0\right)
\]
has six of its seven adjacent 2-intervals of load \(1/4<2/7\), and only the
interval through the first two positions is heavy.  Thus a cyclic proof would
have to use the randomness of the allocation, not just a pointwise interval
count.

There is also a useful sequential identity for \(0<p\le1/3\).  Fix any ordering
of the original weights and put \(S_i=\sum_{j\le i}w_jv_j\), \(S_0=0\).  Since
the increments are non-negative,
\[
 \Pr[S_i<p]=\Pr[S_{i-1}<p]
      -p\,\Pr[p-w_i\le S_{i-1}<p].
\]
Iterating gives
\[
 \Pr[S_m<p]
   =1-p\sum_{i=1}^m\Pr[p-w_i\le S_{i-1}<p].              \tag{10}
\]
Hence the small-\(p\) assertion is equivalent to the crossing-intensity bound
\[
        \sum_{i=1}^m\Pr[p-w_i\le S_{i-1}<p]\ge1
\]
for every deterministic ordering of the weights.

Here are exact reformulations of Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing}.

First, after replacing \(v_i\) by \(1-v_i\), it is equivalent to
\[
   q\ge\frac23,\quad \eta_i\stackrel{\rm iid}{\sim}\Ber(q)
   \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i\eta_i>q\right]\le q ,
   \qquad q=1-p .                                      \tag{1}
\]
The inequality is strict on the left because
\(\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\) is the same as
\(\sum_iw_i(1-v_i)\le1-p\).

Second, it is equivalent to the following two-tail pivot inequality: for every
\(0\le a\le p\), every set of weights \(u_i\ge0\) with
\(\sum_i u_i=1-a\), and i.i.d.\ \(v_i\sim\Ber(p)\),
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i u_i v_i\ge p\right]
   \ge
   \frac{p}{1-p}\,
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i u_i v_i<p-a\right].                 \tag{2}
\]
Indeed, (2) with \(a=0\) gives
\(U\ge\frac{p}{1-p}(1-U)\), hence \(U\ge p\).  Conversely, if
Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing} is known, add one extra weight \(a\).  Conditioning on
its Bernoulli variable gives
\[
 p\,\Pr[Y\ge p-a]+(1-p)\Pr[Y\ge p]\ge p,
\]
where \(Y=\sum_i u_i v_i\); rearranging gives (2).

Third, let \(\mu_p\) denote \(p\)-biased product measure on \(2^{[m]}\).  The
small-\(p\) assertion is equivalent to the following convex-hull statement: for
every non-empty down-set \(\mathcal D\subseteq 2^{[m]}\),
\[
       \mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p
       \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
       (p,\ldots,p)\in
       \conv\{\mathbf 1_A:\,A\in\mathcal D\}.             \tag{3}
\]
For the forward implication, take
\(\mathcal D=\{A:\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\}\).  If (3) supplied a distribution on
\(\mathcal D\) with all coordinate marginals \(p\), then
\[
 p=\sum_i p w_i
   =\mathbb E\sum_{i\in A}w_i
   <p,
\]
a contradiction.

Conversely, suppose (3) fails for a down-set \(\mathcal D\), and put
\(P(\mathcal D)=\conv\{\mathbf1_A:A\in\mathcal D\}\).  This convex set is
coordinatewise down-closed: if \(x\in P(\mathcal D)\) and \(0\le y\le x\),
represent \(x\) as the marginals of a random \(A\in\mathcal D\), then thin each
\(i\in A\) independently with probability \(y_i/x_i\) (with the obvious
convention when \(x_i=0\)).  The resulting random subset is still in
\(\mathcal D\) and has marginals \(y\).  Thus \(P(\mathcal D)\) is disjoint
from the closed upper orthant
\((p,\ldots,p)+\mathbb R_{\ge0}^m\).  Since \(P(\mathcal D)\) is compact and
the two closed convex sets have positive distance, strict separation gives a
non-zero normal vector \(c\).  The vector must have \(c_i\ge0\) for
each \(i\): otherwise the linear functional \(x\mapsto c\cdot x\) would be
unbounded below on the upper orthant
\((p,\ldots,p)+\mathbb R_{\ge0}^m\) and could not separate it from the compact
polytope \(P(\mathcal D)\).  Thus
\[
      \sum_{i\in A}c_i < p\sum_i c_i
      \qquad(A\in\mathcal D)
\]
for some non-zero \(c\ge0\).
After normalizing \(w_i=c_i/\sum_jc_j\), Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing} would imply
\[
 \mu_p(\mathcal D)
 \le \Pr\!\left[\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\right]\le1-p,
\]
contradicting \(\mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p\).

Equivalently, with \(\lambda=p/(1-p)\), the remaining down-set statement is
the partition-function bound
\[
 p{\bf 1}\notin P(\mathcal D)
 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 \sum_{A\in\mathcal D}\lambda^{|A|}
 \le (1+\lambda)^{m-1},
 \qquad 0\le\lambda\le\frac12 .                         \tag{4}
\]

The following slice formulation records the sharpest induction target I have
isolated.  If
\[
 \mathcal D=\mathcal E\cup\{A\cup\{n+1\}:A\in\mathcal F\},\qquad
 \mathcal F\subseteq\mathcal E\subseteq2^{[n]},
\]
then \(Z_\lambda(\mathcal D)=Z_\lambda(\mathcal E)+\lambda Z_\lambda(\mathcal F)\).
For \(c\ge0\), put
\[
 r_{\mathcal F}(c)=\max_{A\in\mathcal F}c(A),\qquad
 \rho^\lambda_{\mathcal E}(c)=
 \max\{c\cdot x:x\in P(\mathcal E),\ 0\le x_i\le\lambda\}.
\]
The condition \(p{\bf1}\notin P(\mathcal D)\) separates the down-convex set
\(P(\mathcal F)\) from the reflected capped set
\(\{y:\lambda({\bf1}-y)\in P(\mathcal E)\}\).  Thus the missing induction would
follow from the following capped replacement lemma:
\[
 \rho^\lambda_{\mathcal E}(c)+\lambda r_{\mathcal F}(c)<\lambda c([n])
 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 Z_\lambda(\mathcal E)+\lambda Z_\lambda(\mathcal F)\le(1+\lambda)^n .
\]
This lemma is not proved here; it is included to make the remaining obstruction
precise.  The uncapped scalar diagonal reach loses information in exactly the
mixed-slice case \(p{\bf1}\in P(\mathcal E)\) but \(p{\bf1}\notin P(\mathcal F)\).

Finally, for \(0<p\le1/3\), the same down-set has a useful
intersection-theoretic description.  If
\(\mathcal D_w=\{A:\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\}\) and
\(r=\lfloor1/p\rfloor\), then no \(r\) members of \(\mathcal D_w\) cover
\([m]\), because the weight of their union is \(<rp\le1\).  Equivalently, the
complement family
\[
   \mathcal H_w=\{B:\sum_{i\in B}w_i>1-p\}
\]
is \(r\)-wise intersecting.  Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing} is therefore the special
weighted-threshold assertion that such a family has \((1-p)\)-biased measure at
most \(1-p\).

\subsection*{A coalescence identity and a failed route}

The complement form also gives a useful local identity.  Put \(q=1-p\) and,
for a probability vector \(w\), define
\[
      \Phi_q(w)=\Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i\eta_i>q\right],
      \qquad \eta_i\stackrel{\rm iid}{\sim}\Ber(q).
\]
Thus Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing} is equivalent to \(\Phi_q(w)\le q\) for all
\(q\ge2/3\).

If two coordinates of weights \(x\le y\) are replaced by one coordinate of
weight \(x+y\), and \(Z\) denotes the contribution of all other coordinates,
then direct conditioning on \(Z\) gives
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
&\Phi_q(\ldots,x+y,\ldots)-\Phi_q(\ldots,x,y,\ldots)        \\
&\qquad
 =pq\Big(
       \Pr[q-x-y<Z\le q-y]
       -\Pr[q-x<Z\le q]
     \Big),
\end{aligned}                                      \tag{5}
\end{equation}
with the displayed half-open endpoints corresponding to the strict event
\(\sum w_i\eta_i>q\).

A tempting induction would be to prove that, unless only one coordinate remains,
some pair has non-negative gain in (5).  This is false, even in the capped regime
\(w_i<p\).  For example, take
\[
        p=\frac3{10},\qquad q=\frac7{10},\qquad
        w_1=w_2=w_3=w_4=\frac14 .
\]
Then
\[
   \Phi_q(w)=\Pr[{\rm Bin}(4,q)\ge3]
            =4q^3(1-q)+q^4=\frac{6517}{10000}.
\]
After coalescing any pair the vector is \((1/2,1/4,1/4)\), and the event
\[
        \frac12\eta_0+\frac14\eta_1+\frac14\eta_2>0.7
\]
occurs exactly when the merged coordinate succeeds and at least one of the two
remaining coordinates succeeds.  Hence the new value is
\[
        q\bigl(1-(1-q)^2\bigr)=\frac{637}{1000},
\]
which is smaller.  Thus pairwise coalescence cannot by itself prove
Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing}.

The obstruction is not isolated.  If \(n\ge4\),
\(1/n<p<1/(n-1)\), and \(w_i=1/n\), then \(w_i<p\) and
\[
 \Phi_q(w)=q^n+npq^{n-1},
\]
because the \(p\)-biased failure set has weight \(<p\) exactly when it is empty
or a singleton.  Coalescing any pair gives
\[
 \Phi_q(w^{ij})=q^{n-1}+(n-2)pq^{n-2},
\]
so
\[
 \Phi_q(w^{ij})-\Phi_q(w)
   =p q^{\,n-2}\bigl(n-2-(n-1)q\bigr)<0 .
\]
Any successful coalescence proof must therefore use larger or adaptive blocks,
or combine coalescence with a separate treatment of these reciprocal-chamber
configurations.
\subsection*{Remaining open issues}

The negative direction is complete, and the positive direction is complete for
the values listed in Corollary~\ref{cor:positive}, their finite products under
Proposition~\ref{prop:closure}, and \(p=1/2,1\).  Therefore a full solution
would follow from Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing}; combined with
Proposition~\ref{prop:negative}, it would give exactly
\([0,1/3]\cup\{1/2,1\}\).

The essential missing assertion is Conjecture~\ref{conj:missing}.  Mathematically, it
is the assertion that every weighted threshold down-set
\(\{A:\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\}\) has \(p\)-biased measure at most \(1-p\) when
\(p\le1/3\).  It appears in the document as the unproved implication
\([0,1/3]\subseteq\mathcal G\).  The approaches recorded above reduce it to
any one of the unit-gap inequality (UG), the strict complement inequality (1),
the pivot inequality (2), the convex-hull statement (3), or the
partition-function inequality (4).  Proposition~\ref{prop:closed} shows that it
would also be enough to prove the inequality on any dense subset of
\([0,1/3]\), or on the base interval \([1/6,1/3]\) by
Corollary~\ref{cor:base}.

The pairwise coalescence assertion suggested by formula (5) is not available:
the equal-weight examples in the preceding section show that every pair can have
negative coalescence gain.  A viable coalescence argument would need either block
coalescence or a dichotomy proving that configurations with all pair gains
negative already obey \(\Phi_q(w)\le q\).

The capped slice lemma displayed after (4) is the most focused remaining
inductive target.  Exhaustive enumeration of all down-sets on at most five
coordinates found no violation of the convex-hull statement (3), and the
extremal separated examples were only the expected dictatorship equalities and
lower-measure singleton-cone examples.  These computations are evidence for
(3), but of course not a proof.

The complement form is also adjacent to the hypotenuse case \(t=q\) of the
``bold play'' problem studied by Fokkink--Meester--Pelekis~\cite{FMP}: maximize
\(\Pr(\sum c_i\beta_i\ge t)\) over convex combinations of iid
\(\Ber(q)\) variables.  For a fixed finite weight vector the strict event
\(\sum c_i\beta_i>q\) is the same as \(\sum c_i\beta_i\ge t_*\), where
\(t_*\) is the next attainable subset sum above \(q\).  Their published
results prove several adjacent rectangles and reciprocal boundary points, but
the near-hypotenuse triangles with \(q<t_*\) close to \(q\), needed for all
\(q\ge2/3\), are not derived here from those results.  Frankl-type
intersection theorems~\cite{Frankl} explain some of the surrounding extremal
combinatorics, but they do not presently supply the weighted-threshold biased
measure bound (4).
