The submission claims that the inequality holds for $p \in [0, 1/3] \cup \{1/2, 1\}$. The submitted proof crucially relies on two results. First, it reduces the inequality from arbitrary convex combinations to the uniform convex combination $w_1 = \ldots = w_m = \frac{1}{m}$ by way of a comparison theorem. This reduces the inequality to the well-studied problem of bounding the probability that a binomial random variable exceeds its mean. Second, it claims that this inequality is true and follows from results in the literature. 

\paragraph{Correctness.}

The submission fails to provide enough justification for both crucial steps. For the comparison theorem (Lemma 3), the submission cites a paper of Hoeffding. But it is unclear how to translate Hoeffding's results into Lemma 3. Moreover, as stated, Lemma 3 cannot hold and we provide a counter-example below. The claimed result might be true if one replaces i.i.d real-valued random variables with Bernoulli random variables, but it is still not obvious how to prove this. 

For the second fact, the submission cites a paper of Samuels and a paper of Jogdeo-Samuels to claim that 
\[
    \Pr[\text{Binom}(n, p) \geq np] \geq p \quad \text{ for } 0 \leq p \leq \frac{1}{3}. 
\]
However, it is unclear how this result comes from these two papers. 

The submission correctly prove that the inequality does not universally hold for any of the values outside of $[0, 1/3] \cup \{1/2, 1\}$. 

\paragraph{Novelty.}

The submission does not propose any new approaches or ideas not already known in the literature. 

\section{Recommendation}

The submission should be \textbf{rejected}. The proposed proof strategy is very natural, but the submission makes no meaningful progress towards this strategy.

\subsection*{Overview / Strategy}

The problem is to determine for which probabilities \(p\) the displayed inequality holds uniformly over all finite convex weight vectors. The main positive argument has two reductions. First, Hoeffding's extremal theorem for convex combinations of i.i.d. random variables reduces the weighted problem to the special case of equal weights. In the Bernoulli case, equal weights give binomial averages. Second, a classical binomial mean-tail estimate of Jogdeo--Samuels type says that for \(0\le p\le 1/3\), every binomial average is at least its mean with probability at least \(p\). These two facts are stated precisely in Section~1 and used in Proposition~\ref{prop:smallp}.

The remaining cases are elementary. At \(p=1/2\), the Bernoulli subset is uniformly distributed over all subsets, and pairing every subset with its complement gives probability at least \(1/2\). At \(p=1\), the assertion is immediate. To show that there are no other probabilities, equal weights on three variables give counterexamples for every \(p\in(1/3,1/2)\), and equal weights on two variables give counterexamples for every \(p\in(1/2,1)\).

\begin{definition}
A Bernoulli\((p)\) random variable is a random variable taking value \(1\) with probability \(p\) and value \(0\) with probability \(1-p\). For \(p\in[0,1]\), let \(\mathcal P(p)\) denote the following assertion:
for every integer \(m\ge1\), every choice of nonnegative real numbers
\[
w_1,\ldots,w_m\ge0,\qquad \sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i=1,
\]
and every family \(v_1,\ldots,v_m\) of i.i.d. Bernoulli\((p)\) random variables, one has
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Headline theorem: the original problem]\label{thm:main}
The assertion \(\mathcal P(p)\) holds exactly for
\[
p\in[0,\tfrac13]\cup\{\tfrac12,1\}.
\]
Equivalently, the complete set of probabilities \(p\) for which
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m} w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p
\]
holds for every finite convex weight vector \((w_1,\ldots,w_m)\) and every i.i.d. Bernoulli\((p)\) family \(v_1,\ldots,v_m\) is
\[
\boxed{[0,\tfrac13]\cup\{\tfrac12,1\}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Classical one-dimensional inputs}

The proof uses two standard one-dimensional results. They are quoted in the precise forms needed below. The first is Hoeffding's reduction theorem for convex combinations of i.i.d. random variables; the second is the binomial mean-tail estimate used to evaluate the equal-weight case.

\begin{lemma}[Hoeffding's reduction for convex combinations]\label{lem:hoeffding}
Let \(X_1,X_2,\ldots\) be i.i.d. real-valued random variables. For \(t\in\mathbb R\), define
\[
L(t):=\inf_{N\ge1}
\Pr\!\left[\frac{X_1+\cdots+X_N}{N}\ge t\right].
\]
Then for every integer \(m\ge1\) and every choice of weights
\[
w_1,\ldots,w_m\ge0,\qquad \sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i=1,
\]
one has
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m} w_iX_i\ge t\right]\ge L(t).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
This is the one-dimensional half-line form of Hoeffding's extremal theorem for convex combinations of i.i.d. random variables; see Hoeffding \cite{Hoeffding1955}. To make explicit that the closed-tail convention used here causes no ambiguity, we record the standard passage from the open-tail formulation.

\review{It is unclear how the paper of Hoeffding gives the open-tail bound. Moreover, as stated, this result is incorrect. For a counter-example, consider the distribution $\Pr(X = 0) = \Pr(X = 1) = 0.1$ and $\Pr(X = 2) = 0.8$. Then 
\begin{align*}
    \Pr \left [0.1 X_1 + 0.1 X_2 + 0.8 X_3 > 0.8 \right ] &= 0.899, \\
    \inf_{N \geq 1} \Pr \left [ \frac{X_1 + \ldots + X_N}{N} \geq t\right ] & = 0.9
\end{align*}
It might be true that the result holds for Bernoulli random variables, but this is not obvious and is equivalent to Csóka's conjecture. 
}
Hoeffding's theorem gives, for every real \(u\),
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_iX_i>u\right]
\ge
\inf_{N\ge1}
\Pr\!\left[\frac{X_1+\cdots+X_N}{N}>u\right].
\]
Fix \(t\in\mathbb R\). For every \(\varepsilon>0\), apply the preceding inequality with \(u=t-\varepsilon\). For each \(N\),
\[
\left\{\frac{X_1+\cdots+X_N}{N}\ge t\right\}
\subseteq
\left\{\frac{X_1+\cdots+X_N}{N}>t-\varepsilon\right\},
\]
and therefore
\[
\inf_{N\ge1}
\Pr\!\left[\frac{X_1+\cdots+X_N}{N}>t-\varepsilon\right]
\ge
\inf_{N\ge1}
\Pr\!\left[\frac{X_1+\cdots+X_N}{N}\ge t\right]
=L(t).
\]
Thus
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_iX_i>t-\varepsilon\right]\ge L(t)
\qquad\text{for every }\varepsilon>0.
\]
As \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\), the events
\[
\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_iX_i>t-\varepsilon\right\}
\]
decrease to
\[
\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_iX_i\ge t\right\}.
\]
By continuity of probability from above,
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_iX_i\ge t\right]\ge L(t).
\]
This is the desired closed-tail statement.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Binomial mean-tail inequality]\label{lem:binomial}
Let
\[
X_{N,p}\sim\operatorname{Bin}(N,p).
\]
Then the following hold.

\begin{enumerate}
\item If \(0\le p\le \frac13\), then for every \(N\ge1\),
\[
\Pr[X_{N,p}\ge Np]\ge p.
\]
Equivalently, since \(X_{N,p}\) is integer-valued,
\[
\Pr[X_{N,p}\ge \lceil Np\rceil]\ge p.
\]

\item If \(p=\frac12\), then for every \(N\ge1\),
\[
\Pr[X_{N,1/2}\ge N/2]\ge \frac12.
\]
\end{enumerate}
Moreover, in the first assertion equality occurs for \(N=1\) and \(0<p\le1/3\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For \(p=0\), \(X_{N,0}=0\) almost surely, so
\[
\Pr[X_{N,0}\ge0]=1\ge0.
\]
For \(0<p\le1/3\), the first assertion is the classical binomial mean-tail estimate of Samuels and Jogdeo--Samuels; see \cite{Samuels1965,JogdeoSamuels1968}. 

\review{It is not clear how the cited papers imply $\Pr[X_{N, p} \geq Np] \geq p$. There are papers of Greenberg–Mohri, Pelekis–Ramon, Doerr, Rigolett-Tong which study the question of bounding this quantity. It is unclear if this inequality follows from their works. For example, from Rigolett-Tong, we get 
\[
    \Pr[X_{N, p} \geq Np] \geq \min \left \{ \frac{1}{4}, p \right \}
\]
but this is not enough. 
}

In the form needed here, that estimate states exactly that for every \(N\ge1\),
\[
\Pr[X_{N,p}\ge \lceil Np\rceil]\ge p.
\]
Because \(X_{N,p}\) is integer-valued, the events
\[
\{X_{N,p}\ge Np\}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\{X_{N,p}\ge \lceil Np\rceil\}
\]
are identical. This proves the first assertion.

For \(p=1/2\), the binomial distribution is symmetric:
\[
\Pr[X_{N,1/2}=j]=\Pr[X_{N,1/2}=N-j]
\qquad (0\le j\le N).
\]
If \(N=2r+1\) is odd, then the values \(0,\ldots,r\) pair with the values \(2r+1,\ldots,r+1\), and exactly one member of each pair is at least \(N/2=r+\frac12\). Hence
\[
\Pr[X_{N,1/2}\ge N/2]=\frac12.
\]
If \(N=2r\) is even, then the values \(0,\ldots,r-1\) pair with \(2r,\ldots,r+1\), while \(r\) is the central value and is included in the event \(\{X_{N,1/2}\ge r\}\). Therefore
\[
\Pr[X_{N,1/2}\ge N/2]
=
\frac12\bigl(1-\Pr[X_{N,1/2}=r]\bigr)
+\Pr[X_{N,1/2}=r]
=
\frac12+\frac12\Pr[X_{N,1/2}=r]
\ge\frac12.
\]
This proves the second assertion.

Finally, if \(N=1\) and \(0<p\le1/3\), then
\[
\{X_{1,p}\ge p\}=\{X_{1,p}=1\},
\]
so
\[
\Pr[X_{1,p}\ge p]=p.
\]
Thus equality is possible in the first assertion.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The positive cases}

\begin{proposition}[The interval \(0\le p\le1/3\)]\label{prop:smallp}
The assertion \(\mathcal P(p)\) holds for every
\[
p\in[0,\tfrac13].
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(p\in[0,1/3]\). Let \(m\ge1\), let
\[
w_1,\ldots,w_m\ge0,\qquad \sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i=1,
\]
and let \(v_1,\ldots,v_m\) be i.i.d. Bernoulli\((p)\) random variables.

Extend \(v_1,\ldots,v_m\), if necessary, to an infinite i.i.d. Bernoulli\((p)\) sequence \(X_1,X_2,\ldots\) with \(X_i=v_i\) for \(1\le i\le m\). Applying Lemma~\ref{lem:hoeffding} with \(t=p\) gives
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i v_i\ge p\right]
=
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i X_i\ge p\right]
\ge
\inf_{N\ge1}
\Pr\!\left[\frac{X_1+\cdots+X_N}{N}\ge p\right].
\]
For each \(N\), the sum \(X_1+\cdots+X_N\) has binomial distribution \(\operatorname{Bin}(N,p)\). Hence, writing \(X_{N,p}\sim\operatorname{Bin}(N,p)\),
\[
\Pr\!\left[\frac{X_1+\cdots+X_N}{N}\ge p\right]
=
\Pr[X_{N,p}\ge Np].
\]
Therefore
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i v_i\ge p\right]
\ge
\inf_{N\ge1}\Pr[X_{N,p}\ge Np].
\]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:binomial}, since \(0\le p\le1/3\),
\[
\Pr[X_{N,p}\ge Np]\ge p
\qquad\text{for every }N\ge1.
\]
Taking the infimum over \(N\) gives
\[
\inf_{N\ge1}\Pr[X_{N,p}\ge Np]\ge p.
\]
Consequently,
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p.
\]
Since \(m\), the weights, and the Bernoulli\((p)\) family were arbitrary, \(\mathcal P(p)\) holds.
\end{proof}


\begin{proposition}[The point \(p=1/2\)]\label{prop:halfp}
The assertion \(\mathcal P(1/2)\) holds.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(m\ge1\), let
\[
w_1,\ldots,w_m\ge0,\qquad \sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i=1,
\]
and let \(v_1,\ldots,v_m\) be i.i.d. Bernoulli\((1/2)\) random variables.

Let
\[
A=\{i\in\{1,\ldots,m\}: v_i=1\}.
\]
Because the \(v_i\) are independent and each is \(1\) with probability \(1/2\), the random set \(A\) is uniformly distributed over all \(2^m\) subsets of \(\{1,\ldots,m\}\). For a subset \(B\subseteq\{1,\ldots,m\}\), write
\[
w(B)=\sum_{i\in B}w_i.
\]
Then
\[
\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i v_i=w(A),
\]
so the desired event is
\[
\{w(A)\ge1/2\}.
\]

Pair every subset \(B\subseteq\{1,\ldots,m\}\) with its complement \(B^c\). These pairs partition the \(2^m\) subsets into \(2^{m-1}\) unordered pairs. Since
\[
w(B)+w(B^c)=\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i=1,
\]
at least one of \(B\) and \(B^c\) has weight at least \(1/2\). Thus at least one subset in each complementary pair belongs to the event \(\{w(A)\ge1/2\}\).

Every subset has probability \(2^{-m}\). Since at least \(2^{m-1}\) subsets satisfy \(w(B)\ge1/2\), we obtain
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i v_i\ge\frac12\right]
=
\Pr[w(A)\ge1/2]
\ge
2^{m-1}\cdot 2^{-m}
=
\frac12.
\]
Therefore \(\mathcal P(1/2)\) holds.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[The point \(p=1\)]\label{prop:onep}
The assertion \(\mathcal P(1)\) holds.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(m\ge1\), let
\[
w_1,\ldots,w_m\ge0,\qquad \sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i=1,
\]
and let \(v_1,\ldots,v_m\) be i.i.d. Bernoulli\((1)\) random variables. A Bernoulli\((1)\) random variable equals \(1\) almost surely, so
\[
v_i=1\qquad\text{almost surely for every }i.
\]
Therefore
\[
\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i v_i
=
\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i
=
1
\]
almost surely. Hence
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{m}w_i v_i\ge1\right]=1\ge1.
\]
Thus \(\mathcal P(1)\) holds.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Counterexamples outside the claimed set}
\begin{proposition}[Failure for \(1/3<p<1/2\)]\label{prop:countermiddle}
For every
\[
p\in\left(\frac13,\frac12\right),
\]
the assertion \(\mathcal P(p)\) fails.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(p\in(1/3,1/2)\). Take
\[
m=3,\qquad w_1=w_2=w_3=\frac13,
\]
and let \(v_1,v_2,v_3\) be i.i.d. Bernoulli\((p)\) random variables. Put
\[
N=v_1+v_2+v_3.
\]
Then \(N\sim\operatorname{Bin}(3,p)\) and
\[
\sum_{i=1}^{3}w_i v_i=\frac{N}{3}.
\]

Because \(p>1/3\), one success is not enough:
\[
\frac13<p.
\]
Because \(p<1/2<2/3\), two successes are enough:
\[
\frac23\ge p.
\]
Thus
\[
\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{3}w_i v_i\ge p\right\}
=
\{N\ge2\}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{3}w_i v_i\ge p\right]
=
\Pr[N\ge2].
\]
Since \(N\sim\operatorname{Bin}(3,p)\),
\[
\Pr[N\ge2]
=
\binom{3}{2}p^2(1-p)+p^3
=
3p^2(1-p)+p^3
=
p^2(3-2p).
\]
Now
\[
p-p^2(3-2p)
=
p(1-3p+2p^2)
=
p(1-p)(1-2p).
\]
For \(p\in(1/3,1/2)\), the factors \(p\), \(1-p\), and \(1-2p\) are all positive. Hence
\[
p-p^2(3-2p)>0,
\]
or equivalently,
\[
p^2(3-2p)<p.
\]
Thus this choice of weights gives
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{3}w_i v_i\ge p\right]<p.
\]
Therefore \(\mathcal P(p)\) fails.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Failure for \(1/2<p<1\)]\label{prop:counterlarge}
For every
\[
p\in\left(\frac12,1\right),
\]
the assertion \(\mathcal P(p)\) fails.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(p\in(1/2,1)\). Take
\[
m=2,\qquad w_1=w_2=\frac12,
\]
and let \(v_1,v_2\) be i.i.d. Bernoulli\((p)\) random variables. Put
\[
N=v_1+v_2.
\]
Then
\[
\sum_{i=1}^{2}w_i v_i=\frac{N}{2}.
\]
Because \(p>1/2\), one success is not enough:
\[
\frac12<p.
\]
Since \(N\) can only take the values \(0,1,2\), the event
\[
\left\{\frac{N}{2}\ge p\right\}
\]
occurs only when \(N=2\), that is, when both \(v_1\) and \(v_2\) equal \(1\). Hence
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{2}w_i v_i\ge p\right]
=
\Pr[v_1=1,\ v_2=1].
\]
By independence,
\[
\Pr[v_1=1,\ v_2=1]=p^2.
\]
Since \(0<p<1\),
\[
p^2<p.
\]
Thus this choice of weights gives
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{2}w_i v_i\ge p\right]<p.
\]
Therefore \(\mathcal P(p)\) fails.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of the classification}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}]
Proposition~\ref{prop:smallp} proves that \(\mathcal P(p)\) holds for every
\[
p\in[0,\tfrac13].
\]
Proposition~\ref{prop:halfp} proves that \(\mathcal P(1/2)\) holds, and Proposition~\ref{prop:onep} proves that \(\mathcal P(1)\) holds. Hence
\[
\mathcal P(p)\quad\text{holds for every}\quad
p\in[0,\tfrac13]\cup\{\tfrac12,1\}.
\]

It remains to show that no other probability works. Since \(p\) is a probability, \(p\in[0,1]\). The complement of
\[
[0,\tfrac13]\cup\{\tfrac12,1\}
\]
inside \([0,1]\) is
\[
\left(\frac13,\frac12\right)\cup\left(\frac12,1\right).
\]
For every \(p\in(1/3,1/2)\), Proposition~\ref{prop:countermiddle} gives an explicit choice of weights for which the desired inequality fails. For every \(p\in(1/2,1)\), Proposition~\ref{prop:counterlarge} gives an explicit choice of weights for which the desired inequality fails.

Therefore \(\mathcal P(p)\) holds exactly for
\[
p\in[0,\tfrac13]\cup\{\tfrac12,1\}.
\]
This proves the theorem.
\end{proof}
