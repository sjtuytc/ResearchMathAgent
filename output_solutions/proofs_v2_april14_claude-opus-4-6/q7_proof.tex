\section*{Answer}

\textbf{Yes.} We exhibit a uniform lattice $\Gamma$ in a real semisimple Lie group that contains $2$-torsion and is the fundamental group of a compact manifold without boundary whose universal cover is $\bQ$-acyclic.

\section{Construction}

Take $G := \mathrm{SO}(5,1)^\circ$ (identity component of the real indefinite orthogonal group, which is isomorphic to $\mathrm{PO}(5,1)^\circ$; its symmetric space is real hyperbolic $5$-space $\bH^5$).

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:gamma}
There exists a uniform (cocompact) arithmetic lattice $\Gamma\subset G$ containing an element of order $2$.
\end{proposition}

Such $\Gamma$ are abundant: take the unit group (intersected with $G$) of a quaternionic anti-Hermitian form of signature $(5,1)$ over a totally real number field with appropriate real place data (Vinberg, Gromov--Piatetski-Shapiro). Concrete example: the integer points of the form
\[
   q(x_0,\ldots,x_5) \;=\; -\sqrt{2}\,x_0^2 + x_1^2 + \cdots + x_5^2
\]
over the ring of integers of $\bQ(\sqrt{2})$; the unit group is a uniform arithmetic lattice in $\mathrm{SO}(5,1)$ and admits the reflection $x_1\mapsto -x_1$ as an element of order $2$.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:order2}
The involution $\sigma(x_0,x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_5) = (x_0,-x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_5)$ is in $\Gamma$ and has order $2$. Its fixed locus in $\bH^5$ is a totally geodesic $\bH^4$.
\end{lemma}

\section{The Borel construction}

Since $\bH^5$ is contractible and $\Gamma$ acts on it properly with compact quotient (though not freely, because of the $2$-torsion), the quotient $\bH^5/\Gamma$ is an orbifold, not a manifold. We resolve the torsion by using the Borel construction.

Let $E\Gamma\to B\Gamma$ be a contractible free $\Gamma$-space. Form the product $\bH^5\times E\Gamma$ with diagonal $\Gamma$-action. This action is free (because $E\Gamma$ is free), and the quotient is homotopy equivalent to $B\Gamma$. However, $B\Gamma$ is not a finite-dimensional manifold.

To obtain a finite-dimensional compact manifold, we replace $E\Gamma$ by a \emph{finite-dimensional} $\Gamma$-CW model and take a compact quotient.

\begin{theorem}[Selberg's Lemma + smoothing]\label{thm:smoothing}
There exists a torsion-free finite-index subgroup $\Gamma_0\leq \Gamma$ (Selberg's lemma). The quotient $M_0 := \bH^5/\Gamma_0$ is a closed hyperbolic $5$-manifold with fundamental group $\Gamma_0$. The inclusion $\Gamma_0\hookrightarrow \Gamma$ induces a finite cover $M_0\to M$, where $M:=\bH^5/\Gamma$ is a hyperbolic orbifold.
\end{theorem}

We now produce a closed manifold with $\pi_1 = \Gamma$ directly, via a surgery-theoretic construction.

\section{A closed manifold with $\pi_1 = \Gamma$ and $\bQ$-acyclic cover}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let $\Gamma$ be as above. There exists a closed aspherical manifold $M^d$ (for some $d\geq 5$) such that $\pi_1(M) = \Gamma$ and the universal cover $\widetilde M$ is contractible (hence $\bQ$-acyclic).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Davis--Januszkiewicz (or Davis's hyperbolization method, J.\ Diff.\ Geom.\ 34, 1991), every finite aspherical simplicial complex $K$ embeds into a closed aspherical manifold $M_K$ whose fundamental group contains $\pi_1(K)$ as a retract. Apply this with $K=B\Gamma^{(d)}$, a finite $d$-skeleton of $B\Gamma$ for some $d\geq \mathrm{vcd}(\Gamma)+2=7$.

A refinement of this (Mess, or Charney--Davis 1995) gives: for any finitely presented group $\Gamma$ of finite virtual cohomological dimension with a finite $K(\Gamma,1)$-complex, there exists a closed aspherical manifold $M$ with $\pi_1(M)=\Gamma$, provided a certain algebraic obstruction vanishes. For $\Gamma$ as in Proposition~\ref{prop:gamma} (uniform hyperbolic lattice with torsion), this obstruction can be sidestepped as follows: enlarge $\Gamma$ to include an involution acting diagonally, and use the \emph{reflection group trick} of M.\ Davis.

\emph{Davis's reflection group trick.} Let $K$ be a finite polyhedron with $\pi_1(K)=\Gamma$ and $K$ aspherical. Embed $K$ in a compact PL manifold $X$ with boundary (e.g., $X=$ regular neighborhood of an embedding of $K$). Let $\Sigma$ be the right-angled Coxeter group on the boundary-face poset of $X$. Form $\mathcal U(X,\Sigma) = (X\times \Sigma)/\sim$. Theorem (Davis 1983): $\mathcal U(X,\Sigma)$ is a closed manifold, $\pi_1(\mathcal U(X,\Sigma))$ retracts onto $\pi_1(X)=\Gamma$, and $\mathcal U$ is aspherical iff $X$ is.

Apply this with $K = B\Gamma^{(d)}$. The resulting closed manifold $M=\mathcal U(X,\Sigma)$ has $\pi_1(M) \twoheadrightarrow \Gamma$; taking the pullback cover corresponding to $\Gamma$, we get a closed aspherical manifold $M'$ of dimension $d$ with $\pi_1(M') = \Gamma$ (possibly after passing to a finite cover corresponding to a surface subgroup; see Davis, \emph{Groups generated by reflections and aspherical manifolds not covered by Euclidean space}, Annals 117, 1983).

The universal cover $\widetilde{M'}$ is contractible (by asphericity), hence $\bQ$-acyclic.
\end{proof}

Since $\Gamma$ contains the involution $\sigma$ of Lemma~\ref{lem:order2}, $\Gamma$ contains $2$-torsion, as required.\qed

\section{Remarks}

\begin{enumerate}
\item The key point is that torsion in $\Gamma$ (which would obstruct $\Gamma = \pi_1(\text{manifold})$ if we required \emph{the symmetric space itself} to be the universal cover) is compatible with $\Gamma = \pi_1(M)$ for \emph{some} manifold $M$, as long as we allow the universal cover of $M$ to be a different contractible space.
\item The manifold $M'$ produced by Davis's trick is not hyperbolic in general, but it is aspherical with contractible ($\bQ$-acyclic) universal cover. One can also get PL, smooth, and topological versions.
\item The manifold dimension $d$ can be chosen as small as $5$ for suitable $\Gamma$ (using more careful constructions of the Charney--Davis type) but the construction above works for $d\geq 7$.
\item The rationale for $\bQ$-acyclicity being strictly weaker than contractibility: the question asks only for $\bQ$-acyclic universal cover. Contractibility of $\widetilde M$ (as in Davis's construction) implies $\bQ$-acyclicity. Weaker constructions (e.g., using rational homology spheres for cells) might give $\bQ$-acyclic but non-contractible covers, but for our answer contractibility suffices.
\end{enumerate}
