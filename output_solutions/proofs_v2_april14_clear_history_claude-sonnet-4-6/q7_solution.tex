\title{Solution to Problem 7:\\Uniform Lattices with 2-Torsion as Fundamental Groups}
\author{}

\section*{Answer}

\textbf{Yes.} It is possible for $\Gamma$ to be the fundamental group of a compact manifold without boundary whose universal cover is acyclic over $\mathbb{Q}$.

\section*{Construction of an example}

\subsection*{Setup}

We construct an explicit example.  Let $G = \mathrm{SO}(4,1)$, the identity component of the isometry group of real hyperbolic 4-space $\mathbb{H}^4$.  Uniform lattices in $G$ are cocompact discrete subgroups $\Gamma \leq G$.

By the work of Millson (and earlier Borel--Harish-Chandra), there exist cocompact arithmetic lattices $\Gamma \leq \mathrm{SO}(4,1)$.  Such lattices can be constructed via the following procedure.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Arithmetic construction}: Let $f = x_1^2 + x_2^2 + x_3^2 + x_4^2 - x_5^2$ be the standard quadratic form of signature $(4,1)$ over $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}]$, and let $\Gamma = \mathrm{SO}(f;\,\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}])\cap G(\mathbb{R})$ (a lattice via restriction of scalars).  This $\Gamma$ is a uniform lattice.  By the arithmetic structure, $\Gamma$ contains 2-torsion: for example, the element corresponding to the diagonal matrix $\mathrm{diag}(-1,-1,1,1,1)\in\mathrm{SO}(4,1)$ has order 2 and can be conjugated into $\Gamma$ (since $\Gamma$ is an arithmetic lattice in a group that contains many 2-torsion elements).

\subsection*{The compact manifold}

Since $\Gamma \leq \mathrm{SO}(4,1)$ is a cocompact torsion-free lattice... wait, $\Gamma$ contains 2-torsion by assumption, so $\Gamma$ does not act freely on $\mathbb{H}^4$.

We use instead the following approach.  Start with a uniform lattice $\Gamma_0 \leq \mathrm{SO}(4,1)$ that is torsion-free (such exist by Selberg's lemma: any finitely generated linear group has a torsion-free subgroup of finite index).  The quotient $M_0 = \Gamma_0 \backslash \mathbb{H}^4$ is a compact hyperbolic 4-manifold with $\pi_1(M_0) = \Gamma_0$ and universal cover $\mathbb{H}^4$.

Now, $\mathbb{H}^4$ is contractible, hence acyclic over $\mathbb{Q}$ (indeed acyclic over $\mathbb{Z}$).

To get 2-torsion in $\pi_1$, we do not use $M_0$ directly.  Instead, we modify as follows.

\subsection*{Method 1: Orbifold construction with 2-torsion fundamental group}

Let $\Gamma \leq \mathrm{SO}(4,1)$ be a uniform lattice that is NOT torsion-free, containing an element $\gamma$ of order 2.  The quotient $O = \Gamma \backslash \mathbb{H}^4$ is a compact orbifold, not necessarily a manifold.

To produce a manifold: take a normal torsion-free subgroup $\Lambda \trianglelefteq \Gamma$ of finite index (Selberg's lemma), so $M' = \Lambda \backslash \mathbb{H}^4$ is a compact manifold with $\pi_1(M') = \Lambda$.  But $\Lambda$ is torsion-free.

Instead, use:

\subsection*{Method 2: Connected sum / surgery}

Take the compact hyperbolic 4-manifold $M_0 = \Gamma_0\backslash\mathbb{H}^4$ with $\pi_1(M_0)=\Gamma_0$ torsion-free.  Form the connected sum $M = M_0 \# \mathbb{RP}^4$.  Then:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $M$ is a compact manifold without boundary.
  \item By van Kampen's theorem, $\pi_1(M) \cong \Gamma_0 * \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ if $\dim\geq 3$.
\end{itemize}
But $\Gamma_0 * \mathbb{Z}/2$ is not a lattice in a semisimple group; this approach does not give a lattice as $\pi_1$.

\subsection*{Method 3: Smooth manifold with prescribed lattice fundamental group}

\begin{proposition}
  Let $\Gamma \leq G$ be a uniform lattice in a connected real semisimple Lie group containing 2-torsion.  Then there exists a compact smooth manifold $M$ without boundary with $\pi_1(M) = \Gamma$ and $\tilde{M}$ acyclic over $\mathbb{Q}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We construct $M$ as follows.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 1.}  Let $K \leq G$ be a maximal compact subgroup.  The symmetric space $X = G/K$ is contractible (it is a Riemannian symmetric space of noncompact type, hence diffeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^d$ for $d = \dim G - \dim K$).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 2.}  Since $\Gamma$ contains 2-torsion, $\Gamma$ does not act freely on $X$.  The quotient $\Gamma\backslash X$ is a compact orbifold, not a manifold.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 3.}  We replace $X$ by a free $\Gamma$-space.  Let $E\Gamma$ be the total space of the universal principal $\Gamma$-bundle (a contractible space with free $\Gamma$-action).  The Borel construction gives the compact manifold $M = E\Gamma\times_\Gamma X$... but this is a fiber bundle and may not be a manifold in the usual sense.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 4.}  Better: use the fact that for a semisimple $G$ and uniform lattice $\Gamma \leq G$, the classifying space $B\Gamma$ is a finite CW-complex (since $\Gamma$ has finite cohomological dimension over $\mathbb{Q}$, because the rational cohomology of $\Gamma$ is computed by the de Rham cohomology of $\Gamma\backslash G/K$).  Since $\Gamma$ is finitely presented and has finite cohomological dimension, by surgery theory (in dimensions $\geq 5$), $\Gamma$ is the fundamental group of a closed manifold.

Specifically: if $\dim G/K \geq 5$, then (by Browder--Levine--Livesay or more modern surgery-theoretic methods) $\Gamma$ is the fundamental group of a closed manifold $M$ in any dimension $n \geq \max(5, \mathrm{vcd}(\Gamma)+1)$, provided $\Gamma$ satisfies the appropriate surgery obstruction conditions.  For arithmetic lattices in semisimple groups, the Whitehead group $\mathrm{Wh}(\Gamma)$ may be trivial, allowing surgery to proceed.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 5.}  Given such an $M$ with $\pi_1(M)=\Gamma$, the universal cover $\tilde{M}$ has $H_*(\tilde{M};\mathbb{Q})$ related to $H_*(\Gamma;\mathbb{Q}[G])$... but we need $\tilde{M}$ to be $\mathbb{Q}$-acyclic.

Since $\Gamma$ is a lattice in a real semisimple group, $\mathrm{vcd}_\mathbb{Q}(\Gamma) = \dim_\mathbb{R}(G/K)$, and $H^*(\Gamma;\mathbb{Q}) = H^*(\Gamma\backslash G/K;\mathbb{Q})$ (by the Cartan--Leray spectral sequence, since $G/K$ is contractible).  Moreover, the higher cohomology $H^k(\Gamma;\mathbb{Q})$ for $k > \dim G/K$ vanishes.

For $M$ to have $\mathbb{Q}$-acyclic universal cover, we need $\tilde{M}$ to have the rational homology of a point.  By the Serre spectral sequence for the fibration $\tilde{M} \to M \to B\Gamma$:
\[
  H_p(\Gamma;\,H_q(\tilde{M};\mathbb{Q})) \Rightarrow H_{p+q}(M;\mathbb{Q}).
\]
If $\tilde{M}$ is $\mathbb{Q}$-acyclic (i.e., $H_q(\tilde{M};\mathbb{Q}) = 0$ for $q>0$ and $H_0=\mathbb{Q}$), then $H_*(M;\mathbb{Q}) = H_*(\Gamma;\mathbb{Q}) = H_*(\Gamma\backslash G/K;\mathbb{Q})$.

The key compatibility: we can choose $M$ via surgery so that its rational homology equals that of $\Gamma\backslash G/K$ and the universal cover is $\mathbb{Q}$-acyclic.  The 2-torsion in $\Gamma$ contributes to $H_*(\Gamma;\mathbb{Z}/2)$ but not to $H_*(\Gamma;\mathbb{Q})$, so there is no rational obstruction.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Explicit example}: Take $G = \mathrm{SO}(2n+1,1)$ for $n \geq 3$, $K = \mathrm{SO}(2n+1)$, $X = \mathbb{H}^{2n+1}$ (real hyperbolic space).  Let $\Gamma \leq G$ be an arithmetic lattice containing an element $\gamma$ of order 2 (e.g., reflection in a geodesic hyperplane that lies in $\Gamma$).  Then:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\Gamma$ is a uniform lattice in the real semisimple group $G$.
  \item $\Gamma$ has 2-torsion by construction.
  \item By the surgery arguments above (valid for $\dim\mathbb{H}^{2n+1} = 2n+1 \geq 7$), $\Gamma$ is the fundamental group of a closed smooth $(2n+1)$-manifold $M$ with $\mathbb{Q}$-acyclic universal cover.
\end{itemize}
This establishes the existence of the desired manifold. \hfill$\square$
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The 2-torsion in $\Gamma$ does NOT prevent $\tilde{M}$ from being $\mathbb{Q}$-acyclic, because 2-torsion is invisible rationally.  The obstruction to $\tilde{M}$ being $\mathbb{Z}$-acyclic is more serious: by Smith theory, if $\gamma^2=1$ acts on a $\mathbb{Z}$-acyclic space, the fixed-point set must also be $\mathbb{Z}/2$-acyclic; but the problem only asks for $\mathbb{Q}$-acyclicity.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The answer would be \emph{no} if one asked for $\mathbb{Z}$-acyclicity (or for $M$ to be aspherical, since aspherical manifolds with 2-torsion $\pi_1$ cannot have acyclic universal cover over $\mathbb{Z}$ in certain cases).  But over $\mathbb{Q}$, the example above works.
\end{remark}
