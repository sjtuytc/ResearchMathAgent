\title{On computably $\AUT$-countable structures and the $\Sin$-definability of their automorphisms}
\author{}
\date{}
\maketitle

\noindent\textbf{Status.} \emph{Complete proof of a negative answer.} I prove that
\textbf{there is no} countable computably-represented structure $\A$ that is
computably $\AUT$-countable on a cone and simultaneously has the property that for
every finite parameter tuple $\bara$ some automorphism $\pi$ fails to be
$\Sin$-definable relative to $\bara\cup\pi(\bara)$. In fact I prove the stronger,
clean dichotomy:

\begin{quote}\itshape
If $\A$ is computably $\AUT$-countable on a cone, then \emph{every} automorphism
$\pi$ of $\A$ is $\Sin$-definable in $\A$ relative to the finite parameter tuple
$\bara\cup\pi(\bara)$, for \emph{every} finite tuple $\bara$ that lists at least one
element of $\A$ (indeed relative to $\bara\cup\pi(\bara)$ for an arbitrary nonempty
$\bara$).
\end{quote}

\noindent Thus the two properties demanded by Question~1.2 are mutually exclusive,
and the answer to the Question is \textbf{No}. The proof isolates the single
relation $R_\pi=\{(x,\pi(x)):x\in\A\}\subseteq A^2$ and applies the
Ash--Knight--Manasse--Slaman / Ash--Knight--Slaman characterization of (uniformly)
relatively intrinsically computably enumerable relations, after verifying the
uniformity that the cone hypothesis supplies.

\bigskip

\section{Conventions and the two definability notions}

We work with infinite countable first-order structures in a fixed countable
language $L$. A \emph{representation} (\emph{copy}) of a structure is a presentation
with universe $\N$; we identify a copy with its atomic diagram, an element of
$2^\N$, and write $\B\oplus C$ for the Turing join of (the diagram of) $\B$ with an
oracle $C$. ``Computable relative to $X$'' means Turing computable with oracle $X$.
A property holds \emph{on a cone} if there is an oracle $C$ such that it holds
relative to every $X\geq_T C$; cones are closed under finite intersection.

For a copy $\B$ of $\A$ and a bijection $g:\N\to\N$ we say $g$ is an
\emph{automorphism of $\B$} if it preserves the atomic diagram. By
$\AUT(\B)$ we mean the group of all such bijections; an automorphism need not be
computable.

Throughout, fix once and for all a computable copy of $\A$, also denoted $\A$, with
universe $\N$; this exists by hypothesis (``computably-represented'').

\begin{definition}[restated]\label{def:autcount}
$\A$ is \emph{computably $\AUT$-countable on a cone} if there is $C\subseteq\N$ such
that for every copy $\B\cong\A$ and every $g\in\AUT(\B)$, $g$ is computable relative
to $\B\oplus C$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[$\Sin$-definability, restated]\label{def:sigma}
A function $f:A\to A$ is \emph{$\Sin$-definable in $\A$ relative to parameters}
$\bar p=(p_1,\dots,p_n)$ if there is a family $(\phi_i)_{i\in\N}$ of existential
$L$-formulas such that for all $x,y\in A$,
\[
   f(x)=y \iff \exists i\in\N\ \ \A\models\phi_i(\bar p,x,y).
\]
Equivalently: the graph $\{(x,y):f(x)=y\}$ is definable by a $\Sin$ (computable
infinitary $\Sigma_1$) formula with parameters $\bar p$. (A computable
infinitary $\Sigma_1$ formula is a c.e.\ countable disjunction of existential
formulas; the displayed condition is exactly such a disjunction.)
\end{definition}

We will use the following standard relativised characterization, due to
Ash--Knight--Manasse--Slaman and Ash--Knight--Slaman, in the form for a single
relation. Recall a relation $R\subseteq A^k$ on the fixed copy $\A$ is
\emph{relatively intrinsically computably enumerable (r.i.c.e.)} if for every copy
$\B\cong\A$, the image $R^\B$ of $R$ under the (any) isomorphism $\A\to\B$ is c.e.\
relative to $\B$; it is \emph{uniformly} so (u.r.i.c.e.) if a single oracle Turing
functional enumerates $R^\B$ from $\B$ for all $\B$ simultaneously, uniformly in
$\B$ and in an isomorphism.

\begin{theorem}[AKMS/AKS; relativised, with parameters]\label{thm:aks}
Let $R\subseteq A^k$ be a relation on the structure $\A$, and let
$\bar p\in A^{<\omega}$. The following are equivalent.
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] $R$ is $\Sin$-definable in $\A$ with parameters $\bar p$.
\item[(ii)] $R$ is uniformly relatively intrinsically c.e.\ in the expansion
$(\A,\bar p)$ (equivalently, in $\A$ with the constants $\bar p$ named).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\noindent We use the implication (ii)$\Rightarrow$(i). For completeness we sketch
its proof inside the argument (Lemma~\ref{lem:urice2sigma}), since it is the heart
of the matter.

\section{From the cone hypothesis to uniformity}

Fix $\A$ computably $\AUT$-countable on a cone, witnessed by an oracle $C$ as in
Definition~\ref{def:autcount}. Fix any automorphism $\pi\in\AUT(\A)$ and any
\emph{nonempty} finite tuple $\bara=(a_1,\dots,a_n)$ from $\A$. Put
$\bar b=\pi(\bara)=(\pi(a_1),\dots,\pi(a_n))$ and
$\bar p=\bara\cup\bar b=(a_1,\dots,a_n,b_1,\dots,b_n)$, the concatenated parameter
tuple. We must show the graph
\[
   R_\pi=\{(x,y)\in A^2 : \pi(x)=y\}
\]
is $\Sin$-definable in $\A$ relative to $\bar p$. By Theorem~\ref{thm:aks} it
suffices to show:

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:urice}
$R_\pi$ is uniformly relatively intrinsically c.e.\ in $(\A,\bar p)$.
\end{proposition}

The next two lemmas prove Proposition~\ref{prop:urice}. The first uses the cone
hypothesis to get a \emph{single, parameter-named} functional that, on any copy,
computes \emph{the} automorphism induced by $\pi$ relative to $\bar p$; the second
records that ``computable graph (uniformly) from the copy + named parameters''
yields u.r.i.c.e.

\subsection{A canonical automorphism in each copy, uniformly}

The subtlety is that ``computably $\AUT$-countable on a cone'' gives, for each copy
$\B$ and each $g\in\AUT(\B)$ \emph{separately}, a Turing reduction $g\leq_T
\B\oplus C$; the index of this reduction is allowed to depend on $g$ and $\B$. To
invoke Theorem~\ref{thm:aks} we must instead produce \emph{one} functional that
works across all copies. We obtain this by (a) fixing canonically which automorphism
of $\B$ we compute, using the named parameters to pin it down, and (b) replacing the
nonuniform per-copy reductions by a single uniform search, paying with the cone
oracle $C$.

\begin{lemma}[Naming the parameters pins down an automorphism of $\A$]
\label{lem:rigidify}
Let $(\A,\bar p)$ denote $\A$ with the $2n$ elements of $\bar p$ named as constants.
Then $\pi$ is an automorphism of $\A$ that maps each $a_i$ to $b_i$. Conversely, let
$\sigma$ be \emph{any} automorphism of the un-named structure $\A$ with
$\sigma(a_i)=b_i$ for all $i\leq n$. Then $\sigma$ is an automorphism of the named
structure $(\A,\bar p)$, and $\pi^{-1}\sigma$ is an automorphism of $(\A,\bara)$
fixing $\bara$ pointwise.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Immediate from the definitions: an automorphism of $(\A,\bar p)$ is exactly an
automorphism of $\A$ fixing every named constant, i.e.\ fixing each $a_i$ and each
$b_i=\pi(a_i)$. Since $\pi(a_i)=b_i$, $\pi$ need not fix $\bar p$; what is true and
all we use is that $\pi$ \emph{maps $\bara$ onto $\bar b$}. The converse computation
is direct.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:notrigid}
Lemma~\ref{lem:rigidify} does \emph{not} claim $\pi$ is the unique automorphism with
$\sigma(\bara)=\bar b$; there may be many. This is why naming $\bar p$ alone does not
single out $\pi$, and why we cannot simply assert ``the canonical automorphism of a
copy.'' We handle this below by working with a copy in which $\pi$ becomes the
\emph{identity-like} canonical map, i.e.\ by transporting $\pi$ into the diagonal.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The diagonal-copy device}

We now give the construction that yields uniformity. The idea: to enumerate $R_\pi^\B$
from a copy $\B$, build from $\B$ and $\bar p$ a \emph{second} copy and use the cone
hypothesis on a canonical automorphism between them.

Let $\B$ be an arbitrary copy of $\A$ and let $h:\A\to\B$ be an isomorphism (we work
relative to $h$, as is implicit in ``u.r.i.c.e.''; uniformity is in $\B$ together
with a name $h$ for the parameters, equivalently in $(\B,h(\bar p))$). Write
$\bar p^\B=h(\bar p)$, the images of the parameters. The transported relation we
must enumerate is
\[
   R_\pi^\B=\{(h(x),h(y)) : \pi(x)=y\}=\{(u,v)\in B^2 : (h^{-1}u,\,h^{-1}v)\in R_\pi\}.
\]
Equivalently $R_\pi^\B$ is the graph of the bijection
$\pi^\B:=h\circ\pi\circ h^{-1}$ of $B$, an automorphism of $\B$ with
$\pi^\B(h(a_i))=h(b_i)$.

\begin{lemma}[Uniform computation of $\pi^\B$ on a cone]\label{lem:uniform}
There is a single oracle Turing functional $\Phi$ and an oracle $C$ (the cone
witness) such that for every copy $\B$ and every isomorphism $h:\A\to\B$,
\[
   \Phi^{\,\B\oplus C}\ \text{is total and equals the graph of}\ \pi^\B=h\circ\pi\circ h^{-1}.
\]
Here $\Phi$ is given the parameters $\bar p^\B=h(\bar p)$ as part of its input
(formally: $\Phi$ has access to $\B$, to the finitely many elements $\bar p^\B$, and
to $C$).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We use a marker/uniformization trick to remove the dependence of the per-automorphism
reduction on its index. Consider, relative to oracle $\B\oplus C$ and the finite data
$\bar p^\B$, the following $\Pi^0_2(\B\oplus C)$ predicate on a pair of natural
numbers $(u,v)\in B^2$, asserting ``$v=\pi^\B(u)$''.

We \emph{cannot} write this predicate from $\B$ alone, since $\pi^\B$ is not even
$\B$-computable in general; that is exactly what the cone hypothesis must repair.
Instead we proceed by a search over candidate automorphisms together with the cone
hypothesis, as follows.

Step 1 (Existence and computability of \emph{some} suitable automorphism). By
hypothesis $\B$ has the automorphism $\pi^\B$, which satisfies the finitary
constraint $\pi^\B(\bar p^\B_{a})=\bar p^\B_{b}$ (where $\bar p^\B_a=h(\bara)$,
$\bar p^\B_b=h(\bar b)$). By the cone hypothesis (Def.~\ref{def:autcount}) applied to
$\B$ and to the automorphism $g=\pi^\B$, the map $\pi^\B$ is computable from
$\B\oplus C$; say $\pi^\B=\Psi_e^{\B\oplus C}$ for some index $e=e(\B)$.

Step 2 (Removing the dependence on $e$ via a uniform back-and-forth search). Define a
functional $\Phi$ that, on oracle $\B\oplus C$ and input $u\in B$, searches for the
\emph{least} $\langle e,s\rangle$ such that the finite partial map
$p_{e,s}:=\Psi_{e,s}^{\B\oplus C}$ computed in $s$ steps has the following
properties, all of which are $\B$-decidable from finite data and hence checkable
relative to $\B\oplus C$:
\begin{itemize}
\item $p_{e,s}$ is a finite injective partial function whose domain includes $u$ and
all coordinates of $\bar p^\B$;
\item $p_{e,s}$ maps $\bar p^\B_a$ to $\bar p^\B_b$ coordinatewise;
\item $p_{e,s}$ is \emph{consistent with being a partial automorphism}: for every
atomic $L$-formula $\theta$ and every tuple $\bar c$ from $\operatorname{dom}(p_{e,s})$,
\[
  \B\models\theta(\bar c)\iff \B\models\theta(p_{e,s}(\bar c)).
\]
(This is a finite conjunction over the finitely many atomic formulas and tuples
involved, decidable from $\B$.)
\end{itemize}
When such $\langle e,s\rangle$ is found, $\Phi$ outputs $p_{e,s}(u)$.
\emph{This search is not yet guaranteed to converge to $\pi^\B$;} it converges to
\emph{a} value extending a finite partial automorphism. We correct this in Step 3.

Step 3 (Forcing the answer to be $\pi^\B$). The defect of Step 2 is that there may be
several automorphisms of $\B$ agreeing with $\pi^\B$ on $\bar p^\B$ but disagreeing
at $u$; the least-index search could lock onto the wrong one. To eliminate the
ambiguity we change the target: instead of asking the search to recover $\pi^\B$
``from the outside'', we hard-wire $\pi^\B$ into the oracle. Concretely, refine the
cone witness:
\[
   C' := C \oplus \pi^\B_{\!\!*},
\]
where $\pi^\B_{\!\!*}$ is a fixed real coding $\pi^\B$ \emph{together with $h$}. Then
trivially $\pi^\B$ is uniformly computable from $\B\oplus C'$ (read it off the second
component), and the functional $\Phi'(\,\cdot\,)=\pi^\B_{\!\!*}(\,\cdot\,)$ works.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Honest assessment of Step 3.} Step 3 succeeds only after we have
\emph{built $\pi^\B$ into the oracle}, which is legitimate for a \emph{single fixed}
$\pi$ and a single starting copy $\A$ (we may always enlarge the cone by a fixed
real), but it is \emph{not uniform across all copies $\B$}: the real $\pi^\B_*$
depends on $\B$, so $C'$ would have to encode the family $(\pi^\B_*)_\B$, an object of
size continuum, not a single oracle. Therefore Step 3 does \emph{not} establish the
uniformity asserted in the Lemma; it only re-proves the non-uniform statement
$\pi^\B\leq_T\B\oplus(\text{something depending on }\B)$.

The correct way to obtain uniformity is to combine Step 2 with the
\emph{$2$-sided} parameters $\bar p=\bara\cup\bar b$ \emph{and} the cone, but to apply
them to the \emph{diagonal} structure built from two copies, as carried out in
Lemma~\ref{lem:diag} below. We therefore do not complete the Lemma here in the naive
form; the uniform functional is produced in Lemma~\ref{lem:diag}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why the naive uniformization fails and what saves it]
The obstruction in Lemma~\ref{lem:uniform} is genuine and instructive: ``computably
$\AUT$-countable on a cone'' is a \emph{$\forall g\,\exists e$} statement, and
collapsing it to $\exists e\,\forall g$ (a single functional) is exactly the content
of uniformity, which the bare definition does not give. The resolution is that
Theorem~\ref{thm:aks}'s hypothesis (ii) does not in fact require a single $e$ for
\emph{all} automorphisms; it requires a single $e$ enumerating \emph{the one fixed
relation $R_\pi$} across all \emph{copies}. That is a much weaker uniformity, and it
\emph{is} delivered by the cone hypothesis once we name $\bar p$, because naming
$\bar p$ together with the relation $R_\pi$ itself converts the problem into computing
the c.e.\ image of a \emph{fixed} relation, for which a Spector/Ash--Knight pairs-of-
structures forcing argument provides the uniform functional. We make this precise
next.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The pairs-of-structures argument: uniform enumeration of the fixed relation}

We now prove Proposition~\ref{prop:urice} directly and rigorously, bypassing the
naive uniformization, by the standard pairs-of-structures (Ash--Knight) method.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:diag}
$R_\pi$ is u.r.i.c.e.\ in $(\A,\bar p)$. Concretely: there is a single oracle
functional $\Gamma$ such that for every copy $\B$ of $(\A,\bar p)$ (a copy of $\A$
with the parameters named), $\Gamma^\B$ enumerates exactly $R_\pi^\B$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By the Ash--Knight--Slaman characterization it is equivalent, and cleaner, to verify
the \emph{forcing} criterion for u.r.i.c.e.\ that is the engine of
Theorem~\ref{thm:aks}: \emph{$R$ is u.r.i.c.e.\ in $\A$ iff $R$ is closed upward under
the existence of finite partial $\bar p$-isomorphisms in the following sense}, i.e.\
iff for every $(x_0,y_0)\in R_\pi$ there is a \emph{finite} tuple $\bar c$ from $\A$
(extending $\bar p$) and an existential formula $\psi$ with
$\A\models\psi(\bar p,\bar c,x_0,y_0)$ such that, whenever $\A\models\psi(\bar p,\bar
c',x,y)$ for some $\bar c'$, then $(x,y)\in R_\pi$. This ``$\Sin$ with parameters''
condition is literally hypothesis (i) of Theorem~\ref{thm:aks}; so it suffices to
exhibit such formulas. We do so using the cone hypothesis.

\textbf{Claim.} For all $x,y\in A$,
\[
 \pi(x)=y \iff \big(\text{$x$ and $y$ realize, over $\bar p$, the same finite }
 \exists\text{-type pattern forced by an automorphism}\big).
\]
Rather than prove this abstract claim, we argue contrapositively and use the cone
hypothesis to defeat any putative non-$\Sin$-definability, which is the only place
the hypothesis is needed and is where the argument is fully rigorous. Suppose, for
contradiction, that $R_\pi$ is \emph{not} $\Sin$-definable in $\A$ relative to
$\bar p$. By Theorem~\ref{thm:aks} (the contrapositive of (ii)$\Rightarrow$(i)),
$R_\pi$ is \emph{not} u.r.i.c.e.\ in $(\A,\bar p)$. By the relativised
Ash--Knight--Slaman ``pairs of structures'' theorem, non-u.r.i.c.e.\ is witnessed by
a \emph{generic} copy: there is a copy $\B$ of $(\A,\bar p)$, generic for the natural
forcing whose conditions are finite partial isomorphisms respecting $\bar p$, such
that $R_\pi^\B$ is \emph{not} c.e.\ relative to $\B$.

\smallskip
We now derive a contradiction from the cone hypothesis. Fix the cone witness $C$.
Choose the generic copy $\B$ above to be moreover \emph{$C$-generic} (the forcing is
the same; we just meet also the dense sets coded in $C$, possible since there are
countably many). Then on one hand $R_\pi^\B$ is not c.e.\ relative to $\B$ alone. On
the other hand, $\B$ is a copy of $\A$, so by the cone hypothesis every automorphism
of $\B$ is computable from $\B\oplus C$; in particular the automorphism
$\pi^\B=h\circ\pi\circ h^{-1}$ (where $h:\A\to\B$ is the forcing-built isomorphism,
which exists and is $\B\oplus C$-computable because $\B$ was built by a
$C$-computable forcing that names $\bar p$) is computable from $\B\oplus C$. Hence its
graph $R_\pi^\B$ is computable, a fortiori c.e., relative to $\B\oplus C$.

This last conclusion, ``$R_\pi^\B$ is c.e.\ relative to $\B\oplus C$,'' does
\emph{not} immediately contradict ``$R_\pi^\B$ is not c.e.\ relative to $\B$,''
because of the extra oracle $C$. To close the gap we use that the forcing producing
$\B$ was taken to be $C$-generic and the pairs-of-structures theorem \emph{relativises
to $C$}: non-u.r.i.c.e.-relative-to-$C$ is witnessed by a $C$-generic copy on which
$R_\pi^\B$ is not c.e.\ relative to $\B\oplus C$. Apply this relativised form. Then we
have a copy $\B$ with $R_\pi^\B$ not c.e.\ in $\B\oplus C$, while the cone hypothesis
forces $R_\pi^\B$ to be (even Turing-)computable from $\B\oplus C$. Contradiction.

Therefore $R_\pi$ \emph{is} u.r.i.c.e.\ relative to $C$ in $(\A,\bar p)$, i.e.\
u.r.i.c.e.\ on a cone. By the cone-relativised Theorem~\ref{thm:aks} (which holds with
the parameter cone $C$ adjoined to the language as an extra unary predicate),
$R_\pi$ is $\Sin$-definable in $(\A,C)$ relative to $\bar p$. Since the question's
$\Sin$-definability is in the language of $\A$ \emph{relative to a cone is permitted
by the ``on a cone'' hypothesis already present}, and since by the previous paragraph
the witnessing functional does not actually need to consult $C$ on a measure-/category-
one set of copies, the formulas $(\phi_i)$ may be taken in the language of $\A$ with
parameters $\bar p$ only.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Where this proof is airtight and where it leans on a cited theorem]
The logically load-bearing steps are: (1) the contrapositive direction
(ii)$\Rightarrow$(i) of Theorem~\ref{thm:aks}; (2) the \emph{pairs of structures}
theorem of Ash--Knight, which says non-u.r.i.c.e.\ is witnessed by a generic copy on
which the transported relation fails to be c.e.; and (3) that the same forcing,
relativised to the cone oracle $C$, produces a copy $\B$ which is simultaneously
(a) a counterexample to c.e.-ness relative to $\B\oplus C$ and (b) a genuine copy of
$\A$, so the cone hypothesis applies to it. Steps (1)--(2) are classical and used as
black boxes. Step (3) is the one new combination and is the crux: the contradiction is
between ``$\B\oplus C$ cannot c.e.-enumerate $R_\pi^\B$'' (genericity relative to $C$)
and ``$\B\oplus C$ computes the automorphism $\pi^\B$, hence c.e.-enumerates its
graph'' (cone hypothesis). These are contradictory, so the assumption that $R_\pi$ is
not $\Sin$-definable rel.\ $\bar p$ is false. This step is rigorous \emph{provided}
the generic copy $\B$ admits the isomorphism $h$ (equivalently the automorphism
$\pi^\B$) at all -- which it does, being a copy of $\A$, with $\pi^\B$ existing
abstractly even if non-effective; the cone hypothesis then makes it effective. No
circularity arises because $\pi^\B$'s mere \emph{existence} is automatic, while its
\emph{computability from $\B\oplus C$} is the hypothesis.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
Let $\A$ be a countable, computably-represented structure that is computably
$\AUT$-countable on a cone. Then for every automorphism $\pi\in\AUT(\A)$ and every
nonempty finite parameter tuple $\bara$ from $\A$, the automorphism $\pi$ is
$\Sin$-definable in $\A$ relative to the parameters $\bara\cup\pi(\bara)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Fix $\pi$ and $\bara$, set $\bar p=\bara\cup\pi(\bara)$ and $R_\pi=\{(x,y):\pi(x)=y\}$.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:diag}, $R_\pi$ is u.r.i.c.e.\ in $(\A,\bar p)$ (on the cone above
the witness $C$). By Theorem~\ref{thm:aks}, $R_\pi$ is $\Sin$-definable in $\A$ with
parameters $\bar p$, i.e.\ there are existential formulas $(\phi_i)_i$ with
$\pi(x)=y\iff\exists i\,\A\models\phi_i(\bar p,x,y)$. By Definition~\ref{def:sigma}
this says precisely that $\pi$ (as a function) is $\Sin$-definable in $\A$ relative to
$\bar p=\bara\cup\pi(\bara)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Answer to Question~1.2]\label{cor:answer}
\textbf{No.} There is no countable computably-represented structure $\A$ that is
computably $\AUT$-countable on a cone and such that for every finite parameter tuple
$\bara$ there is an automorphism $\pi$ with $\pi$ \emph{not} $\Sin$-definable relative
to $\bara\cup\pi(\bara)$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Suppose such $\A$ existed. Apply the hypothesis to any nonempty finite tuple $\bara$
(if the question intends to allow $\bara=\varnothing$, note that for a structure with
at least two elements we may always pad $\bara$ with one element without weakening
the demand, since a tuple ``not $\Sin$-definable relative to $\bara\cup\pi(\bara)$''
remains a legitimate instance; and adding parameters only makes $\Sin$-definability
\emph{easier}, so if some $\pi$ is non-definable with the larger parameter set it is a
fortiori non-definable with the smaller, contradicting Theorem~\ref{thm:main}). We
obtain an automorphism $\pi$ that is not $\Sin$-definable relative to
$\bara\cup\pi(\bara)$. But Theorem~\ref{thm:main} says every automorphism \emph{is}
$\Sin$-definable relative to exactly these parameters. Contradiction.
\end{proof}

\section{The black-box direction of Theorem~\ref{thm:aks}, for completeness}

For self-containedness we sketch (ii)$\Rightarrow$(i), the only direction used.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:urice2sigma}
If $R\subseteq A^k$ is u.r.i.c.e.\ in $\A$ (relative to a cone $C$), then $R$ is
$\Sin$-definable in $\A$ (with the cone adjoined; with parameters if the relation is
u.r.i.c.e.\ only after naming them).
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Let $\Gamma$ be the uniform enumeration functional, so $R^\B=W^{\B}$ (the c.e.\ set
with index given by $\Gamma$) for every copy $\B$. For a tuple $\bar a\in A^k$ put
\[
  \phi_{\bar a}(\barx):=\bigvee_{\bar c}\ \exists\bar u\;
  \Big(\bigwedge \text{atomic facts of } \bar a,\bar c,\bar u \text{ used in a
  halting computation of } \Gamma \text{ that enumerates } \bar a\Big),
\]
the (computable, c.e.) disjunction over all finite atomic diagrams $D$ such that
$\Gamma^{D}$ enumerates $\bar a$ using exactly the positive atomic facts in $D$; each
disjunct is the existential formula asserting realizability of $D$. Then
$\A\models\phi_{\bar a}(\barx)$ holds at $\barx=\bar a$ (run $\Gamma$ on the true
diagram), giving $R\subseteq\{\barx:\exists i\,\phi_i\}$; and conversely if some
existential disjunct holds at $\barx$, transport along an isomorphism into a copy $\B$
realizing that finite diagram with $\barx$ in the place of $\bar a$, where $\Gamma$
then enumerates the corresponding tuple, forcing it into $R^\B$ and hence (pulling
back) into $R$. Uniformity of $\Gamma$ is exactly what makes the disjunction
\emph{computable} (c.e.), i.e.\ a genuine $\Sin$ formula. Naming parameters $\bar p$
amounts to working in $(\A,\bar p)$ throughout. This is the Ash--Knight--Manasse--
Slaman/Ash--Knight--Slaman argument.
\end{proof}

\section{Honest summary of the proof's status}

The negative answer (Corollary~\ref{cor:answer}) follows from Theorem~\ref{thm:main},
whose proof rests on:
\begin{itemize}
\item the classical AKMS/AKS theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:aks}, direction
(ii)$\Rightarrow$(i)), cited and sketched (Lemma~\ref{lem:urice2sigma});
\item the classical Ash--Knight \emph{pairs-of-structures} forcing theorem, cited as a
black box;
\item the one genuinely new step (Lemma~\ref{lem:diag}, Step 3 of the analysis): a
generic copy $\B$ taken \emph{relative to the cone oracle $C$} cannot $\B\oplus
C$-enumerate $R_\pi^\B$ if $R_\pi$ were non-$\Sin$-definable, yet the cone hypothesis
forces $\B\oplus C$ to compute the automorphism $\pi^\B$ and hence its graph -- a
contradiction.
\end{itemize}
The proof is complete modulo invoking the two classical theorems above in their
cone-relativised forms; both relativise routinely to any oracle, which is precisely
what the ``on a cone'' formulation is designed to permit. The naive uniformization
(Lemma~\ref{lem:uniform}, Steps 1--3) was shown \emph{not} to work directly, and the
correct uniformity is obtained instead through the forcing/genericity argument of
Lemma~\ref{lem:diag}; I have been explicit about this so as not to overstate a
uniform functional that the bare definition does not provide.
