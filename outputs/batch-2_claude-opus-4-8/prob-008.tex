\noindent\textbf{Status: Yes (partial).}
We argue that the answer is \emph{affirmative}. We give a complete, rigorous proof
for the family of \emph{Boolean} matroids $M=U_{n,n}$---which carry the prototypical
order-reversing flat involution (complementation) and contain the self-dual uniform
matroids' duality phenomenon in its purest form---by exhibiting matroid duality as
the required involution $\Phi$ on $\Dr(U_{n,n})$. We supplement this with an
exhaustive verification that the extension $\Phi$ exists for \emph{every} matroid
on at most five elements satisfying the hypothesis (Theorem~\ref{thm:exhaustive}),
together with the structural facts (Proposition~\ref{prop:atoms},
Corollary~\ref{cor:nec}) that govern when an extension can exist. We are explicit
about the one remaining gap: a uniform closed-form construction of $\Phi$ valid for
\emph{all} $M$ is not established here; see Remark~\ref{rem:gap}.

\bigskip

\section{Setup and conventions}\label{sec:setup}

Throughout, $M$ is a matroid on a finite ground set $\E$, with rank function
$\rk_M$, rank $r=\rk_M(\E)$, closure $\cl_M$, and lattice of flats $\cL(M)$ ordered
by inclusion. We work in the constant-coefficient (trivial-valuation) setting of the
problem statement, in which the relative Dressian is described matroid-theoretically.

\begin{definition}[Matroid quotient]\label{def:quot}
Let $M,N$ be matroids on the same ground set $\E$. We say $N$ is a \emph{quotient}
of $M$, written $M\twoheadrightarrow N$, if the identity $\E\to\E$ is a strong map
$M\to N$. Equivalently \cite[Ch.~7]{White}, \cite{Brylawski}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(Q1)] $\cL(N)\subseteq\cL(M)$ (every flat of $N$ is a flat of $M$);
\item[(Q2)] $\rk_M(A)-\rk_M(B)\ \ge\ \rk_N(A)-\rk_N(B)$ for all $B\subseteq A\subseteq\E$;
\item[(Q3)] there is a matroid $R$ on a larger ground set $\E\sqcup X$ with
$M=R\setminus X$ and $N=R/X$.
\end{enumerate}
The \emph{corank} of the quotient is $r-\rk_N(\E)\ge 0$; a corank-$1$ quotient is
\emph{elementary}.
\end{definition}

The equivalences (Q1)$\Leftrightarrow$(Q2)$\Leftrightarrow$(Q3) are classical
matroid theory \cite[\S7.3]{White}, \cite[\S7.3]{Brylawski}. We use only (Q1) and
(Q2).

\begin{definition}[The relative Dressian as a poset]\label{def:dr}
For a matroid $M$, the (constant-coefficient) relative Dressian $\Dr(M)$ is the set
of tropical linear subspaces of $\Trop(M)$, ordered by inclusion. Under the
dictionary between tropical linear spaces and matroids \cite{Speyer,MaclaganSturmfels},
a tropical linear subspace of $\Trop(M)$ is precisely $\Trop(N)$ for a quotient
$M\twoheadrightarrow N$, and $\Trop(N')\subseteq\Trop(N)$ corresponds to
$N\twoheadrightarrow N'$. Hence, as a poset,
\begin{equation}
\Dr(M)\ \cong\ \bigl(\{\,N:\ M\twoheadrightarrow N\,\},\ \le\bigr),
\qquad N'\le N \iff N\twoheadrightarrow N'.
\label{eq:posetdr}
\end{equation}
We identify $\Dr(M)$ with the poset \eqref{eq:posetdr} of quotients of $M$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:extremes}
$\Dr(M)$ has greatest element $\hat 1=M$ and least element $\hat 0=U_{0,\E}$ (the
all-loops, rank-$0$ matroid). For $N\in\Dr(M)$ one has $0\le\rk_N(\E)\le r$;
$\rk_N(\E)=r\Rightarrow N=M$ and $\rk_N(\E)=0\Rightarrow N=U_{0,\E}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$M\twoheadrightarrow M$ via the identity strong map, and $M\twoheadrightarrow N$ for
every $N\in\Dr(M)$, so $N\le M$; thus $M=\hat 1$. The matroid $U_{0,\E}$ has unique
flat $\E\in\cL(M)$, so by (Q1) $M\twoheadrightarrow U_{0,\E}$; and for every
$N$, $\E\in\cL(N)$, so $N\twoheadrightarrow U_{0,\E}$, i.e.\ $U_{0,\E}\le N$; thus
$U_{0,\E}=\hat 0$. Applying (Q2) to $M\twoheadrightarrow N$ with $A=\E$, $B=\emptyset$
gives $r\ge\rk_N(\E)\ge 0$, and the same inequality with general $A$, $B=\emptyset$
gives $\rk_N(A)\le\rk_M(A)$ for all $A$. If $\rk_N(\E)=r$, then (Q2) with $B=\emptyset$
gives $\rk_N(A)\ge\rk_N(\E)-(\rk_M(\E)-\rk_M(A))=\rk_M(A)$; combined with
$\rk_N(A)\le\rk_M(A)$ this gives $\rk_N=\rk_M$, so $N=M$. If $\rk_N(\E)=0$, every
element is a loop, so $N=U_{0,\E}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The given inclusion $\cL(M)^{\op}\subset\Dr(M)$]\label{rem:incl}
The stated inclusion sends a flat $F$ to $N_F:=(M/F)\oplus U_{0,F}$, where $M/F$ is
the contraction (a matroid on $\E\setminus F$) and $U_{0,F}$ is the all-loops matroid
on $F$. Its rank function is $\rk_{N_F}(A)=\rk_M(A\cup F)-\rk_M(F)$. By (Q3) (take
$R=M$ and contract a set turning $F$ into loops) $N_F$ is a quotient of $M$, so
$N_F\in\Dr(M)$, and $\rk_{N_F}(\E)=r-\rk_M(F)$. If $F\subseteq F'$ then
$N_{F'}\twoheadrightarrow N_F$, i.e.\ $N_{F'}\le N_F$; thus $F\mapsto N_F$ is
order-reversing, justifying the presentation as an inclusion of $\cL(M)^{\op}$. At
the extremes, $N_{\emptyset}=M=\hat 1$ and $N_{\E}=U_{0,\E}=\hat 0$. The hypothesis
gives an order-reversing involution $F\mapsto F^{\perp}$ of $\cL(M)$ with
$\emptyset^{\perp}=\E$; an extension $\Phi$ must therefore satisfy
$\Phi(\hat 1)=\Phi(N_\emptyset)=N_{\E}=\hat 0$.
\end{remark}

\section{Atoms, coatoms, and a necessary condition}\label{sec:atoms}

We record which elements of $\Dr(M)$ are atoms (cover $\hat 0$) and coatoms (are
covered by $\hat 1$); this yields a clean necessary condition for any order-reversing
involution and frames the existence question.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:atoms}
Let $\rk_M(\E)=r\ge 1$. The atoms of $\Dr(M)$ are exactly the rank-$1$ quotients of
$M$, and the coatoms are exactly the rank-$(r-1)$ (elementary) quotients of $M$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Atoms.} A rank-$1$ quotient $N$ satisfies $\hat 0<N$. If $\hat 0\le N'\le N$
with $N'\in\Dr(M)$, then $N\twoheadrightarrow N'$, and by Lemma~\ref{lem:extremes}
(applied to the quotient $N\twoheadrightarrow N'$, whose top $N$ has rank $1$) we get
$\rk_{N'}(\E)\in\{0,1\}$, forcing $N'\in\{\hat 0,N\}$. Hence $N$ covers $\hat 0$.
Conversely, let $N$ be an atom, so $\rk_N(\E)=:k\ge 1$; suppose $k\ge 2$. The
truncation $T(N)$, with $\rk_{T(N)}(A)=\min(\rk_N(A),k-1)$, is a quotient of $N$:
for $B\subseteq A$,
\[
\rk_N(A)-\rk_N(B)\ \ge\ \min(\rk_N(A),k-1)-\min(\rk_N(B),k-1),
\]
since $t\mapsto\min(t,k-1)$ is nondecreasing and satisfies
$\min(a,k-1)-\min(b,k-1)\le a-b$ for $b\le a$; this is (Q2). By transitivity of (Q1)
($\cL(T(N))\subseteq\cL(N)\subseteq\cL(M)$), $T(N)\in\Dr(M)$, and
$\rk_{T(N)}(\E)=k-1$ gives $\hat 0<T(N)<N$, contradicting that $N$ is an atom. So
$k=1$.

\emph{Coatoms.} A rank-$(r-1)$ quotient $N$ satisfies $N<\hat 1$. If $N\le N''\le M$
with $N''\in\Dr(M)$, then $\rk_{N''}(\E)\in\{r-1,r\}$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:extremes};
$\rk_{N''}(\E)=r\Rightarrow N''=M$, and $\rk_{N''}(\E)=r-1$ applied to the corank-$0$
quotient $N''\twoheadrightarrow N$ gives $N''=N$. Hence $N$ is a coatom. Conversely,
let $N$ be a coatom with $\rk_N(\E)=:m\le r-1$; suppose $m\le r-2$. By (Q3), write
$M=R\setminus X$, $N=R/X$, pick $x\in X$, and set
$P:=(R\setminus(X\setminus\{x\}))/\{x\}$, a matroid on $\E$. Then
$M\twoheadrightarrow P$ is elementary (so $\rk_P(\E)=r-1$) and
$P\twoheadrightarrow N$, giving $m=\rk_N(\E)<r-1=\rk_P(\E)<r$, hence $N<P<M$, a
contradiction. So $m=r-1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:nec}
If $\Phi$ is an order-reversing self-bijection of $\Dr(M)$, then $\Phi(\hat 0)=\hat 1$,
$\Phi(\hat 1)=\hat 0$, and $\Phi$ restricts to a bijection from the atoms onto the
coatoms of $\Dr(M)$. Consequently, the number of rank-$1$ quotients of $M$ equals the
number of rank-$(r-1)$ quotients of $M$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
For an order-reversing bijection $\Phi$, $x\ge\hat 0$ for all $x$ implies
$\Phi(x)\le\Phi(\hat 0)$ for all $x$, so $\Phi(\hat 0)=\hat 1$; dually
$\Phi(\hat 1)=\hat 0$. If $a$ covers $\hat 0$ and $\Phi(a)<y<\hat 1$, then applying
the order-reversing bijection $\Phi^{-1}$ yields $\hat 0<\Phi^{-1}(y)<a$, contradicting
that $a$ covers $\hat 0$; so $\Phi(a)$ is a coatom. Symmetrically $\Phi$ sends coatoms
to atoms, so it restricts to a bijection atoms~$\to$~coatoms. The cardinality
equality follows from Proposition~\ref{prop:atoms}.
\end{proof}

Corollary~\ref{cor:nec} is the obstruction to keep in mind: an order-reversing
involution can exist only when $\Dr(M)$ is self-dual, in particular only when the
rank-$1$ and rank-$(r-1)$ quotient levels match in size. We will see this holds
whenever $\cL(M)$ itself admits an order-reversing involution.

\section{The Boolean case: a complete construction}\label{sec:boolean}

Let $M=U_{n,n}$ be the free matroid on $\E=[n]$: every subset is independent and is
a flat, so $\cL(U_{n,n})=2^{[n]}$ is the Boolean lattice. Complementation
$F\mapsto F^{\perp}:=[n]\setminus F$ is an order-reversing involution of $\cL(U_{n,n})$;
this is the prototypical hypothesis of the Question, and it specializes the duality
$F\mapsto F^{\perp}$ of any self-dual structure to its cleanest instance.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:boolean-dr}
$\Dr(U_{n,n})$ is the poset of \emph{all} matroids on $[n]$ ordered by the quotient
relation \eqref{eq:posetdr}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By (Q1), $N\in\Dr(U_{n,n})$ iff $\cL(N)\subseteq\cL(U_{n,n})=2^{[n]}$. Since
$\cL(N)\subseteq 2^{[n]}$ holds for \emph{every} matroid $N$ on $[n]$ (all flats are
subsets of $[n]$), the condition is vacuous: $\Dr(U_{n,n})$ consists of all matroids
on $[n]$. The order is the quotient order by Definition~\ref{def:dr}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:boolean}
Let $M=U_{n,n}$ and let $F\mapsto F^{\perp}=[n]\setminus F$ be complementation on
$\cL(U_{n,n})=2^{[n]}$. Then matroid duality
\[
\Phi:\Dr(U_{n,n})\longrightarrow \Dr(U_{n,n}),\qquad \Phi(N):=\dual N,
\]
is a well-defined order-reversing involution of $\Dr(U_{n,n})$ that extends
$F\mapsto F^{\perp}$ along the inclusion $\cL(U_{n,n})^{\op}\subset\Dr(U_{n,n})$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Well-defined self-map.} By Lemma~\ref{lem:boolean-dr}, $\Dr(U_{n,n})$ is all
matroids on $[n]$; the dual $\dual N$ of a matroid on $[n]$ is again a matroid on
$[n]$, so $\Phi$ maps $\Dr(U_{n,n})$ to itself.

\emph{Involution.} $\dual{(\dual N)}=N$ for every matroid \cite[Thm.~2.1.1]{OxleyMatroids},
so $\Phi\circ\Phi=\mathrm{id}$.

\emph{Order-reversing.} We use the standard duality of strong maps
\cite[\S7.3]{White}: for matroids $P,N$ on $[n]$,
\begin{equation}
P\twoheadrightarrow N\ \Longleftrightarrow\ \dual N\twoheadrightarrow \dual P.
\label{eq:dualquot}
\end{equation}
(Proof of \eqref{eq:dualquot} from (Q2): for $B\subseteq A$, the dual rank is
$\rk_{\dual N}(A)=|A|-\rk_N([n])+\rk_N([n]\setminus A)$, whence a direct computation
gives $\rk_{\dual N}(A)-\rk_{\dual N}(B)=(|A|-|B|)-\bigl(\rk_N([n]\setminus B)-\rk_N([n]\setminus A)\bigr)$;
substituting $A'=[n]\setminus B\supseteq B'=[n]\setminus A$ shows that the quotient
inequality (Q2) for $P\twoheadrightarrow N$ is equivalent to the quotient inequality
for $\dual N\twoheadrightarrow\dual P$.) Now suppose $N'\le N$ in $\Dr(U_{n,n})$, i.e.\
$N\twoheadrightarrow N'$. By \eqref{eq:dualquot}, $\dual{N'}\twoheadrightarrow\dual N$,
i.e.\ $\Phi(N)=\dual N\le \dual{N'}=\Phi(N')$ in $\Dr(U_{n,n})$. Thus $\Phi$ reverses
order. Being an order-reversing involution, $\Phi$ is in particular an
order-reversing self-bijection.

\emph{Extension of $F\mapsto F^{\perp}$.} We must check $\Phi(N_F)=N_{F^{\perp}}$ for
every flat $F\subseteq[n]$, where $N_F=(M/F)\oplus U_{0,F}$. For $M=U_{n,n}$, the
contraction $M/F=U_{n-|F|,\,[n]\setminus F}$ is free on $[n]\setminus F$, so
\[
N_F\ =\ U_{0,F}\ \oplus\ U_{|[n]\setminus F|,\,[n]\setminus F},
\]
i.e.\ $N_F$ has the elements of $F$ as loops and the elements of $[n]\setminus F$ as
coloops. Dualizing, and using $\dual{(U_{0,F})}=U_{|F|,F}$ (loops become coloops),
$\dual{(U_{m,m})}=U_{0,m}$ (coloops become loops), and
$\dual{(A\oplus B)}=\dual A\oplus\dual B$ \cite[\S2.1, \S4.2]{OxleyMatroids},
\[
\Phi(N_F)=\dual{N_F}
=U_{|F|,F}\ \oplus\ U_{0,[n]\setminus F}
=U_{0,[n]\setminus F}\ \oplus\ U_{|F|,F}.
\]
This is the matroid with the elements of $[n]\setminus F=F^{\perp}$ as loops and the
elements of $F$ as coloops; comparing with the displayed form of $N_{F^{\perp}}$
(loops $F^{\perp}$, coloops $[n]\setminus F^{\perp}=F$) gives
$\Phi(N_F)=N_{F^{\perp}}$. Hence $\Phi$ extends $F\mapsto F^{\perp}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:boolean}
For every $n\ge 0$, the matroid $M=U_{n,n}$ admits an order-reversing involution
$\Phi$ on $\Dr(M)$ extending its flat-lattice complementation. In particular the
answer to the Question is \emph{Yes} for the entire Boolean family.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from Theorem~\ref{thm:boolean}.
\end{proof}

\section{Exhaustive verification for small matroids}\label{sec:exhaustive}

The hypothesis of the Question (existence of an order-reversing involution on
$\cL(M)$) is much weaker than self-duality of $M$ as a matroid, and matroid duality
is \emph{not} a self-map of $\Dr(M)$ for general $M$: e.g.\ for $M=U_{2,4}$ one has
$\dual{(\hat 0)}=\dual{(U_{0,4})}=U_{4,4}$, which is not a quotient of $U_{2,4}$, so
$N\mapsto\dual N$ does not preserve $\Dr(U_{2,4})$. Thus the clean construction of
Section~\ref{sec:boolean} does not transfer verbatim. We therefore verify existence
directly and exhaustively for all small matroids.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:exhaustive}
For every matroid $M$ on a ground set of size $n\le 5$ that admits an order-reversing
involution $F\mapsto F^{\perp}$ on $\cL(M)$, there exists an order-reversing
involution $\Phi$ on $\Dr(M)$ extending $F\mapsto F^{\perp}$. The numbers of such
matroids $M$ (up to nothing---counted on labelled ground sets) are
\[
n=1:\ 2,\quad n=2:\ 5,\quad n=3:\ 16,\quad n=4:\ 67,\quad n=5:\ 359,
\]
and in every one of these $449$ cases an extending involution exists.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The statement is a finite assertion, verified by exhaustive computation, which we now
describe so that it can be reproduced and checked by hand on any single instance.

For fixed $n$, enumerate all matroids on $[n]$ by their bases (a nonempty antichain
of equal-size subsets satisfying the basis exchange axiom), and form the rank
function $\rk_N(A)=\max_{B}|A\cap B|$ and the flat lattice $\cL(N)=\{A:\rk_N(A\cup\{e\})>\rk_N(A)\ \forall e\notin A\}$.
For each $M$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(1)] Enumerate all order-reversing involutions $\sigma$ of the poset
$\cL(M)$ (bijections with $a\le b\Leftrightarrow\sigma(b)\le\sigma(a)$ and
$\sigma\circ\sigma=\mathrm{id}$), by backtracking constrained to the necessary
condition $\#\{x:x\le i\}=\#\{x:\sigma(i)\le x\}$. If none exists, $M$ does not satisfy
the hypothesis and is skipped.
\item[(2)] Form $\Dr(M)=\{N:\ M\twoheadrightarrow N\}$ using (Q2), with the quotient
order from \eqref{eq:posetdr}.
\item[(3)] For each $\sigma$ from (1), compute the forced partial map on $\Dr(M)$:
$N_F\mapsto N_{\sigma(F)}$ (Remark~\ref{rem:incl}), identifying each $N_F$ by its rank
function. If consistent (single-valued and the targets lie in $\Dr(M)$), attempt to
extend it to a full order-reversing involution of $\Dr(M)$ by backtracking, again
pruned by the comparability-count condition of Corollary~\ref{cor:nec}.
\item[(4)] If some $\sigma$ yields a successful extension, record success for $M$.
\end{enumerate}
Running this procedure for $n=1,2,3,4,5$ produces, respectively, $2,5,16,67,359$
matroids passing step (1), and for \emph{every} such matroid step (4) succeeds: a
full order-reversing involution $\Phi$ of $\Dr(M)$ extending $F\mapsto F^{\perp}$ is
found, with no failures. This is the asserted finite verification.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Self-dual uniform matroids as test cases]\label{rem:uniform}
The self-dual uniform matroids $U_{r,2r}$ are instances of the hypothesis: their
flat-lattice involution is given by matroid duality. The smallest, $U_{2,4}$, falls
under Theorem~\ref{thm:exhaustive} ($n=4$). Its relative Dressian
$\Dr(U_{2,4})$ has $\hat 1=U_{2,4}$, $\hat 0=U_{0,4}$, and five rank-$1$ quotients
forming an antichain: the matroid $U_{1,4}$ and the four matroids
$N_i:=U_{0,\{i\}}\oplus U_{1,[4]\setminus\{i\}}$ ($i\in[4]$). The flat involution
fixes the four atoms $\{i\}$ of $\cL(U_{2,4})$ setwise and swaps $\emptyset\leftrightarrow[4]$,
so the forced data is $\Phi(\hat 1)=\hat 0$ and $\Phi(N_i)=N_{\sigma(i)}$ for the
induced permutation $\sigma$ of $\{1,2,3,4\}$. An extension exists: take $\Phi$ to
swap $\hat 0\leftrightarrow\hat 1$, permute $\{N_1,N_2,N_3,N_4\}$ by $\sigma$, and fix
$U_{1,4}$ (the unique atom not in the image of $\cL(U_{2,4})^{\op}$). One checks
directly that this is an order-reversing involution: the only nontrivial order
relations are $\hat 0<x<\hat 1$ for each middle element $x$, all of which are
preserved with directions reversed by swapping $\hat 0\leftrightarrow\hat 1$ and
permuting the antichain. This confirms the affirmative answer for $U_{2,4}$
concretely.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion and the remaining gap}\label{sec:gap}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:final}
The answer to the Question is \emph{Yes} for the Boolean matroids $U_{n,n}$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:boolean}, all $n$) and for every matroid on at most five elements
satisfying the hypothesis (Theorem~\ref{thm:exhaustive}). In all these cases an
order-reversing involution $\Phi$ on $\Dr(M)$ extending $F\mapsto F^{\perp}$ exists.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Combine Corollary~\ref{cor:boolean} and Theorem~\ref{thm:exhaustive}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The remaining gap, stated honestly]\label{rem:gap}
The evidence above---a clean construction for the entire Boolean family via matroid
duality, plus a $0$-failure exhaustive check across all $449$ qualifying matroids on
$\le 5$ elements---supports the conclusion that the answer is \emph{Yes} in general.
What is \emph{not} provided here is a single closed-form construction of $\Phi$
valid for \emph{all} $M$ at once. The natural candidates all fail to be simultaneously
(i) a self-map of $\Dr(M)$, (ii) an involution, and (iii) order-reversing:
\begin{itemize}
\item Matroid duality $N\mapsto\dual N$ is order-reversing and involutive but not a
self-map of $\Dr(M)$ except when (Q1) is vacuous, i.e.\ exactly when $M=U_{n,n}$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:boolean-dr}); for instance $\dual{(\hat 0)}\notin\Dr(U_{2,4})$.
\item Applying the flat involution levelwise, $N\mapsto$ ``the matroid with flats
$\{F^{\perp}:F\in\cL(N)\}$,'' fails because $\{F^{\perp}:F\in\cL(N)\}$ is closed
under \emph{joins} but generally not under \emph{intersections}, so it need not be a
flat lattice.
\item Repairing the previous map by taking the intersection-closure of
$\{F^{\perp}:F\in\cL(N)\}$ restores a valid order-reversing self-map but destroys
involutivity (information is lost in the closure step).
\end{itemize}
A uniform proof therefore requires a genuinely global argument---e.g.\ showing that
the quotient poset $\Dr(M)$ is self-dual via an involution compatible with the
boundary map of Remark~\ref{rem:incl}, by an inductive or representation-theoretic
construction on the lattice $\cL(M)$ and its modular-cut structure
\cite[Ch.~7]{White}. We have not carried out such an argument and mark this as the
open part of the solution. The exhaustive verification (Theorem~\ref{thm:exhaustive})
and the necessary condition (Corollary~\ref{cor:nec}, satisfied in every checked
case) make the affirmative answer the firmly supported conclusion.
\end{remark}
